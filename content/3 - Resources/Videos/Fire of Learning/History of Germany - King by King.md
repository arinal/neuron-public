# History of Germany — King by King

> A ruler-by-ruler walk through German history, from the earliest Germanic tribes to the end of the German Empire in 1918, told at the level of detail in the Fire of Learning documentaries. For **each ruler**: what the documentary actually says about them, an **illustration of a key event**, and their **place in the family tree** (how they inherited — son, brother, nephew, cousin, or elected outsider).
>
> Reading the trees: a **blue box** = the reigning ruler; **👑** = crowned Emperor (not just German king). In the medieval trees, yellow = German kings, blue = France, green = Italy, orange = Burgundy; in the later trees, colour codes the branch (Austrian Habsburg, Spanish Habsburg, Habsburg-Lorraine, Hohenzollern-Prussia).
>
> ⚠️ Rulers are numbered continuously (1–52). A **pre-royal prologue** (Germania before 481) opens the note; the Merovingians predate the family tree (which begins at Charlemagne). Where the documentary skips a ruler that the family tree includes, that's noted.
>
> ⚠️ **The story changes shape partway through.** Up to the Reformation it is a fairly tidy king-list. After the **Thirty Years' War** the Holy Roman Empire decays into "a loose confederation of hundreds of microstates" (Voltaire: *neither holy, nor Roman, nor an empire*), and the real story becomes a **duel between two rising powers — Habsburg Austria and Hohenzollern Prussia** — until Bismarck welds the German states into one empire in 1871. The ruler spine continues (emperors *and* Prussian kings) with the wars, treaties and revolutions woven under them, as the documentaries do.

## Before the Kings — Germania (pre-481)
*(No kings yet, and long before the UsefulCharts family tree. This is the world the Frankish monarchy grew out of — a prologue the documentary spends its first nine minutes on.)*

**"Germany" is a Roman name.** *Deutschland* to its own people; the Romans called the lands east of the **Rhine** *Germania*. From the start this was never the history of one nation but of many German peoples — a disunity that recurs across the whole story. Modern humans reached the area ~35,000 years ago, but the **Germanic peoples** themselves arrived only ~3,000 years ago, migrating south from **Scandinavia and northern Germany** as the climate cooled. As they spread they bordered **Celts, Slavs, Baltic peoples and Scythians**, and their languages and culture drifted apart from the peoples further north.

Written records are scarce — most of what we know before Roman contact comes from archaeology, linguistics and genetics. The earliest fragments are from **Pytheas of Massalia**, a Greek explorer of the 3rd century BC. The first serious contact was the **Cimbrian War (113–101 BC)**, when the Cimbri, Teutones and Ambrones pushed south and were defeated by Rome — the opening of a long, intense relationship.

**Julius Caesar** was the first to call them *Germani* — a geographic label more than an ethnic one, lumping together diverse, frequently warring tribes across the Rhine. He described them as barbarians without towns or cities, living on primitive subsistence farming. Their **paganism** resembled Norse mythology, worshipping gods like **Mani, Tiw, Woden, Thunor and Frig** — names that survive in our weekdays (Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday), later carried to England by the Anglo-Saxons. **Tacitus** filled in the picture: a large, red-haired, blue-eyed people spread across some 50 tribes.

### Arminius and the Teutoburg Forest (9 AD)
Under **Augustus**, Rome tried to absorb Germania — but the thick forests and marshes were a logistical nightmare, utterly unlike Gaul. Rome taxed the tribes, confiscated weapons, imposed its law, and took noblemen's sons as hostages to be **raised as Romans** and sent home loyal. It backfired spectacularly. One such Romanised German, **Arminius**, son of a Cherusci chief and an officer in the Roman army, defected — and used his knowledge of Roman tactics against them. In **September 9 AD** he lured the legions of **Publius Quinctilius Varus** into ambush near Kalkriese: the **Battle of the Teutoburg Forest**, where ~20,000 Romans were annihilated. Rome abandoned its designs on *Magna Germania* for good, halting the Romanisation of Germany. Arminius — hailed by some as **Germany's first hero** — dreamed of uniting the tribes but was murdered by rivals before any German nation could form. Germania fell back into disunity: *Germania* was a Roman idea, alive in Roman minds but not yet in the hearts of the peoples living there.
![[germany-p1-01-arminius.jpg]]
*Arminius, the Romanised Cheruscan who destroyed Varus's legions in the Teutoburg Forest — "Germany's first hero."*

### Migration, Rome's fall, and the rise of the Franks
In the 2nd–3rd centuries, as Rome decayed, the tribes coalesced and expanded — **Goths, Vandals, Franks, Alemanni, Marcomanni, Bavarii, Angles, Saxons, Lombards**. The **Goths** were the most troublesome, raiding and destabilising the empire. In the 4th century the **Huns** pushed westward, forcing the Germanic peoples into Roman territory; too weak to stop them, Rome tried to integrate them, and its army came to be majority-Germanic. Many were **Christianised** — the Bible was translated into **Gothic around 350 AD**. As the empire fell, Germanic rulers took over many successor states (some as far as North Africa); most assimilated into local populations, but the **Anglo-Saxon** settlement of England left a permanent mark. Out of these post-Roman conflicts came greater cohesion among the Germans. By the late 5th century, in the west and south, **Franks, Frisians, Bavarians, Saxons, Alemanni and Thuringians** were becoming recognisably German — and power was centring on the **Franks** under **Clovis I**, where the king-by-king story properly begins.

## The Merovingian Franks
*(Predate the UsefulCharts family tree, which starts at Charlemagne.)*

### 1. Clovis I (481–511) — Merovingian
Power in the post-Roman West began to centre on the **Franks** under Clovis I, who became king in 481. He expanded aggressively into lands still held by the last Romans and rival Germanic tribes; by **508** his realm covered much of modern Germany, France and the Low Countries. That same year he made the fateful decision to **convert his kingdom to Catholicism**, launching the large-scale Christianisation of peoples who had been outside the old Christian Roman Empire. When he died around 512, his kingdom was **divided among his four sons** in line with Frankish tradition — the first appearance of **partible inheritance** (splitting land among all heirs rather than primogeniture), the habit that would cripple German unity for centuries.
![[germany-p1-02-map-508ad.jpg]]
*Europe in 508: Clovis's Franks (purple) among the post-Roman kingdoms.*

### 2. Chlothar II (reunited 613) — Merovingian
In 613, **Chlothar II of Neustria** reunited the fractured Frankish realm and set up the office of **"mayor of the palace"** to help administer it — in effect a prime minister. It seemed a useful reform; in time it would hollow out the monarchy entirely, as the mayors accumulated the real power.

### 3. Childeric III (deposed 751) — last Merovingian
By the 8th century the Merovingians were **puppet kings** — the mayors of the palace ran everything. Childeric III was the last of the line, deposed in 751 with the Pope's blessing, closing the Merovingian era.

## The Carolingians
![[germany-p1-chart-06-carolingians.jpg]]
*The Carolingian tree: Charlemagne → Louis the Pious → the three Verdun heirs (Charles the Bald, Lothair, Louis the German) → down to Charles the Fat, Arnulf and Conrad I, where the Ottonians take over.*

### 4. Charles Martel (mayor of the palace, 718–741) — Carolingian
*(Charlemagne's grandfather — precedes the family tree.)* Charles secured the mayorship in 718. Nicknamed **"the Hammer,"** he is most famous for his victory at the **Battle of Tours (732)**, where he defeated the Muslim Umayyads and — in the documentary's framing — prevented an Islamic takeover of France and Germany. But he fought Germans too: from 718–730 he warred against the **Bavarians** (independent-minded and uncooperative), **Saxons** (pagans who raided Frankish land), Alemanni and Frisians.
![[germany-p1-03-charles-martel.jpg]]
*Charles "the Hammer" Martel, victor of Tours (732).*

### 5. Pepin the Short (751–768) — Carolingian
*(Charlemagne's father — precedes the tree.)* Martel's son. In **751**, with the Pope's support, he deposed the last Merovingian, Childeric III, ending that dynasty and founding the **Carolingians**. He ruled ten years; his most lasting act was the **Donation of Pepin**, a grant of land to the Pope that became the **Papal States**.
![[germany-p1-13-pepin.jpg]]
*Pepin the Short, first Carolingian king.*

### 6. Charlemagne 👑 (768–814) — Carolingian
**Relation:** son of Pepin; became sole ruler in 771 when his brother **Carloman** died of natural causes. Remembered as *Pater Europae*, the **"father of Europe."**
He turned at once to Germany and his great foe, the pagan **Saxons** (led by rulers like **Widukind**, "forest child"). In 772 he invaded Saxony and desecrated pagan monuments such as the **Irminsul**, a sacred pillar the Saxons believed held up the sky. The **Saxon Wars dragged on for 30 years (to 804)** because Saxon society was fractured and kept revolting whenever he was busy elsewhere; the Franks answered brutally — at **Verden in 782 he had 4,500 beheaded**, and enforced Christianity on pain of death (Widukind finally submitted and was baptised in 785). He also fought the Bavarians (788), Avars and Slavs. On **Christmas Day 800**, Pope Leo III crowned him **Emperor of the Romans** — a title that would outlive him a thousand years (though "Holy" wasn't added for centuries, and the Byzantines, who still saw themselves as the real Romans, were offended). His reign also saw the intellectual flourishing called the **Carolingian Renaissance**. He died in 814.
![[germany-p1-05-charlemagne-coronation.jpg]]
*Raphael's Coronation of Charlemagne, Christmas Day 800.*
![[germany-p1-04-saxon-wars.jpg]]
*The 30-year Saxon Wars — "baptism or death."*
![[germany-p1-chart-07-charlemagne.jpg]]
*Top of the tree: Charlemagne, sons beneath him.*

### 7. Louis the Pious 👑 (814–840) — Carolingian
**Relation:** Charlemagne intended to divide the empire among his sons, but only **Louis** outlived him, so the empire briefly stayed whole. Louis, however, was **not the man for the times** — said to have wished he were a priest. He spent his reign struggling to hold the empire together against outside enemies and his own rebellious sons. He tried to leave everything to his eldest to prevent Christian Europe's collapse, but the other sons revolted successfully. He died in 840.
![[germany-p1-14-louis-pious.jpg]]
*Louis the Pious, Charlemagne's sole surviving heir.*

### 8. Charles the Bald, Lothair & Louis the German (Treaty of Verdun, 843) — Carolingian
**Relation:** the **three sons** of Louis the Pious. After the Battle of Fontenoy (841), the **Treaty of Verdun (843)** split the empire three ways — a milestone in German history:
- **Charles the Bald** 👑 — the **west** → became France.
- **Lothair** 👑 — the unstable **middle** (Lotharingia and Italy), which soon fragmented; its name survives in **Lorraine**, a source of Franco-German conflict for a millennium (later divided again by the Treaty of Meerssen).
- **Louis the German** — the **east** (Bavaria, Franconia, Saxony, Swabia) → became **Germany**. He later took the old Roman title *Germanicus*, "conqueror of the Germans," as a distinct German identity was coalescing.
![[germany-p1-15-verdun-843-map.jpg]]
*843: West, Middle and East Francia — the birth of France and Germany.*

### 9. Charles the Fat 👑 (881–887) — Carolingian
**Relation:** youngest son of Louis the German (who died in 876, splitting his realm among sons Louis the Younger, Carloman and Charles). Charles briefly **reunited Charlemagne's entire empire** — not by conquest but because **every other heir had died** — making him the last to rule a united Frankish realm. His reign was consumed by **Magyar raids** (who had reached East Francia in the 860s and would found Hungary) and **Vikings** (whose raids struck Aachen, Paris and the Rhineland). Plagued by sickness and epilepsy, he was **deposed by his nephew Arnulf in 887**.

### 10. Arnulf (887–899) — Carolingian
**Relation:** **illegitimate nephew** of Charles the Fat, whom he deposed. He couldn't claim the whole empire — it now split apart **permanently** — but kept East Francia. Tellingly, he was **elected** by the nobility, reviving an old Germanic practice that would come to define the country.

### 11. Louis the Child (899–911) — last East-Frankish Carolingian
**Relation:** **son** of Arnulf, king at age six. Every corner of Germany was **ravaged by Magyar horsemen** during his reign, while the dukes of the duchies grabbed ever more independence, threatening to tear the kingdom apart. He died around age 20 in 911, the **last Carolingian king** of Germany.

### 12. Conrad I (911–918) — Franconian
**Relation:** with the Carolingians extinct, the Germans **elected** Conrad, Duke of Thuringia, rather than accept the French king's claim. He never got the imperial title (which had drifted to rulers in Italy as a papal reward). He had **little real authority over the dukes who elected him** — "more a grand duke than a king" — and fought his own subjects and foreigners throughout his reign, establishing the **monarch-vs-nobles struggle** that would mark the whole Holy Roman Empire. Mortally wounded fighting the rebellious Bavarians, he did something remarkable on his deathbed: he named his **enemy**, Henry of Saxony, as the man who could hold Germany together.

## The Ottonians
![[germany-p1-chart-01-ottonians.jpg]]
*Henry the Fowler → Otto the Great (highlighted) → Otto II → Otto III; and via a second son, Henry II. "Germany becomes an elective monarchy."*

### 13. Henry the Fowler (919–936) — Ottonian (Saxon)
**Relation:** Duke of Saxony, **elected** on Conrad I's dying recommendation — the **first native German king**, founder of the Saxon (Ottonian) dynasty. Nicknamed "the Fowler" for his love of falconry. His urgent problem was the **Magyars**: beaten at first, he then got lucky, capturing their leader's son and trading him back for a **ten-year truce**. He used the breathing space to fight the Slavs, train infantry and build fortifications, then crushed the Magyars at the **Battle of Riade (933)**. A great soldier but no centraliser — Germany stayed a loose confederation of duchies — his most important choice was **not to divide his lands**, leaving the realm intact to one heir. Died 936.
![[germany-p1-16-henry-fowler-map.jpg]]
*East Francia (~920), ringed by Magyars, Slavs and Vikings — the realm Henry defended.*

### 14. Otto the Great 👑 (936–973) — Ottonian
**Relation:** **son** of Henry the Fowler, elected 936. In a near-40-year reign he earned the title "the Great." Ambitious, he saw himself as **Charlemagne's successor**. After early revolts by his younger brother and the nobles, he broke the cycle of chaos by **centralising power on the monarchy** — replacing rebellious dukes with loyal relatives, ruling from horseback on constant tours, and harnessing the Church. Drawn into **Italy** by the widowed queen **Adelaide** (usurped by Berengar II), he crossed the Alps in 951, took Pavia and married her — which enraged his son **Liudolf**, who rebelled in 953 and even sided with the invading Magyars, until the nobles recoiled at that treachery and the revolt collapsed in 954 (Otto mercifully forgave him). Then came his masterpiece: the **Battle of Lechfeld (955)** near Augsburg — ~8,000 men against roughly double the Magyar force, on a narrow plain between two rivers, where his **heavy cavalry (forerunner of the medieval knight)** destroyed the Magyar horse-archers and **ended their raids on Western Europe for good**. Hailed as the savior of Christendom, in **962** he took northern Italy and was crowned **Emperor** — the beginning of the **Holy Roman Empire** ("the First Reich"), Otto the first German to hold the title. Conflict with the papacy began immediately (he deposed John XII). He married his heir to the Byzantine princess **Theophanu** and died in 973.
![[germany-p1-06-otto-the-great.jpg]]
*Otto the Great enthroned.*
![[germany-p1-07-battle-of-lechfeld.jpg]]
*Lechfeld (955): Otto's knights destroy the Magyar horse-archers.*

### 15. Otto II 👑 (973–983) — Ottonian
**Relation:** **son** of Otto the Great, elected king even before his father died (with Otto I "breathing down the nobles' necks"). Without his father, domestic conflict flared, alongside wars with Danes, French, Arabs, Slavs and Byzantines. He **died suddenly at 28 of malaria**.

### 16. Otto III 👑 (983–1002) — Ottonian
**Relation:** **son** of Otto II, elected and crowned as a child; came of age in 994. Exceptionally bright, he **dreamed of truly reviving the Roman Empire** and ruling as the head of Christendom. In January 1002, at just 21, he **died of a sudden fever** while suppressing a rebellion in Italy — **unmarried and childless**.

### 17. Henry II 👑 (1002–1024) — Ottonian
**Relation:** **cousin** (Duke of Bavaria) of Otto III, who won a heated, war-torn contest for the throne. He was king in 1002, King of Italy in 1004 and Emperor in 1014, spending his reign trying to recover the authority the Ottonians once held and fighting Poland over Bohemia. The independent-minded princes were happy to have a distracted, weakened king. He **died in 1024 with no heir**, ending the Ottonian dynasty.

## The Salians
![[germany-p1-chart-02-salians.jpg]]
*Conrad II (highlighted) → Henry III → Henry IV → Henry V. The Welf ("Guelph") and Hohenstaufen ("Ghibelline") shields emerge below.*

### 18. Conrad II 👑 (1024–1039) — Salian
**Relation:** **elected** when the Ottonians died out (a great-great-grandson of Otto the Great through a female line), founding the **Salian dynasty**. Like his predecessors he had no capital — "the capital was wherever the emperor was," the closest thing being Aachen — and spent his early reign touring. In **1032 he incorporated Burgundy** (won diplomatically, then taken by force), so the Empire now rested on three kingdoms: **Germany, Italy and Burgundy**. The Salian emperors were still perhaps the strongest men in Europe — but a storm was brewing.

### 19. Henry III 👑 (1039–1056) — Salian
**Relation:** **son** of Conrad II. *(The documentary doesn't single him out — it moves straight to the reforms that "began in 1056," the year he died and his six-year-old son took the throne. In the family tree he sits between Conrad II and Henry IV.)* He represents the last stretch of strong Salian rule before the Investiture Controversy.

### 20. Henry IV 👑 (1056–1105) — Salian
**Relation:** **son** of Henry III; king at age six. His reign is the great collision of Emperor and Pope. Traditionally, **emperors appointed bishops** to keep the Church loyal (bishops held power like princes; the Church was a unifying force), and sometimes even made or unmade popes — while only the Pope could crown an emperor. The **Gregorian reform** movement challenged this. In **1075 Pope Gregory VII issued the *Dictatus Papae*** — the Pope alone may appoint or depose bishops, and may even depose emperors. Henry wrote back declaring he'd remove the Pope; Gregory **excommunicated him and declared him no longer king**. When Henry's own subjects seized the chance to rebel with papal backing, he faced defeat — leading to the famous **Walk to Canossa (1077)**: barefoot in thin clothing, he crossed the snow and **waited and fasted three days at the castle gates** until Gregory received him and lifted the excommunication. Humiliating — his predecessors could break popes with a wave. The German nobles fought on anyway (the **Great Saxon Revolt**), electing an anti-king, **Rudolph von Rheinfelden**; Gregory backed Rudolph and excommunicated Henry again in 1080 — but Rudolph conveniently **died that year**. Henry then invaded Rome; the Pope's Norman rescuers (1084) ended up sacking the city, and Gregory died in exile a year later. Henry installed the antipope **Clement III**, who crowned him Emperor.
![[germany-p1-08-investiture-controversy.jpg]]
*Pope Gregory VII vs. Emperor Henry IV — who appoints the bishops?*
![[germany-p1-09-walk-to-canossa.jpg]]
*Henry IV barefoot in the snow at Canossa (1077).*

### 21. Henry V 👑 (1105–1125) — Salian
**Relation:** **son** of Henry IV — against whom he had often rebelled. He finally ended the Investiture Controversy with the **Concordat of Worms (1122)** with Pope Callixtus II, in which the **Church won greater independence**. The long fight **weakened the Empire and strengthened the papacy**, and left the German nobility used to defying the monarch. Henry V died **childless in 1125**, ending the Salians — after which England and France began to overtake the Empire as Europe's leading powers.

## The Hohenstaufen (and their Welf rivals)
![[germany-p1-chart-03-hohenstaufen.jpg]]
*House of Welf (Guelphs) vs House of Hohenstaufen (Ghibellines). Barbarossa (highlighted) → Philip & Henry VI → Frederick II; Otto IV branches off the Welf side.*

### 22. Lothair III 👑 (1125–1137) — Supplinburg
**Relation:** *(The documentary skips him, mentioning only "attempts to prevent" the Hohenstaufen succession "and an interregnum" after Henry V.)* In the family tree he is the count elected as a stop-gap over the powerful Hohenstaufen candidate — the disputed succession that lit the Welf–Hohenstaufen feud.

### 23. Conrad III (1138–1152) — Hohenstaufen
**Relation:** the documentary says simply that "**Conrad of the Hohenstaufen dynasty eventually came to the throne after attempts to prevent him and an interregnum.**" In the tree he is a grandson of Henry IV; his contested election against the Welfs ignited the century-long **Guelph–Ghibelline** rivalry. He was never crowned Emperor.

### 24. Frederick Barbarossa 👑 (1152–1190) — Hohenstaufen
**Relation:** **nephew** of Conrad III, elected king in 1152. His first act was to campaign in **Italy**, subduing the independent northern Italians (especially Milan) and getting crowned Emperor in Rome; the Italians gave him the name **"Barbarossa," Redbeard**. Marrying **Beatrice of Burgundy** secured all three parts of the Empire. His long struggle with **Pope Alexander III** is why **"Holy"** was added to the Empire's name — not to flatter the Pope but to assert that the imperial title came **directly from God**, not as a papal gift. He campaigned in Italy **five times**, aiming even at Sicily, but was beaten by the **Lombard League** (rebellious northern cities plus the Pope, Venice, Sicily and Byzantium) at the **Battle of Legnano**. At home his over-mighty cousin **Henry the Lion**, Duke of Bavaria and Saxony — founder of **Munich** and a driver of the **Ostsiedlung** (German eastward settlement / Northern Crusades against the Baltic pagans) — grew powerful enough to defy him; Barbarossa stripped his titles and exiled him. Barbarossa's last campaign was the **Third Crusade** (alongside Philip Augustus of France and Richard the Lionheart of England); in **1190 he fell off his horse into a river and drowned**.
![[germany-p1-10-barbarossa.jpg]]
*Frederick Barbarossa, Redbeard.*

### 25. Henry VI 👑 (1190–1197) — Hohenstaufen
**Relation:** **son** of Barbarossa. In just seven years he **extended the Empire all the way to Sicily** (through his wife) — a claim that alarmed the Pope. He died in 1197, leaving a three-year-old heir and a succession crisis.

### 26. Philip of Swabia (1198–1208) — Hohenstaufen
**Relation:** **son** of Barbarossa, younger brother of Henry VI. With Henry VI's heir only three, **two kings were elected** and civil war erupted: Philip on the Hohenstaufen side, Otto IV on the Welf side. Philip was the only German king ever **assassinated**.

### 27. Otto IV 👑 (1208–1215) — Welf
**Relation:** **son of Henry the Lion** (House of Welf) — the rival elected against Philip. He briefly became sole, crowned Emperor, but soon fell out with the Pope and was pushed aside once Frederick II came of age.
![[germany-p1-chart-08-otto4.jpg]]
*Otto IV (highlighted) — note the English/Plantagenet connection to the left (his mother's family).*

### 28. Frederick II 👑 (1215–1250) — Hohenstaufen
**Relation:** **son** of Henry VI (the three-year-old of 1197). Coming of age, he **won the Pope's support by promising to give up Sicily** and became undisputed king in 1215; he **won the nobles by granting them further rights and independence** — doing willingly what his predecessors had resisted. A brilliant, intellectual ruler Nietzsche later called **"the first European."** He broke his promise and kept **Sicily**, building a strong centralised state there and angering the Pope; he also kept delaying his promised crusade (blamed for the Fifth Crusade's failure) and was **excommunicated four times**, even branded "the antichrist" by Gregory IX. He did crusade twice — winning **Jerusalem by negotiation** on the Sixth Crusade (which annoyed the Pope, who wanted a battle) — and elevated **Hermann von Salza** as Grandmaster of the **Teutonic Knights**, whose conquest of Prussia and Livonia drove **massive German migration eastward**, with vast long-term consequences. In the 1240s the papacy backed rival kings (Wilhelm of Holland, Henry Raspe) while the Teutonic Order faced the **Mongols**. He died in 1250.
![[germany-p1-17-frederick2-falcon.jpg]]
*Frederick II with a falcon — he wrote a celebrated treatise on falconry.*
![[germany-p1-chart-09-frederick2.jpg]]
*Frederick II (highlighted); below, Conrad IV and the coming Interregnum.*

### 29. Conrad IV (1250–1254) — Hohenstaufen
**Relation:** **son** of Frederick II. He died after just a few years, **ending Hohenstaufen rule** and plunging the Empire into its **first Great Interregnum** — no king with undisputed approval until 1272, during which the nobility and bishops grew ever more autonomous (this is the great age of German castle-building).

## The "Count-Kings" & the Great Interregnum
![[germany-p1-chart-11-countkings.jpg]]
*After the Interregnum the crown bounced between four houses — Wittelsbach, Luxembourg, Nassau, Habsburg — mostly minor counts.*

### 30. Rudolph I (1273–1291) — Habsburg
**Relation:** the **first Habsburg** elected king, from the Archduchy of Austria (which had split off from Bavaria in the 12th century). Notably he was **never crowned Emperor** — the princes now wanted "a servant, not a leader." The documentary stresses this was **not yet the Habsburg rise to power, only foreshadowing**: he made his sons Dukes of Austria and Styria, but the fearful electors then deliberately chose kings from **other, weaker houses**.

### 31. Adolf of Nassau (1292–1298) — Nassau
**Relation:** *(The documentary doesn't name him — he appears only in the family tree.)* An elected king from the House of Nassau, chosen precisely because he lacked power; never Emperor.

### 32. Albert I (1298–1308) — Habsburg
**Relation:** *(Also only in the family tree.)* **Son** of Rudolph I; briefly returned the crown to the Habsburgs, but again never crowned Emperor.

### 33. Henry VII 👑 (1308–1313) — Luxembourg
**Relation:** crowned king in 1308 and **Emperor in 1312**. During his reign the **Teutonic Knights captured Danzig (Gdańsk)**, geographically **linking Germany and Prussia**. He died only a few years into his reign.

### 34. Louis IV 👑 (1314–1347) — Wittelsbach
**Relation:** elected in a **contested election** (House of Wittelsbach). The Pope refused to recognise him, but in **1338 the German electors issued the Declaration of Rhense**: the emperor's right to rule **no longer depended on papal approval** — whomever the electors chose was king, and with the German kingship came the title of Emperor / **Kaiser** (from *Caesar*).

### 35. Charles IV 👑 (1346–1378) — Luxembourg
**Relation:** **grandson** of Henry VII; also King of Bohemia. Rhense paved the way for his landmark **Golden Bull of 1356**, which fixed the election of emperors by **seven prince-electors** — three ecclesiastical (archbishops of **Mainz, Cologne, Trier**) and four secular (King of **Bohemia**, Count Palatine of the **Rhine**, Duke of **Saxony-Wittenberg**, Margrave of **Brandenburg**). Called the "German Magna Carta," it ended succession wars but made emperors **dependent on the electors** — a milestone on the Empire's slow slide toward irrelevance. In these years the **Black Death (1347–51)** killed about half of Europe; Germany lost ~20%.
![[germany-p1-11-golden-bull.jpg]]
*The Golden Bull (1356): rule by seven prince-electors.*
![[germany-p1-chart-04-interregnum-luxembourg.jpg]]
*Charles IV (highlighted) of Luxembourg among the four count-king houses.*

## Luxembourg → Habsburg
![[germany-p1-chart-05-luxembourg-to-habsburg.jpg]]
*Charles IV → Wenceslaus, Rupert & Sigismund; then the crown passes by marriage to the Habsburg Albert II.*

### 36. Wenceslaus (1378–1400) — Luxembourg
**Relation:** **son** of Charles IV, elected 1378. His behaviour was seen as **degenerate** and his rule ineffective, so the electors **deposed him in 1400** (he kept Bohemia).

### 37. Rupert (1400–1410) — Wittelsbach
**Relation:** elected to replace Wenceslaus, but **as long as Wenceslaus lived his reign was contested** and plagued by domestic conflict. He died in 1410 on the verge of civil war; never crowned Emperor.

### 38. Sigismund 👑 (1410–1437) — Luxembourg
**Relation:** **younger brother** of Wenceslaus, who took the throne though Wenceslaus wanted it back. Bright and energetic, already **King of Hungary**, he had campaigned against the Ottomans as head of the **Order of the Dragon**. He fought Venice, was drawn into the Teutonic–Polish wars, and **helped end the papal schism** (three rival popes) by proposing the **Council of Constance**. At Constance the reformer **Jan Hus** — a Czech critic of Church practice — was **burned at the stake in 1415 despite Sigismund's promise of protection**. This enraged the Czechs, who made Hus a martyr; when Wenceslaus died and Sigismund took Bohemia (vowing to "drown all the Hussite heretics"), it exploded into the **Hussite Wars (from 1419)**. The Pope called a crusade against them in 1421, but the Hussites were formidable, carrying the fight into Germany, Hungary and the Teutonic lands and allying with Poland (1431–35). They eventually split; the moderate **Utraquists** prevailed, and the war ended in 1434 with a compromise — Hussites reincorporated into Bohemia but allowed some of their practices. Sigismund died in 1437, the **last Luxembourg Emperor**, with **no sons**.
![[germany-p1-12-jan-hus.jpg]]
*Jan Hus, burned at Constance in 1415 under Sigismund's broken safe-conduct — a spark a century before Luther.*

### 39. Albert II (1438–1439) — Habsburg
**Relation:** *(The documentary jumps past him straight to Frederick III; the family tree shows him as Sigismund's **son-in-law**, married to Elizabeth of Luxembourg.)* His brief reign begins the near-unbroken **Habsburg** hold on the imperial crown.

### 40. Frederick III 👑 (1440–1493) — Habsburg
**Relation:** **second cousin** of Albert II; Sigismund's Habsburg successor in the documentary. Meanwhile the **Hohenzollerns** were rising in Brandenburg-Prussia. With the Hussite compromise, radical Church reform was delayed a century — but it was coming: a monk named **Martin Luther**. The documentary ends here, on the eve of the Reformation.
![[germany-p1-chart-10-frederick3.jpg]]
*Frederick III (highlighted) → Maximilian I → the Habsburg superpower to come.*

## Reformation & the Habsburg split (1493–1558)
*(The first documentary ends on **Frederick III** (1440–1493), on the eve of the Reformation; the second opens at 1558. Here's the connective tissue between them.)*

Frederick III's son **Maximilian I** married **Mary, Duchess of Burgundy**, pulling the wealthy Low Countries into the Habsburg orbit. Their son Philip married **Joanna of Castile** (daughter of Ferdinand of Aragon and Isabella of Castile) — and *their* son **Charles V** inherited what the charts video calls "the best inheritance anyone has ever received": the Holy Roman Empire, the Low Countries, and the Spanish crown with its South Italian and New World domains — more of Europe than anyone had ruled since Charlemagne. His reign was consumed by the **Reformation** (Luther's 1517 theses splitting German Christendom, later standardising the German language via his Bible) and endless war.

Before dying he made a wise split: **Spain and the Low Countries** went to his son **Philip II** (the **Spanish Habsburgs**), while the **imperial title** went to his brother **Ferdinand I** (the **Austrian Habsburgs**), who through his wife also held Bohemia and Hungary. From here the two branches diverge; this note follows the **Austrian** line — and the new **Hohenzollern** power growing in Brandenburg-Prussia.

![[germany-p2-chart-01-habsburg-split.jpg]]
*Charles V and Ferdinand I split the inheritance: Spanish Habsburgs (Philip II) vs Austrian Habsburgs (Ferdinand I) — with the House of Hohenzollern already on the chart's edge.*

## The Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)

### 41. Ferdinand I 👑 (1558–1564) — Austrian Habsburg
**Relation:** **brother** of Charles V; took the imperial title on Charles's 1558 abdication. He was the **first emperor to receive the title automatically on being crowned King of Germany** — the separate papal coronation (Charles V was the last to have one) was done away with.

### 42. Maximilian II · Rudolf II · Matthias (1564–1619) — Austrian Habsburg
**Relation:** Ferdinand's **son** Maximilian II, then Maximilian's sons **Rudolf II** (from 1576) and **Matthias**. **Rudolf** was a strange recluse who preferred private hobbies to ruling and dodged the mounting Protestant–Catholic conflict — granting the Bohemian Hussites the **Letter of Majesty**. The **Protestant Union** and **Catholic League** formed in 1608–09. Neither Rudolf nor Matthias had legitimate sons.

### 43. Ferdinand II 👑 (1619–1637) — Austrian Habsburg
**Relation:** **cousin** of Matthias. An ardent Catholic; became **King of Bohemia in 1617**. When he moved to revoke the Letter of Majesty, his representatives in Prague were seized and thrown out a window — the **Defenestration of Prague** (they survived, reportedly landing in manure). Bohemia revolted, igniting the **Thirty Years' War**.
![[germany-p2-01-defenestration-prague.jpg]]
*The Defenestration of Prague (1618): Ferdinand II's officials thrown from a Hradčany window — the spark of the Thirty Years' War.*

The war ran in **four phases**, and though it began over religion it became a struggle over imperial power and the European balance — Catholic France would end up funding Protestants to hem in the Habsburgs:
- **Bohemian phase** — crushed at the **Battle of White Mountain (1620)** near Prague, a decisive Catholic victory that ended the Bohemian revolt.
- **Danish phase** — Christian IV of Denmark-Norway intervened (with English/Scottish troops and French money) and was repelled by the brilliant general **Albrecht von Wallenstein**; Denmark surrendered in 1629.
- **Swedish phase** — **Gustavus Adolphus** of Sweden intervened in 1630, winning at Breitenfeld. He and Wallenstein met at the **Battle of Lützen (1632)** — a Swedish win, but Gustavus died leading a cavalry charge; Wallenstein was assassinated the next year. Ended with the 1635 Peace of Prague.
- **French phase** — the longest and most violent; France entered directly. **Ferdinand III** succeeded mid-war (1637) and wanted peace.
![[germany-p2-02-battle-white-mountain.jpg]]
*Battle of White Mountain (1620): the Catholic/Habsburg victory that ended the Bohemian revolt.*
![[germany-p2-03-gustavus-adolphus-lutzen.jpg]]
*Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden at Lützen (1632) — a Swedish victory in which the king was killed.*

### 44. Ferdinand III 👑 (1637–1657) — Austrian Habsburg
**Relation:** **son** of Ferdinand II. Ended the war with the **Peace of Westphalia (1648)**. Switzerland and the Netherlands became fully independent; France took Alsace and became the dominant continental power; religious coexistence was officially accepted. **Germany lost the most** — up to **8 million dead**, nearly a third of its population and half its men. The Empire survived but the **princes gained sweeping autonomy**; hopes of a unified HRE were finished. One state that came out ahead: **Brandenburg-Prussia**, with territorial gains under **Frederick William, the Great Elector**.
![[germany-p2-04-peace-of-westphalia.jpg]]
*The Peace of Westphalia (1648): the treaty that ended the war and entrenched the Empire's fragmentation.*

## Two powers rise — Austria & Prussia

### The Great Elector — Brandenburg-Prussia consolidates
**Frederick William, the Great Elector** turned a poor, scattered, militarily feeble Brandenburg into a rising state — building the army, growing the economy, even founding a small African colony (the *Brandenburger Gold Coast*). Bavaria, Saxony and above all **Austria** were the other consolidating players as the HRE faded into irrelevance.
![[germany-p2-05-great-elector.jpg]]
*Frederick William, the Great Elector of Brandenburg — architect of the Prussian rise.*

### 45. Leopold I 👑 (1658–1705) — Austrian Habsburg
**Relation:** **son** of Ferdinand III. His long reign saw the **Great Turkish War** and the climactic **Battle of Vienna (1683)** — the Ottomans besieged the Habsburg capital, and a relief army from across the Empire plus Poland broke the siege with **the largest cavalry charge in history**, killing or capturing ~20,000 Ottomans and saving the imperial capital. Austria then pushed the Turks out of Hungary. Two other landmarks: the **Spanish Habsburg line went extinct** (triggering the War of the Spanish Succession), and the **Duke of Prussia (House of Hohenzollern)** was elevated to **King *in* Prussia** — soon two German great powers instead of one.
![[germany-p2-06-siege-of-vienna-1683.jpg]]
*The Battle of Vienna (1683): the Ottoman siege broken by an imperial–Polish relief army.*
![[germany-p2-chart-02-leopold-prussia-rise.jpg]]
*Leopold I's line, with the Hohenzollerns of Prussia rising alongside — Frederick William (Great Elector) → Frederick I → Frederick William I.*

### 46. Joseph I & Charles VI 👑 (1705–1740) — Austrian Habsburg
**Relation:** Leopold's **sons**. The **War of the Spanish Succession** (1701–1714) ended roughly in a draw; Bourbon Philip V kept Spain but renounced the French throne. Meanwhile **Frederick I** styled himself **King *in* Prussia (1701)**, and his son **Frederick William I**, the **Soldatenkönig ("Soldier King")**, built Prussia's army into the "Sparta of the North," living frugally and selling off the African colony to fund it. **Charles VI** died **without a male heir**, having tried to secure his daughter's inheritance — triggering the next war.

*Frederick William I, the "Soldier King," who forged the Prussian military machine.*

## Frederick the Great vs Maria Theresa

### 47. Frederick II "the Great" (1740–1786) — King of Prussia, Hohenzollern
**Relation:** **son** of the Soldier King, who despised the boy's love of arts over soldiering — abusing him, and in 1730 forcing him to **watch the execution** of his friend von Katte after a failed escape. Yet Frederick became **Frederick der Große**. On his accession he stunned Austria by invading **Silesia (1740)**, seizing one of the Habsburgs' richest provinces (victories at **Mollwitz**, Hohenfriedberg). A patron of Voltaire ("Sparta in the morning, Athens in the afternoon"), he was also a military genius who made Prussia a first-rank power.
![[germany-p2-08-frederick-the-great.jpg]]
*Frederick the Great — Prussia's philosopher-warrior king.*
![[germany-p2-10-battle-of-mollwitz.jpg]]
*The Battle of Mollwitz (1741): Prussian infantry seizing Silesia from Austria.*

### 48. Maria Theresa & Francis I 👑 (1740–1780) — Habsburg / Habsburg-Lorraine
**Relation:** **daughter** of Charles VI. German law barred a woman from the imperial title, so she became **Archduchess of Austria** and Queen of Bohemia and Hungary while her husband **Francis I** (House of Lorraine) held the imperial crown — but **she wielded the real power**. The **War of the Austrian Succession (1740–48)** secured her inheritance but confirmed Prussia's grab of Silesia. Their descendants are the **House of Habsburg-Lorraine**.
![[germany-p2-09-maria-theresa.jpg]]
*Maria Theresa — the Habsburg empress in all but title, and Frederick's great rival.*
![[germany-p2-chart-03-austrian-succession.jpg]]
*The War of the Austrian Succession on the tree: Maria Theresa & Francis I vs Frederick the Great; Charles VII of Wittelsbach briefly holds the imperial title.*

### The Seven Years' War (1756–1763) & Partition of Poland
The **Seven Years' War** — arguably the first true world war (the American theatre = the French and Indian War) — saw Prussia and Austria **swap allies** (France now backing Austria, Britain backing Frederick). Surrounded by Austria, France, Russia and Sweden, Frederick struck first into Saxony (1756) and won against the odds at **Rossbach** and **Leuthen**; at one point a bullet was stopped by his snuffbox. Prussia neared collapse until the **"Miracle of the House of Brandenburg" (1762)** — Empress Elizabeth of Russia died and her pro-Frederick successor **Peter III** switched sides. The war ended in 1763 with borders essentially unchanged — a Prussian triumph. In **1772** Frederick engineered the **First Partition of Poland** with Austria and Russia, linking his territories and letting him style himself **King *of* Prussia**. Maria Theresa died in 1780; Frederick, mourning her, wrote he "was her opponent but never her enemy," and died in his armchair in 1786.

*The Seven Years' War — Frederick fighting a coalition of Europe's great powers and surviving.*

*The First Partition of Poland (1772): Prussia, Austria and Russia carve up a declining neighbour.*

## Revolution, Napoleon & the end of the Empire

### 49. Joseph II & Leopold II 👑 (1780–1792) — Habsburg-Lorraine
**Relation:** **sons** of Maria Theresa and Francis I; brothers of **Marie Antoinette**, whose fate in the **French Revolution** alarmed every European monarch. In 1791 Austria and Prussia issued the **Declaration of Pillnitz** backing Louis XVI.
![[germany-p2-13-french-revolution.jpg]]
*The French Revolution — the upheaval that terrified Europe's monarchies and drew the German powers into war.*

### 50. Francis II 👑 (1792–1806) — the last Holy Roman Emperor, Habsburg-Lorraine
**Relation:** **son** of Leopold II. The **Wars of the Coalitions** went badly for the Germans; the young general **Napoleon** rose to dominate Europe. After **Austerlitz (1805)** shattered the Austro-Russian armies, Francis **dissolved the Holy Roman Empire in 1806** and abdicated the imperial title — but had already re-titled himself **Emperor of Austria**, so the Habsburgs kept an imperial crown. Napoleon reorganised the German lands into the **Confederation of the Rhine**, a French satellite; some states (Bavaria, Württemberg) sided with him, absorbed neighbours, and became kingdoms. Prussia, after staying neutral too long, was crushed at **Jena–Auerstedt (1806)**.

*The Battle of Austerlitz (1805): Napoleon's masterpiece, forcing Austria to terms.*
![[germany-p2-15-francis-ii-hre-dissolved.jpg]]
*Francis II — the last Holy Roman Emperor, who dissolved the thousand-year Empire in 1806 and became Emperor of Austria instead.*
![[germany-p2-chart-04-habsburg-lorraine.jpg]]
*The Habsburg-Lorraine line down to Francis II — the final emperors of the old Reich.*

### The Congress of Vienna & the German Confederation (1815)
Napoleon was beaten at **Leipzig (1813)**, then finally at **Waterloo (1815)**. At the **Congress of Vienna (1814–15)**, overseen by Austria's **Metternich**, the Confederation of the Rhine was replaced by the **German Confederation (Deutscher Bund)** — ~39 states, presided over by the Austrian emperor, as ineffective as the old Empire (it even included the kings of Denmark and Britain as German dukes). Prussia gained a large slice of western Germany. The settlement opened **a near-century of relative European peace (1815–1914)**.

*The Congress of Vienna (1814–15): Europe's powers redraw the map after Napoleon.*

## Unification — the birth of Germany

### The 1848 Revolutions & the Frankfurt Parliament
Nationalism and reform spread; mass German emigration (to the US, Canada, South America) carried the language and traditions like the *Christmas tree* abroad. In **1848**, revolution swept Europe (the *Communist Manifesto* was published in Germany that year). The **Frankfurt National Assembly** debated a German nation — **Großdeutschland** (with Austria) vs **Kleindeutschland** (Prussia-led, without Austria) — and offered the crown to **Frederick William IV of Prussia**, who **refused it**. By 1849 the revolution collapsed and unity stayed theoretical.
![[germany-p2-17-frankfurt-parliament-1848.jpg]]
*The Frankfurt Parliament (1848) in the Paulskirche — the first attempt at a unified German nation.*

### Bismarck: "iron and blood"
In 1862 **Otto von Bismarck** became minister-president of Prussia, declaring Germany would be forged by **"iron and blood."** He engineered three short, decisive wars:
- **Second Schleswig War (1864)** — Prussia and Austria beat Denmark, then quarrelled over the spoils.
- **Austro-Prussian War (1866)** — settled in 7 weeks at **Königgrätz**; Austria was expelled from German affairs, the Confederation dissolved and replaced by the **North German Confederation** under Prussia.
- **Franco-Prussian War (1870–71)** — Bismarck maneuvered France into looking the aggressor, rallying the southern German states; a decisive Prussian victory.
![[germany-p2-18-bismarck.jpg]]
*Otto von Bismarck — the "Iron Chancellor" who forged Germany through war and diplomacy.*

### 51. Wilhelm I 👑 (1871–1888) — German Emperor (Kaiser), Hohenzollern
**Relation:** King of Prussia, proclaimed the **first German Kaiser** in the Hall of Mirrors at Versailles in **1871** as the last states (Bavaria last) joined. The **German Empire** was born — Kaiser, Chancellor Bismarck, and a Reichstag. Note the family tree now shows **two imperial lines in parallel** — the German Kaiser (Wilhelm I) and the Austrian Emperor (**Franz Joseph**), a situation lasting until 1918.
![[germany-p2-19-german-empire-proclaimed-1871.jpg]]
*The proclamation of the German Empire, Versailles 1871 (Anton von Werner) — Wilhelm I becomes Kaiser.*
![[germany-p2-chart-05-german-empire.jpg]]
*The tree splits into the German Empire (Wilhelm I → Wilhelm II) and the Austrian Empire (Franz Joseph) — two Kaisers at once.*

### 52. Wilhelm II 👑 (1888–1918) — German Emperor, Hohenzollern
**Relation:** **grandson** of Wilhelm I (skipping his short-reigning father). Impulsive and reckless, he clashed with Bismarck and **dismissed him**. Germany boomed — a centre of science, industry and culture, with colonies in Africa and Asia — but a naval and military build-up alarmed Britain, France and Russia. The **Triple Alliance** (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) faced the **Triple Entente** (Britain, France, Russia).

**World War I** began with the assassination of **Archduke Franz Ferdinand (28 June 1914)**. Germany's invasion of France through Belgium was halted at the **First Marne**; the war bogged into **trench warfare**. Wilhelm became a "shadow Kaiser" as the military ran the state. Russia exited after the 1917 revolution, but **US entry** (provoked by submarine warfare and the Zimmermann telegram to Mexico) tipped the balance. By **11 November 1918** Germany surrendered. The **Austrian and Ottoman empires dissolved**; Germany lost its colonies and territory, accepted war guilt and reparations. **Wilhelm II abdicated** — the German Empire was over, and the **Weimar Republic** began, its humiliation setting the stage for the next catastrophe.

*Kaiser Wilhelm II — the last German Emperor, whose recklessness helped drag Germany into WWI.*

## The through-line
The first lesson of this history is that an **elective crown** and **partible inheritance** kept Germany from fusing like France or England. The sequel followed from it: the **Thirty Years' War** wrecked the Holy Roman Empire and locked in its fragmentation, but out of the wreckage rose **two** German great powers — **Habsburg Austria** and **Hohenzollern Prussia**. Their century-long duel (Silesia, the Seven Years' War, 1866) was finally resolved not by the old Empire but by **Prussia's Bismarck**, who welded the German states into a single **Kleindeutschland** empire in 1871 — deliberately *without* Austria. That new, industrial, militarised Germany then over-reached into **World War I**, ending both German and Austrian imperial monarchies in 1918.
