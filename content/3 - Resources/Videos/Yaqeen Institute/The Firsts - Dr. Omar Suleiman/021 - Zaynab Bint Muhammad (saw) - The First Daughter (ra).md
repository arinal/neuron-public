# Episode 21 — Zaynab bint Muhammad ﷺ (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 21 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tCSDgXCZliE) · ⏱ 35:09

**Who they were:** Zaynab al-Kubra (ra) was the eldest daughter of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and Khadijah (ra), known for the painful trials of her marriage to a non-Muslim husband during the early years of Islam and her unwavering faith and loyalty to her father ﷺ.

## Summary
Zaynab (ra) was born when the Prophet ﷺ was about thirty years old. As the eldest child, she helped her mother Khadijah (ra) and mentored her younger sisters Ruqayyah, Umm Kulthum, and Fatimah (ra). The Prophet ﷺ loved and praised her greatly. She married young to Abu al-As ibn al-Rabi' ibn Abd al-Uzza, who was also her cousin — his mother was Hala, the sister of Khadijah whom the Prophet ﷺ would later remember fondly in Medina. As a wedding gift, Khadijah (ra) gave Zaynab a special necklace.

When revelation came, Zaynab (ra) was among the first to embrace Islam, while her husband Abu al-As was away on trade. Unlike Abu Lahab's sons, who divorced Ruqayyah and Umm Kulthum to harm the Prophet ﷺ, Abu al-As — a noble and honest merchant — resisted tribal pressure to divorce Zaynab, fearing only that people would say he embraced Islam merely for his wife. At that early stage, marriage between a believer and a polytheist was not yet forbidden, so the couple remained together and had two children: a son, Ali, who died young, and a daughter, the famous Umamah.

The hijrah created a defining crisis. The Prophet ﷺ permitted Zaynab to stay in Mecca with her husband so her family would not be torn apart, while he made hijrah to Medina, leaving behind both his late wife's grave and his eldest daughter. At the Battle of Badr, Abu al-As was forced out to fight against the Muslims and was captured by Abdullah ibn Jubair (ra). Zaynab sent her mother's necklace to ransom him. When the Prophet ﷺ saw the necklace, he wept, remembering Khadijah, and asked the companions to free the prisoner and return the necklace to her.

After Badr, Allah commanded the separation of believing women from polytheist husbands, so Zaynab was sent to Medina, suffering a miscarriage during a skirmish on the way. She joined her father in Medina but refused to remarry for six years, holding out hope that Abu al-As would accept Islam. Years later, Abu al-As was captured a second time when a caravan he traveled with was intercepted by a party led by Zayd ibn Harithah (ra). Reaching Zaynab at night, he still would not accept Islam, so after Fajr she publicly proclaimed that she had granted him her protection — which the Prophet ﷺ honored.

Abu al-As returned to Mecca, repaid all the trusts and debts owed to him, then declared his Islam and returned to Medina as a believer. He asked to be reunited with Zaynab, and the Prophet ﷺ restored them to one another in Muharram of the seventh year after hijrah — nearly two decades after the trials began. Yet their joy was short-lived: within a year Zaynab (ra) passed away, devastating the Prophet ﷺ and Abu al-As. The Prophet ﷺ instructed the women, led by Umm Ayman (Barakah) (ra), in washing her body, shrouded her in his own waist-wrap, prayed her janazah, and descended into her grave at al-Baqi'. Abu al-As died about a year later.

## Key Moments
- Khadijah (ra) gave Zaynab a treasured necklace at her wedding to Abu al-As ibn al-Rabi'.
- Zaynab embraced Islam among the first, while Abu al-As refused tribal pressure to divorce her.
- The Prophet ﷺ permitted Zaynab to remain in Mecca during the hijrah so her family wouldn't be separated.
- At Badr, Abu al-As was captured; Zaynab sent her mother's necklace as ransom, moving the Prophet ﷺ to tears.
- Following revelation separating believers from polytheist spouses, Zaynab was brought to Medina.
- She refused to remarry for six years, waiting for her husband to accept Islam.
- After a second capture, Zaynab publicly granted Abu al-As her protection following Fajr prayer.
- Abu al-As repaid his debts, accepted Islam, and was reunited with Zaynab — who died less than a year later.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Faith sometimes requires enduring painful, complicated family situations for the sake of Allah.
- The trials of the earliest Muslims came in many forms — Zaynab's was a divided household between a persecuted father and a non-Muslim husband.
- The Prophet ﷺ showed no favoritism to his family, applying the same justice to Abu al-As as to all other prisoners.
- Genuine sincerity matters: Abu al-As delayed Islam to avoid accusations of converting for convenience, then settled his debts before declaring faith.
- Patience and steadfast hope, as shown by Zaynab's six years of waiting, can be rewarded even when relief comes through hardship.
- The deep love between the Prophet ﷺ and his family is reflected in his tears, his shrouding Zaynab in his own garment, and his descending into her grave.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on freeing Abu al-As at Badr: "If you will, then free her prisoner and return her necklace to her."
- The Prophet ﷺ instructing the women washing Zaynab: wash her an odd number of times — three, five, or seven — with water and camphor, and inform him when finished.
- When Hala's voice reminded him of Khadijah, the Prophet ﷺ would say, "Allahumma Hala" — "O Allah, let it be Hala."
