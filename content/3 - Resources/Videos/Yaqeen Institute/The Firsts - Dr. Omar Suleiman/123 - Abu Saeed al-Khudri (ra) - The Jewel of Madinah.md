# Episode 123 — Abu Saeed al-Khudri (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 63 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zN1bek9maYc) · ⏱ 57:41

**Who they were:** Abu Saeed al-Khudri (ra), born Sa'd ibn Malik, was the great scholar and Mufti of Madinah — one of the seven companions who narrated the most ahadith from the Prophet ﷺ (around 1,170 narrations), and a lifelong Madani who never left the city of the Prophet ﷺ for long.

## Summary
Abu Saeed al-Khudri (ra) is among the most frequently cited narrators in the books of hadith, yet his personal story is among the least known. He is counted among the seven companions — alongside Abu Hurayrah, Aisha, Ibn Umar, Anas ibn Malik, Jabir, and Abdullah ibn Abbas — from whom over 60% of the Prophet's ﷺ traditions are preserved. His title is the Mufti of Madinah, and he narrated not only from the Prophet ﷺ but from all ten companions promised Paradise. Born to parents who both accepted Islam among the Ansar (from the tribe of Khazraj), the young Sa'd was taken as a ten-year-old boy to pledge allegiance to the Prophet ﷺ when he arrived in Madinah; his mother gave her own pledge directly.

His family was deeply intertwined with the early Muslim community: his half-brother Qatadah ibn al-Nu'man and his sister Furay'ah bint Malik (about whose widowhood a ruling of the waiting period was revealed) were notable companions. At the Battle of Uhud, when Abu Saeed was thirteen, his father Malik brought him to the Prophet ﷺ hoping he would be accepted as a fighter, but the Prophet ﷺ turned him back as too young. His father then fought and was martyred at Uhud — and in his final act, instinctively caught the Prophet's ﷺ dripping blood so it would not touch the ground, prompting the Prophet ﷺ to say, "Whoever wants to look at a man whose blood and my blood have mixed, let him look at Malik."

Left as an orphan caring for his widowed mother, the family sank into poverty. When his mother sent him to ask the Prophet ﷺ for help, he overheard the Prophet ﷺ teaching that whoever seeks dignity and sufficiency through patience will be granted it — "No one is given a gift better and more expansive than patience." Moved, Abu Saeed chose not to ask, and turned instead to Allah. Allah blessed their household so abundantly that he later said no house of the Ansar became wealthier than theirs.

Too young for Badr and Uhud, Abu Saeed went on to fight in every subsequent battle and was among the people of Ridwan who took the pledge under the tree. Through his many narrations we glimpse his close relationship with the Prophet ﷺ — describing his modesty ("more shy than a bride in her chamber"), his poverty, his prayer in the leaking mud-roofed mosque, and his care in visiting even sick teenagers. He transmitted famous hadiths on fasting, the rewards of striving in Allah's path, ruqyah, and the virtues of the three mosques and the Rawdah ("what is between my home and my pulpit is one of the gardens of Paradise").

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away (when Abu Saeed was 23), he became a teacher in the Prophet's Mosque, sitting to teach Quran five verses at a time and narrating hadith to students who came from around the world. He greeted seekers of knowledge as "the lovers of the Prophet ﷺ" and the "inheritors of the Prophet ﷺ." Living through the fitan, he sided with Ali (ra), spoke truth to rulers without fear, yet strove to keep the community united. He survived the brutal attack on Madinah in 63 AH, where he was robbed of even the stuffing of his pillows but spared in a cave when he refused to fight a fellow Muslim. He died in his 80s, having personally chosen his burial spot in al-Baqi and instructing his sons in the very deathbed practices he had narrated from the Prophet ﷺ.

## Key Moments
- As a ten-year-old, taken by his parents to pledge allegiance to the Prophet ﷺ upon his arrival in Madinah; his mother gave her pledge directly.
- Brought by his father to fight at Uhud at age thirteen but turned back by the Prophet ﷺ as too young.
- His father Malik was martyred at Uhud after catching the Prophet's ﷺ blood; the Prophet ﷺ said their blood had mixed.
- The Prophet ﷺ personally delivered the news of his father's martyrdom to the grieving thirteen-year-old.
- Overhearing the Prophet's ﷺ teaching on patience, he chose not to ask for charity — and his household was blessed to become the wealthiest of the Ansar.
- Narrated the incident of reciting al-Fatihah as ruqyah on a non-Muslim chief during a journey, approved by the Prophet ﷺ.
- As Mufti of Madinah, taught Quran and hadith in the Prophet's Mosque, welcoming students as "the inheritors of the Prophet ﷺ."
- On his deathbed, walked with his son to choose his exact burial place in al-Baqi and instructed his sons in the prophetic practices for the dying.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Patience and turning to Allah for sufficiency, rather than to people, opens doors of provision and dignity — "No one is given a gift better and more expansive than patience."
- The Ansar's love for the Prophet ﷺ was so total that they offered their own children and lives in his cause, as Abu Saeed's father did at Uhud.
- Pleasure with Allah as Lord, Islam as religion, and Muhammad ﷺ as Prophet makes Paradise mandatory — and striving in Allah's path elevates one's rank immensely.
- Preserving and spreading the words of the Prophet ﷺ is a lifelong service that extends one's good through generations — Abu Saeed's narrations live on through his sons and students.
- One must not let fear of people prevent speaking the truth, while still working to keep the community united.
- Changing evil with the hand, then the tongue, then hating it in the heart — the last being the weakest of faith.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ at Uhud: "Whoever wants to look at a man whose blood and my blood have mixed, let him look at Malik."
- "No one of you will be given a gift, a blessing, that is better and more expansive than patience."
- "Whoever is pleased with Allah as his Lord, with Islam as his religion, and with Muhammad ﷺ as his Prophet — Paradise has become mandatory for him."
- "No one should let the fear of people stop them from speaking the truth when they know the truth."
- "Whoever among you sees an evil, let him change it with his hand; if he cannot, then with his tongue; and if he cannot, then let him hate it in his heart, and that is the weakest of faith."
- "What is between my home and my pulpit is one of the gardens of Paradise, and my pulpit is upon my pool (Hawd)."
- On fasting: "No one fasts a single day for the sake of Allah except that Allah places between him and the Hellfire a distance of seventy years."
