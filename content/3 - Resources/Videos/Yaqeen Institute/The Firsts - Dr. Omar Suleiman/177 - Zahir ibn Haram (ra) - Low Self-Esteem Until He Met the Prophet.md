# Episode 177 — Zahir ibn Haram (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 9 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9JtW5lP6tro) · ⏱ 17:25

**Who they were:** Zahir ibn Haram (ra) was a poor bedouin companion from a tribe in the desert north of Medina, known for his physical deformity and low self-esteem, whom the Prophet ﷺ embraced and uplifted with affection and gentle humor.

## Summary
Zahir (ra) came from one of the bedouin tribes living to the north and northwest of Medina — among the *Banu Ghatafan* type tribes that were tribal, rough desert dwellers, and largely indifferent to questions of religion. These surrounding tribes represented a strategic vulnerability to the Prophet ﷺ; some of them joined the confederate forces (the *Ahzab*) that sought to wipe out the Muslim community, not out of love for the idols of Quraysh, but because they preferred the predictable and familiar and saw the new message as a threat to their alliances and economy. His tribe was among the closest to Medina and thus interacted with the community most, though they mostly embraced Islam later when other Arab tribes did.

Zahir himself, however, embraced Islam early during one of his trips into Medina. He was a young bedouin man, poor, physically deformed, not pleasant-looking, and without a strong tribe to protect him. Yet every time he came to Medina he brought the Prophet ﷺ a gift — some fruit, vegetables, milk, or whatever a bedouin could carry. In return, the Prophet ﷺ always had something ready for him: new clothes, perfume, or gifts to take back, as though he kept a stockpile waiting for Zahir's visits.

The Prophet ﷺ described him with the famous words that Zahir was "the bedouin of the family of Muhammad" — meaning Zahir was like a beloved relative living out in the desert. In a society where every city dweller had "their" person from the outskirts, this declaration instantly elevated Zahir's worth in the eyes of the entire community.

The most pivotal moment came in the marketplace. The Prophet ﷺ spotted Zahir selling his goods, snuck up behind him, grabbed him, and playfully began calling out, "Who will buy this slave from me?" Zahir at first struggled to be let go, then realized it was the Prophet ﷺ and relaxed into the embrace, savoring the closeness. He said, "O Messenger of Allah, you would find me to be cheap — no one would want to buy me anyway." The Prophet ﷺ turned him around and told him, "But in the eyes of Allah you are priceless; you are worth so much to Allah."

The only other detail known about Zahir is that Ibn Abd al-Barr records him among the people of Badr — no small thing, since the Prophet ﷺ called the people of Badr the best of the companions, just as the angels of Badr were the best of the angels. This man who thought so little of himself may belong to the most elite group of the Sahaba.

## Key Moments
- Zahir (ra) embraced Islam on one of his trading trips into Medina, despite his tribe largely remaining distant from the faith.
- He brought a small gift for the Prophet ﷺ every time he visited, and the Prophet ﷺ always had clothes, perfume, or gifts ready in return.
- The Prophet ﷺ called him "the bedouin of the family of Muhammad," treating him like a beloved desert-dwelling relative.
- The Prophet ﷺ would rise to embrace him, care for him, and provide for his journey back to the desert.
- In the marketplace, the Prophet ﷺ snuck up, grabbed him, and joked, "Who will buy this slave from me?" — laughing with him before the companions.
- When Zahir said no one would want to buy him because he was "cheap," the Prophet ﷺ turned him around and told him he was priceless in the sight of Allah.
- He is recorded among the people of Badr, potentially placing him among the most elite of the companions.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ noticed and uplifted the overlooked — the woman who cleaned the masjid, and the lowly bedouin from the outskirts.
- True self-worth is measured by one's value to Allah, not by appearance, wealth, or tribal standing — "in the eyes of Allah you are priceless."
- The Prophet's humor was always used to lighten the mood and build people up, never to break them down.
- A joke is not righteous merely because it is "true"; it must also have a truthful, good intention behind it — to build confidence and goodness, not to wound.
- Be careful joking about people's appearance or vulnerabilities; people often laugh along not out of enjoyment but because they lack the confidence to defend themselves, and a cruel nickname can stick for life.
- One transformative sentence of kindness can sustain a person for the rest of their life — Zahir could replay "you are priceless in the sight of Allah" forever.
