# Episode 49 — Abu Jandal, Abdullah, & Suhayl Ibn Amr (ra): Switching Sides

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 49 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qsFO9ZHig7o) · ⏱ 1:11:46

**Who they were:** A remarkable family of Quraysh — Suhayl ibn Amr (ra), the famed orator and negotiator of Quraysh who resisted Islam for over twenty years before embracing it and dying a redeemed martyr, and his two sons Abdullah ibn Suhayl (ra) and Abu Jandal (ra), among the earliest Muslims who endured great hardship for their faith.

## Summary
The episode opens with Suhayl's elder son Abdullah, who became Muslim as a teenager. In a striking conversation with the still-pagan Umar (ra), Abdullah agonizes over whether a man can "live with two hearts" — one to please his family and one true to his conscience. Umar advises him to follow his conscience courageously even if it makes them enemies. Abdullah then confronts his father, declaring his Islam; Suhayl threatens, disowns, and eventually imprisons and tortures him.

Suhayl ibn Amr was the pride of Quraysh — their orator, negotiator, and a man admired by all the tribes. Remarkably, nearly his entire family embraced Islam early: his children Abdullah, Abu Jandal, Sahla (wife of Abu Hudhayfa), and Umm Kulthum, as well as all his brothers (including As-Sakran, first husband of Sawda bint Zam'a (ra)). Suhayl alone held out, his tribalism overriding even his loyalty to his persecuted family. Abdullah feigned renouncing Islam to escape imprisonment and continued secretly aiding the Prophet's ﷺ cause.

At Badr, Suhayl marched out with Abdullah, believing his son a pagan — but Abdullah defected at night to join the Muslims, the only person to switch sides in that way. Suhayl was captured; when Umar (ra) asked permission to knock out his teeth so his eloquent tongue could never harm the Muslims again, the Prophet ﷺ refused, prophesying that one day Suhayl's mouth would please Umar and that he would take a stance Umar would envy. Suhayl ransomed himself and returned to Mecca, later imprisoning his younger son Abu Jandal as well once his Islam was discovered.

At Hudaybiyyah, Suhayl came as Quraysh's negotiator — the Prophet ﷺ took his name (meaning "ease") as a good omen. As the treaty was finalized, Abu Jandal escaped his torture chamber and ran over a hundred miles in chains, bloodied, crying out for rescue. By the treaty's terms, the Prophet ﷺ had to return him to his father — an agonizing test for all the Muslims. Abu Jandal remained steadfast in faith. Later he escaped again with Abu Basir, and together they formed a band of some seventy fugitive Muslims who disrupted Quraysh's trade routes until Abu Sufyan begged the Prophet ﷺ to absorb them into Medina.

At the conquest of Mecca, Suhayl — one of the few not granted general amnesty — was pardoned through his son Abdullah's intercession, and finally embraced Islam after the Prophet ﷺ quoted Prophet Yusuf's words of forgiveness. Though among the last to convert (the "tulaqa"), Suhayl became a devout worshipper, famed for prayer, charity, and weeping at the Ka'bah. When the Prophet ﷺ died and Meccans rumbled about reverting, Suhayl stood at the Ka'bah and stilled them with the same words Abu Bakr (ra) used in Medina — fulfilling the Prophet's ﷺ prophecy. He and his sons then fought together in Allah's path; Abdullah was martyred at Yamama, and Suhayl, Abu Jandal, and their families later died in the plague of Amwas as martyrs.

- Abu Jandal escapes his torture chamber in chains and runs to Hudaybiyyah, only to be returned under the treaty.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Integrity means refusing to "live with two hearts" — courage to follow one's true convictions over the approval of family or society.
- The Prophet's ﷺ insight saw potential good in Suhayl that no one else perceived; we should not write people off.
- The ethics of war forbid mutilation: "I will not mutilate him, lest Allah mutilate me."
- The trial of Hudaybiyyah teaches submission to divine wisdom even when events seem unjust — Abu Jandal's steadfastness despite being sent back is profound yaqeen (certainty).
- Redemption is always possible: Suhayl, who resisted for over twenty years, became a devout worshipper and martyr.
- Those who raced to Islam early (the sabiqun al-awwalun) hold a rank no latecomer easily equals — but everyone can still strive to catch up through sincere effort and sacrifice.

## Notable Quotes & Narnations
- The Prophet ﷺ to Umar (ra) about Suhayl: "Leave him, for it may be that one day this mouth will please you... and perhaps he will take a stance you would envy him over."
- The Prophet ﷺ at Hudaybiyyah: "Suhayl has come to you, and so your affairs have been eased for you."
- The Prophet ﷺ at the conquest of Mecca: "There is no blame upon you today; may Allah forgive you, and He is the most merciful of those who show mercy." (echoing Prophet Yusuf)
- Suhayl in Mecca, echoing Abu Bakr (ra): "Whoever used to worship Muhammad ﷺ, Muhammad ﷺ has died; but whoever worships Allah, Allah is ever-living and does not die. Do not be the last to enter Islam and the first to leave it."
- Suhayl to Abu Sufyan and al-Harith on Umar's (ra) ordering of the line: "Be angry at yourselves... How will it be if they are called to the gates of Jannah and we are left behind?"
- "Whoever dies in the plague (ta'un) is a martyr (shaheed)." — saying of the Prophet ﷺ referenced regarding their deaths at Amwas.
