# Episode 143 — Muhammad ibn Maslamah ibn Salamah al-Ansari (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 43 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4xGVVShGlNE) · ⏱ 1:09:33

**Who they were:** A companion from the Aws tribe of Madinah who, remarkably, carried the name Muhammad from before the Prophet ﷺ received revelation. Known as the *Faris* (knight) of Rasulullah ﷺ, he was the trusted watchman and intelligence operative of Madinah, sent on the riskiest missions to guard the Prophet ﷺ and the Muslims.

## Summary
Muhammad ibn Maslamah (ra) was one of only roughly five Arabs known to bear the name Muhammad before the Prophet ﷺ — a name al-Qadi Iyad explains spread among a few when rabbis and priests foretold the coming prophet's name. Eighteen years younger than the Prophet ﷺ, he embraced Islam early at the hands of Mus'ab ibn Umayr, even before Sa'd ibn Mu'adh and Usayd ibn Hudayr. Tall, very strong, dark-skinned, handsome, and awe-inspiring (*dhu haybah*), he was a man of few words but great presence and action. He brought his wives, children (he had 16, named after the Muhajirun), and siblings — including his brother Mahmud and sister Umm Abs — into Islam.

He was the most skilled fighter on horse and on foot, never once seen taking a step back in battle, and was deeply analytical — able to read a battlefield, count an army, and analyze points of attack. For these qualities the Prophet ﷺ paired him as host with Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah (ra), the trustworthy one of this ummah, a near-twin in disposition. He attended every battle except Tabuk — and only because the Prophet ﷺ appointed him as governor of Madinah during that expedition. He became the perpetual *murabit*, the watchman embodying every hadith about guarding the borders for the sake of Allah.

After Badr, the Prophet ﷺ assigned him to deal with Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf, a wealthy poet, warlord, and politician of Banu Nadir who actively plotted against the Muslims, authored vulgar poetry about Muslim women, and travelled to Mecca to incite the Quraysh — incitement that directly fuelled the Battle of Uhud. Muhammad ibn Maslamah, with the convert Abu Na'ilah, gained access to Ka'b's fortress through clever speech (permitted by the Prophet ﷺ), drew him out at night by praising his perfume, and eliminated him, returning safely.

He served as the intelligence of Madinah, foiling plots and overseeing the expulsions of Banu Qaynuqa and Banu Nadir. He patrolled the camp the night before Uhud with 50 men, defended the Prophet ﷺ during Khalid's flanking attack, scouted Khaybar (where he avenged his brother Mahmud, the first martyr of that day, against the warrior Marhab), occupied the wells at Hudaybiyah, and captured the powerful prisoner Thumamah ibn Uthal. At the conquest of Mecca he rode ahead to reassure the Meccans and walked before the Prophet ﷺ holding the reins of al-Qaswa during the first tawaf.

Under Umar (ra), who loved him for the same trustworthiness he loved in Abu Ubaidah, he became the caliph's chief investigator of governors — confronting Amr ibn al-Aas, burning Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas's gate, and being himself admonished by Umar for missing a poor widow's zakat. Heeding the Prophet's ﷺ command to break his sword when Muslims fought one another, he withdrew entirely from the fitna following Uthman's assassination — retiring to the wilderness of ar-Rabadha — until he was killed in his home by a Syrian man angered by his neutrality. He was martyred at age 77 and buried in Madinah.

## Key Moments
- Embraced Islam at the hands of Mus'ab ibn Umayr, among the very earliest of the Ansar.
- Paired by the Prophet ﷺ as host of Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah (ra) due to their matching trustworthy character.
- Appointed governor of Madinah during the Battle of Tabuk — the only campaign he missed.
- Carried out the operation against Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf, whose incitement had helped galvanize Quraysh for Uhud.
- Patrolled the camp before Uhud with 50 men and defended the Prophet ﷺ when Khalid attacked from behind.
- Scouted Khaybar and avenged his martyred brother Mahmud by slaying the warrior Marhab.
- Captured Thumamah ibn Uthal and foiled an attack of 50 men at Hudaybiyah before negotiations.
- Served as Umar's investigator of governors (Amr ibn al-Aas, Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas), and was himself held to account over a neglected widow's zakat.
- Broke his sword and withdrew to ar-Rabadha during the fitna, as the Prophet ﷺ had instructed him.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Accept the role Allah created you for and excel in it without trying to veer outside your specialty.
- The Prophet's ﷺ pattern was mercy and forgiveness; striking against a present, atrocity-committing danger was the rare exception — sometimes itself a mercy to the victims.
- "Break your sword" when fitna erupts: if you cannot control yourself, abandon every avenue of participation — withdraw from harmful conversations, leave the situations that lead you to sin.
- Trustworthiness (being safe from hypocrisy) earns a closeness and trust that nothing else can buy.
- True leadership embraces mutual accountability — Umar accepted being "straightened like an arrow," and held even beloved companions to account.
- Beware the supplication of the wronged; the dua of a sincere believer may strike long after the moment passes (as with Usama ibn Qatadah).

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on the night-watch role: "Two eyes will not be touched by the Fire — the eye that weeps from the fear of Allah, and the eye that keeps watch guarding the borders of the Muslims."
- The Prophet ﷺ, after speaking with Jibril: "That was Jibril, and he told me, 'This is Muhammad ibn Maslamah.' Had he greeted us, we would have returned the greeting." — and Jibril "kept urging me concerning the neighbor until I thought he would make him an heir."
- The Prophet ﷺ giving him the sword: "Fight the polytheists with it. But when you see the Muslims fighting one another, strike it against Uhud until it breaks, then sit in your house until death comes to you."
- Qur'an cited: Surah an-Nisa 4:51–52 and Surah al-Ma'idah 5:82, on those given scripture who side with falsehood against the believers.
