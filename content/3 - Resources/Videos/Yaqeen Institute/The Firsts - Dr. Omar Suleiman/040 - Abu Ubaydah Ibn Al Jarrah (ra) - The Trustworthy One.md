# Episode 40 — Abu Ubaydah Amir ibn Abdillah ibn al-Jarrah (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 40 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pvOonz9_Yuc) · ⏱ 52:00

**Who they were:** A Qurashi companion from the tribe of Banu al-Harith ibn Fihr and one of the ten promised Paradise, Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah (ra) was a brilliant military general renowned for his asceticism and trustworthiness — the man the Prophet ﷺ named "the trustworthy one (al-Amin) of this Ummah."

## Summary
Abu Ubaydah's given name was Amir ibn Abdillah ibn al-Jarrah, though he is known by his grandfather al-Jarrah rather than his father. Dr. Omar explains the scholarly reasons for this: a grandfather sometimes raised or eclipsed the father in fame, or — as applied here — a father known for something exceedingly negative could be passed over. Abu Ubaydah grew up in a family of archers and became an extraordinarily skilled warrior, able to track armies by their footprints, ride and shoot at once, and split targets in two; by twelve he surpassed the most skilled fighters, being prepared for greatness even as a youth.

He accepted Islam as a teenager (around 18–19) at the hands of Abu Bakr (ra), among the first ten Muslims, and joined the school of Dar al-Arqam. Little is recorded of his Meccan life beyond his patience under torture; he made the migration to Abyssinia and later the hijrah to Medina, earning the distinction of being among "Ashab al-Hijratayn" (those who made both migrations). His Ansari brother in Medina was Mu'adh ibn Jabal (ra). He was a hafiz of the Qur'an who memorized revelation as it descended, and he fought in every battle alongside the Prophet ﷺ, always in a leadership capacity.

At Badr, later sources narrate the difficult account that his own father pursued him to kill him, and Abu Ubaydah was forced to kill his father in self-defense — said to be why he is called "ibn al-Jarrah." Some commentators link Surah al-Mujadila verse 22 to this, describing believers who loved the Messenger ﷺ more than their own kin. At Uhud, when the Prophet's ﷺ helmet was driven into his cheek, Abu Ubaydah pulled out the rings with his teeth, losing two of his own teeth — and it was said his speech became more beautiful with the resulting impediment than before.

The Prophet ﷺ praised him repeatedly, most famously when Christian leaders from Najran requested a trustworthy man to teach them; he ﷺ stood Abu Ubaydah up and declared, "Every Ummah has an Amin, and the Amin of this Ummah is Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah." He led the Prophet's ﷺ central banner into Mecca on the day of Fath, and was famously appointed over the "expedition of the whale" recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari.

After the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr (ra) presented Abu Ubaydah and Umar to the Ansar at Saqifa as candidates for the caliphate. Under Abu Bakr and Umar he served as a general against the Byzantines and became the governor (amir) of al-Sham, where he built mosques, hospitals, and a dam. Umar (ra) appointed him over Khalid ibn al-Walid (ra), and the two complemented each other — Khalid the fierce warrior, Abu Ubaydah the diplomat. He died of the plague of Amwas, appointing Mu'adh ibn Jabal (ra) as his successor, his entire family believed to have died with him. Umar wept, saying that had Abu Ubaydah lived, he would have appointed him caliph and met his Lord in peace.

## Key Moments
- Accepted Islam as a teenager at the hands of Abu Bakr (ra), among the first ten Muslims, and studied in Dar al-Arqam.
- Made both migrations (to Abyssinia and Medina), becoming one of "Ashab al-Hijratayn."
- At Badr, the difficult later narration of being forced to kill his own father in self-defense.
- At Uhud, pulled the Prophet's ﷺ helmet rings from his cheek with his teeth, losing two teeth in the process.
- The Prophet ﷺ named him "the Amin (trustworthy one) of this Ummah" before the delegation from Najran.
- Carried the central banner leading the army into Mecca during Fath Makkah; pitched the Prophet's ﷺ tent.
- Led the "expedition of the whale" recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, where 300 men survived on a single date per day before Allah provided a massive whale.
- As governor of al-Sham, lived in stark poverty; Umar wept on visiting his bare home, saying "the dunya changed us all except you, O Abu Ubaydah."
- Died in the plague of Amwas, refusing to abandon his army and embracing martyrdom.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True worthiness for leadership often lies with those who do not seek it — Abu Ubaydah had to be repeatedly pushed into command because he had no desire for position.
- Trustworthiness (amana) is the heart of leadership; the Prophet ﷺ gave his own quality of al-Amin to none but Abu Ubaydah.
- Zuhd (asceticism) means remaining unchanged by worldly status — even as governor of al-Sham, he kept nothing of this world.
- Obedience and humility: when the Prophet ﷺ forbade disputing over leadership, Abu Ubaydah readily followed Amr ibn al-As without complaint.
- Contentment with Allah's decree: he chose to stay with his army during the plague, content to die as a martyr rather than flee.
- The worth of righteous men cannot be quantified — Umar wished for "a room full of men like Abu Ubaydah" over all the gold of the world.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Every Ummah has an Amin (trustworthy one), and the Amin of this Ummah is Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah."
- The Prophet ﷺ described him as "qawiyy amin" — strong and trustworthy.
- On his speech impediment: "No appearance/sound was ever more beautiful than that of Abu Ubaydah" after he lost his teeth.
- Aisha (ra), asked who was most beloved to the Prophet ﷺ after Abu Bakr and Umar, replied: "Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah."
- The exchange on the plague — Abu Ubaydah: "Are you fleeing from the decree of Allah?" Umar (ra): "We are fleeing from the decree of Allah to the decree of Allah."
- The hadith narrated by Abu Ubaydah: one who dies from a plague is a martyr (shaheed).
- Umar (ra): "Had Abu Ubaydah lived, I would have appointed him [caliph] and met my Lord in peace."
- Umar's wish: "I wish I had a room full of men like Abu Ubaydah al-Jarrah."
- Mu'adh ibn Jabal (ra) at his death: "A man truthful to his Lord, trustworthy — and Allah was great in his heart."
