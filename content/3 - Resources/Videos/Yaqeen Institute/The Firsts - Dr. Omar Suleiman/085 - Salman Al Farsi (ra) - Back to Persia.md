# Episode 85 — Salman al-Farsi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 85 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zu_lJIPeW2M) · ⏱ 1:11:39

**Who they were:** Salman al-Farsi (ra) was a Persian seeker of truth who journeyed from Zoroastrianism through Christianity to Islam, became known as "Salman ibn al-Islam," was declared by the Prophet ﷺ to be "one of us, the people of the household," and later helped bring Islam to his native Persia.

## Summary
This episode picks up the often-omitted second half of Salman's story — his life after meeting the Prophet ﷺ. Dr. Omar opens with Ibn al-Qayyim's meditation on Salman in *al-Fawa'id*, contrasting those who drowned in heedlessness (Abu Talib, al-Walid ibn al-Mughira, Abu Jahl) with those who rushed toward guidance from afar (Salman from Persia, Suhayb from Rome, Bilal, the Najashi). The lesson: no one has an excuse to remain bound by cultural, social, or inherited norms when Salman overcame all of them to reach the truth.

At the Battle of the Trench (Khandaq) — Salman's first battle, fought at his own suggestion — he struggled with a great rock. The Prophet ﷺ struck it three times, and with each blow recited "The word of your Lord has been fulfilled in truth and justice," and a light flashed. The Prophet ﷺ revealed he saw the palaces of Kisra (Persia), the empire of Rome, and Yemen and Abyssinia — a sign that Islam would spread across the known world even as the Muslims feared annihilation. The Prophet ﷺ made dua that Salman would be among those who carried that light.

Salman gained a unique identity among the companions: he claimed no tribe, only "my father is Islam." When Surat al-Jumu'ah mentioned "others who have not yet joined them," the Prophet ﷺ placed his hand on Salman and said that even if faith were in the stars, men from his people would reach it — pointing to the great non-Arab scholars (Abu Hanifa, al-Bukhari, al-Tirmidhi, Hasan al-Basri and others) who became this religion's stewards. Known for his rare scholarship in four faiths, Salman was nonetheless famous for silence; Ali (ra) called him the Luqman of this Ummah.

The most famous incident is his pairing with Abu al-Darda (ra), whose excessive worship neglected his family and self. Salman taught him balance — "your self has a right, your family has a right, your Lord has a right" — and the Prophet ﷺ confirmed: "Salman has spoken the truth, and Salman has deeper understanding than you."

In the era of Abu Bakr and Umar (ra), Salman served as a general and advisor while keeping his humility, once carrying a stranger's goods through the market though he was a famed companion. He boldly challenged Umar publicly over a cloth distribution — a challenge Umar answered with explanation, not tyranny. Finally, decades after the Prophet ﷺ wrote to Kisra (who tore the letter and was prophesied to have his kingdom torn apart), Salman returned to Persia under Sa'd ibn Abi Waqas, crossed the Tigris, conquered al-Mada'in (later renamed after him), governed simply from a single room, and called his own people to Islam. He died in his eighties, weeping not from love of dunya nor fear of meeting Allah, but from fearing he had not lived up to the Prophet's ﷺ counsel to take from this world only "like the provision of a traveler."

## Key Moments
- Ibn al-Qayyim's chapter in *al-Fawa'id* contrasting Salman's spiritual identity (servant of Allah, son of Islam, wealth is poverty, garment is piety) with the worldly pride of Abu Talib.
- At Khandaq, the Prophet ﷺ strikes the rock three times, each flash revealing the future spread of Islam to Persia, Rome, Yemen, and Abyssinia; Salman alone among the companions sees the light.
- The revelation of Surat al-Jumu'ah and the Prophet ﷺ placing his hand on Salman: "Even if faith were in the stars, men from these people would reach it."
- Salman's identity as "Salman ibn al-Islam," with no tribal claim — his father is Islam, his destination is Jannah.
- The pairing with Abu al-Darda (ra) and the lesson of balance, confirmed by the Prophet ﷺ: "Salman has spoken the truth."
- Salman's public challenge to Umar (ra) over the cloth, resolved when Abdullah ibn Umar explained he had given his father his own share.
- The crossing of the Tigris with Sa'd ibn Abi Waqas, the conquest of al-Mada'in, and Salman calling his fellow Persians to Islam.
- Salman's final letter to Abu al-Darda: "The land does not sanctify anyone; only a person's deeds sanctify him."

## Lessons & Takeaways
- No cultural, social, or inherited limitation is an excuse to stay away from the truth — Salman overcame all of them.
- Islam is universal; it does not belong to any tribe, empire, or power class, and Allah preserves it through sincere seekers of any people.
- True wisdom lies in practicing silence and speaking only with intention — half of wisdom is silence.
- Religion must be balanced: the self, the family, and the Lord each have a right that must be fulfilled; extremism in worship is not piety.
- Humility and simplicity, not luxury, mark the truly honored — Salman governed Persia from a single room and gave his salary in charity.
- Take from this world only "like the provision of a traveler"; do not let dunya spoil you.
- Holiness comes from deeds, not from places.
- Allah's decree is perfect; Salman's lifetime of struggle was Allah protecting and preparing him — every dua was answered in Allah's perfect timing.
