# Episode 76 — Sa'd ibn Ubadah (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 76 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Nac6gUPmulE) · ⏱ 59:54

**Who they were:** Sa'd ibn Ubadah (ra) was the chief of the Khazraj tribe of Madinah and one of the leaders of the Ansar, renowned for his extraordinary generosity ("Sa'd al-Kamil," Sa'd the Perfect). His acceptance of Islam, alongside Sa'd ibn Mu'adh of the Aus, signaled that Islam had truly come to Madinah.

## Summary
Sa'd ibn Ubadah was the son of Ubadah ibn Dulaym, chief of Banu Sa'idah, a sub-tribe of the larger Khazraj tribe. His father died in the Bu'ath Wars, and his mother, Amrah bint Mas'ud (ra), lived to embrace Islam. Sa'd assumed leadership of Khazraj, becoming the counterpart to Sa'd ibn Mu'adh of the Aus. The Arabs understood that if "the two Sa'ds" became Muslim, Islam would inevitably take root in Yathrib — a poet even proclaimed from Mount Abu Qubays in Mecca that if the two Sa'ds accepted Islam, the Prophet ﷺ need fear no opposition.

His defining trait — inherited from his father and passed to his son Qais — was *al-karam*, generosity. The family were the celebrated hosts of Yathrib, sponsoring the poor and travelers. Sa'd would stand atop his home calling out for anyone wanting good food to come eat with him. He was the last of the famous five (Mus'ab ibn Umayr, As'ad ibn Zurarah, Usayd ibn Hudayr, and the two Sa'ds) to embrace Islam through Mus'ab's da'wah — all in a single day.

After pledging at the Second Pledge of Aqaba, Sa'd was the only Ansari captured and tortured by the Meccans, an ordeal that gave him empathy with the persecuted Muhajireen. He was eventually released after invoking his hosting relationships with the Meccan noblemen Jubayr ibn Mut'im and al-Harith ibn Harb. After the Hijrah, the Prophet ﷺ paired him in brotherhood with al-Miqdad and Khabbab ibn al-Aratt, who lived in his house for three years.

In Madinah, Sa'd made a famous du'a asking Allah to keep him wealthy so he could continue to give, recognizing that poverty would be a trial for him. He effectively ran Madinah's soup kitchen — feeding around 80 of the Ahl al-Suffah every night, providing his date reserves during the Battle of Khandaq, and sending trays of food to follow the Prophet ﷺ. The famous iftar du'a ("May the fasting break their fast with you...") was taught by the Prophet ﷺ to Sa'd. The Prophet ﷺ even raised his hands and prayed for blessings upon Sa'd and his family during his lifetime.

Sa'd was also a man of intense *ghira* (protective honor), illustrated when he declared he would act decisively if he found his wife in adultery — prompting the Prophet ﷺ to note that Allah has even greater honor. He carried the banner of the Ansar in battles after the deaths of As'ad and Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, became Sayyid al-Ansar, and played key roles in the events of Ifk and Hunayn. After the Prophet's ﷺ death, the Ansar nearly appointed Sa'd as khalifa at the Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah, but Abu Bakr's (ra) wisdom united the community, and Sa'd ultimately did not lead a rebellion. He later lived quietly and passed away in Ghouta, in Sham.

## Key Moments
- Recognized as "Sa'd al-Kamil" — strikingly handsome and skilled in reading, poetry, archery, swimming, writing, and calligraphy — his defining trait being generosity inherited across generations.
- The last of the famous five to accept Islam through Mus'ab ibn Umayr (ra), all in a single day, bringing Islam to Madinah.
- The only Ansari captured and tortured by the Meccans after the Second Pledge of Aqaba; freed by invoking his ties to Jubayr ibn Mut'im and al-Harith ibn Harb.
- Hosted al-Miqdad and Khabbab ibn al-Aratt (ra) in his home for three years after the Hijrah.
- Fed about 80 of the Ahl al-Suffah every night and provided crucial date reserves during the Battle of Khandaq.
- Responded in a low voice to the Prophet's ﷺ greeting so he would repeat the salam and Sa'd could receive its blessing repeatedly.
- Had his banner taken from him at the Conquest of Mecca after declaring it a "day of revenge," handed to Ali (ra), without being humiliated.
- The Ansar nearly pledged him as khalifa at the Saqifah; he conceded leadership to Quraish, saying "You are the leaders and we are the ministers."

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Good qualities from before Islam — like generosity — are polished and elevated by Islam: "People are like precious stones; the best of you in ignorance are the best of you in Islam, if they have understanding."
- Generosity is often a transferable family identity, passing from parents to children across generations.
- The Prophet ﷺ led by accommodating and growing each companion's natural genius rather than forcing them into a single mold.
- Crying and compassion are a mercy Allah places in the hearts of His servants; grief is permissible as long as the tongue says only what pleases Allah.
- One can continue to benefit deceased parents through ongoing charity, fulfilling their oaths and debts, and especially by building wells — sadaqah jariyah.
- Internal division (fitna) can paralyze a community in crucial moments even when external enemies cannot.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "People are like precious stones. The best of you in the days of ignorance are the best of you in Islam, if they have understanding."
- Sa'd's du'a: "O Allah, grant me praise and honor — for there is no honor except through giving, and no giving except through wealth. O Allah, little is not fit for me, nor I for little."
- The iftar du'a taught by the Prophet ﷺ to Sa'd: "May the fasting break their fast with you, may the righteous eat your food, and may the angels send peace upon you."
- The Prophet ﷺ at Sa'd's illness: "Allah does not punish for the tears of the eye, nor for the grief of the heart, but He punishes for this" (pointing to the tongue).
- When Sa'd asked the best charity for his deceased mother, the Prophet ﷺ replied: "Water" — and Sa'd dug wells, saying, "This is for the mother of Sa'd."
