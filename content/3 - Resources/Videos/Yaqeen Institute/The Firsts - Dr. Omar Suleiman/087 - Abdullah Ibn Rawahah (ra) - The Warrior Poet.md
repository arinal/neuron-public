# Episode 87 — Abdullah ibn Rawahah (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 87 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wsAGvOymTQY) · ⏱ 56:11

**Who they were:** One of the three poets of the Prophet ﷺ (alongside Hassan ibn Thabit and Ka'b ibn Malik) and the first among them, Abdullah ibn Rawahah (ra) was a warrior-poet of the Ansar, a trusted envoy of the Prophet ﷺ, and a martyr at the Battle of Mu'tah.

## Summary
Abdullah ibn Rawahah (ra) was from the tribe of Khazraj in Medina. Among the Ansar he was rare in being highly literate, eloquent, and famous as a poet even in the days of ignorance (jahiliyyah) — to the point that the Prophet ﷺ knew his poetry before meeting him. He was renowned for his sharp wits: he could estimate the weight or value of objects at a glance and read people's lineages and traits. When he finally saw the Prophet ﷺ, his intelligence led him straight to faith, declaring that even without the clear signs of revelation, the Prophet's ﷺ bearing alone would tell you he was a recipient of divine revelation. He accepted Islam immediately and was among the 73 Ansar who took the pledge at Mecca before the Hijrah.

His family was woven into the early Muslim community: his mother Kabsha and sister Amrah accepted Islam; his sister married Bashir ibn Sa'd, making al-Nu'man ibn Bashir his maternal nephew; through his mother he was the maternal brother of Thabit ibn Qays. His lifelong best friend was Abu Darda (ra), whom he brought to Islam by smashing Abu Darda's idol and persistently inviting him.

Of the three poets, Abdullah ibn Rawahah has the most virtues established on the tongue of the Prophet ﷺ, and his poetry was the only one the Prophet ﷺ is known to frequently quote. Unlike Hassan and Ka'b, who answered the Quraysh tribe-for-tribe, his style was to disgrace disbelief directly and elevate faith and the worship of Allah over idolatry. His war poetry, though aggressive, served to rally believers in battle.

The Prophet ﷺ relied on him as a trustee — sending him as an envoy to the tribes of Medina, having him fairly estimate and divide the date palms at Khaybar, and dispatching him to announce the victory of Badr. He was the first to stand up to the chief hypocrite Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul. The Prophet ﷺ placed him in charge of over thirty expeditions, and he was distinguished as a poet who never once slipped into displeasing the Prophet ﷺ.

His worship matched his courage: he famously invited friends to "come, let us believe for an hour," loving the gatherings the angels boast about; he fasted in extreme heat when none but the Prophet ﷺ did the same; and he prayed two raka'at every time he entered and left his house. He wept over verses about touching the Fire and about poets being followed by the misguided — until Allah revealed the exceptions for the believers.

He was martyred at the Battle of Mu'tah, where 3,000 Muslims faced a vast Byzantine army. He was the third commander appointed — after Zayd ibn Harithah and Ja'far ibn Abi Talib — and when his turn came, he steeled his hesitant soul with poetry and advanced to his death. The Prophet ﷺ wept on the pulpit as he announced the loss of all three. Abu Darda (ra) mourned him for decades, seeking refuge in Allah from ever doing a deed that would shame him before Abdullah ibn Rawahah in the next life.

## Key Moments
- Recognized the Prophet ﷺ instantly through his sharp intellect and accepted Islam immediately, becoming one of the 73 Ansar to pledge at Mecca before the Hijrah.
- Smashed Abu Darda's idol and persistently called him to Islam — the Prophet ﷺ foretold Abu Darda would convert.
- Was the first to stand up to the hypocrite Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul, inviting the Prophet ﷺ to address the gathering.
- Served as the Prophet's ﷺ trusted estimator at Khaybar, fairly dividing the date harvest, and announced the victory of Badr to Medina.
- Authored the poem the Prophet ﷺ sang with the companions while digging the trench at Khandaq ("O Allah, were it not for You, we would never have been guided...").
- Recited fierce poetry against the Quraysh during Umrat al-Qada; the Prophet ﷺ told Umar to leave him, "for his words are harsher upon them than arrows."
- His worship-call "Come, let us believe for an hour" was misunderstood by a man, prompting the Prophet's ﷺ praise of his love for the gatherings the angels boast about.
- Martyred at Mu'tah as the third commander after Zayd and Ja'far, advancing despite his soul's hesitation, after which Khalid ibn Walid (ra) took the banner and won victory.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Art and poetry that carry good meaning and do not undermine Islam are permissible — the Prophet ﷺ loved and quoted good poetry, including from before Islam.
- Prophetic methodology: the Prophet ﷺ corrected mistakes while preserving people's dignity and unique personalities, finding a role for everyone's talents.
- Sincere intelligence leads to truth — Abdullah ibn Rawahah, Abdullah ibn Salam, and Hassan ibn Thabit all recognized the Prophet ﷺ at first sight.
- Consistency in small voluntary acts (nawafil) — like praying two raka'at on entering and leaving home — is a powerful means of nearness to Allah.
- Read the Qur'an as if addressed to yourself: rather than assuming warnings are about others, Abdullah ibn Rawahah feared they applied to him, and Allah comforted him.
- Inviting others to faith is a lasting charity (sadaqa jariyah): all of Abu Darda's knowledge and legacy flowed from Abdullah ibn Rawahah's da'wah to him.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "Had there not been clear signs that he has, his very essence would tell you that he is a recipient of divine revelation" — his first poem on seeing the Prophet ﷺ.
- "O Allah, were it not for You, we would never have been guided, nor given charity, nor prayed; so send down calmness upon us and make firm our feet if we meet [the enemy]..." — the Khandaq poem sung by the Prophet ﷺ and the companions.
- "Come, let us believe for an hour" (تعال نؤمن ساعة) — his call to remembrance; the Prophet ﷺ responded, "May Allah have mercy on Ibn Rawahah; he loves the gatherings about which the angels boast."
- "O soul, do I see you resisting Paradise? I swear by Allah you will enter it, willingly or by force" — his words to himself before advancing at Mu'tah.
- Abu Darda (ra): "O Allah, I seek refuge in You from doing a deed that would humiliate me before Abdullah ibn Rawahah."
- "If you spent the entire world in gold, you would not achieve the reward of those who went out early" — the Prophet ﷺ to him when he stayed back to pray Fajr.
