# Episode 94 — Umm Waraqa bint Abdullah (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 94 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SNAIIAOtqBM) · ⏱ 11:18

**Who they were:** A wealthy, noble, and literate woman of Medina who became the foremost female memorizer and teacher of the Qur'an among the companions, known by the title "the Martyred Hafidha" (al-Shahidah) after the Prophet ﷺ foretold her martyrdom.

## Summary
This episode answers a question that recurred while covering the reciters around the Prophet ﷺ: were there any female huffaz, women who memorized and preserved the Qur'an? Dr. Omar prefaces the answer by noting that literacy was uncommon among the Arabs as a whole, and rarer still among women. Those few who could read and write often became scribes for the Prophet ﷺ. Umm Waraqa bint Abdullah (ra) stands out as a woman known to have gathered, memorized, and mastered the recitation of the Qur'an.

Umm Waraqa was a wealthy and noble woman of Medina who embraced Islam before the Prophet's arrival there. Several circumstances allowed her to dedicate herself fully to the Qur'an: she was among the few literate people in Medina, she had inherited great wealth so she had no worldly worries, and she was uninvolved in the pre-Islamic politics and wars of the city. When the Prophet ﷺ arrived, she devoted herself to the Qur'an among the mothers of the believers and other female scholars, becoming distinguished in its recitation early on.

A modern controversy surrounds a narration in which the Prophet ﷺ instructed her to lead the people of her household in prayer. Dr. Omar clarifies that this ambiguous report cannot be used to overturn scholarly consensus by claiming a woman led a mixed congregation of men and women; her "household" could have meant children or servants. What is clear and undisputed is that she was a distinguished reciter who taught the women of Medina the Qur'an.

At the time of the Battle of Badr, Umm Waraqa came to the Prophet ﷺ asking to join the expedition — even just to tend the sick — explicitly seeking martyrdom (shahada) at his side. The Prophet ﷺ told her to remain in her home and assured her that Allah would grant her martyrdom there. From then on she was known among the companions by the nickname "al-Shahidah" (the Martyr), and the Prophet ﷺ would visit her, saying, "Let us go visit the martyr."

She lived to old age — into her sixties or seventies — maintaining her station as the great female authority of the Qur'an, with students coming to recite to her. She was also famed for her fasting and her night prayer; people passing her home at night could hear her recitation. One morning, during his night patrols, Umar (ra) noted that he had not heard her recitation the night before. Investigating, it was discovered that her male and female servants had plotted against her, murdered her, and stolen her wealth. The Prophet's prophecy was fulfilled: she died a martyr in her own home. Umar himself found and executed the two killers.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Women played a vital role in preserving and teaching the Qur'an; Umm Waraqa is the hafidha among the female companions.
- Sincere intention and longing for martyrdom and service are honored by Allah, even when the form of that reward differs from what one imagines.
- The spirit of the Ansar was to be present with the Prophet ﷺ in his most difficult moments, mirroring the man who sought to be struck by an arrow at his side.
- Ambiguous narrations should not be stretched to overturn established scholarly consensus.
- A life of devotion — recitation, night prayer, and teaching — can elevate a person to a station foretold by the Prophet ﷺ himself.
- Wealth and worldly security, when freed of distraction, can be channeled entirely into the service of faith and knowledge.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
