# Episode 89 — Hassan ibn Thabit (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 99 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DA3oy36JgxU) · ⏱ 55:04

**Who they were:** Hassan ibn Thabit (ra) was a distant maternal relative of the Prophet ﷺ from the Khazraj tribe of Medina and the foremost of the three poets of the Prophet ﷺ — the believer often called the greatest poet of all time, who defended the Messenger ﷺ with his tongue from a pulpit in the mosque.

## Summary
Hassan ibn Thabit (ra) came from Banu al-Najjar, the maternal relatives of the Prophet ﷺ, a subtribe of Khazraj. He was a world-renowned poet from a young age whose fame extended beyond Medina to the courts of the Ghassanids in Syria, the Romans, and the Persians, where kings would hire him to humiliate their rivals. In Medina he was the poet of Khazraj against the Aws, whose poet was Qays ibn al-Khatim. His entire family embraced Islam: his father al-Mundhir (in his 80s, a veteran of Badr), his intellectual mother (whom Hassan would proudly call himself "son of" after), his brothers (two of whom were martyrs), and his sisters, several of whom were also companions and poets.

Hassan was 60 years old when the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Medina, making him seven years older than the Prophet ﷺ. He had survived the pre-Islamic Battles of Bu'ath that killed many of his peers, notably because he never picked up a sword in his life. Hired by opponents of Islam to mock and dissect the Prophet ﷺ as he was famous for doing to others, Hassan instead fell instantly in love upon seeing him, declaring he could find no flaw in his appearance or character — composing the famous lines, "My eye has never seen anything more beautiful than you... You were created without any flaw, as if you created yourself as you wished."

A defining trait was that Hassan never fought in a single battle — not from mere reluctance but a genuine, serious fear of the battlefield, which the Prophet ﷺ accepted. This is vividly illustrated during the Battle of the Trench, when he hid with the women and children in the fortress alongside Safiyyah bint Abdul-Muttalib, the Prophet's aunt. When an enemy approached, Hassan was too terrified to act, so the elderly Safiyyah struck the man down with a pole, dragged in his body, and threw it out to scatter the attackers.

His true battle was poetry. When enemies attacked the Prophet ﷺ with lyrics, the Prophet ﷺ chose Hassan to respond, telling him the angel Jibril would support him. Concerned that his lineage-based mockery would strike the Prophet's own Qurayshi relatives, the Prophet ﷺ instructed him to learn the tribes from Abu Bakr, who knew all of Qurayh's lineages. Hassan studied carefully and crafted poetry so precise it dissected every Qurayshi subtribe while perfectly extracting and sparing the Prophet ﷺ — leaving Quraysh to suspect Abu Bakr's hand in it.

Tragically, Hassan's greatest strength became his weakness. With a quick tongue and the lessened filters of old age, he was among those who repeated the slander against Aisha (the incident of al-Ifk), alongside Mistah and Hamnah. Though not the originator (the chief hypocrite Abdullah ibn Ubayy began it), he was punished after revelation cleared Aisha. Safwan ibn al-Mu'attal, also slandered, ambushed and beat Hassan, but the Prophet ﷺ told Hassan to let it go. Hassan composed a moving poem praising Aisha's chastity and purity. Despite the stain that followed him, Aisha herself defended Hassan throughout her life, refusing to let anyone curse him because "he used to defend the Prophet ﷺ." He lived 120 years — 60 in ignorance, 60 in Islam — going blind in old age, and is remembered not as the slanderer but as the poet of the Prophet ﷺ.

## Key Moments
- His entire family — father, mother, two martyred brothers, and several sister-companions — embraced Islam.
- Hired to mock the Prophet ﷺ, Hassan instead fell in love at first sight and composed verses declaring he had no flaw.
- His genuine, debilitating fear of the battlefield meant he never witnessed a single battle.
- During the Battle of the Trench, Safiyyah bint Abdul-Muttalib killed an attacker with a pole while Hassan was too frightened to act.
- The Prophet ﷺ appointed him to answer the enemy's poetry, promising the support of the angel Jibril.
- He studied Qurayshi lineages under Abu Bakr to attack the tribes while skillfully sparing the Prophet ﷺ.
- He fell into the slander of Aisha (al-Ifk), repeating it with his quick tongue, and was punished after revelation.
- Aisha repeatedly defended him for the rest of his life, praising his defense of the Prophet ﷺ.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Your greatest strength can become your greatest weakness — Hassan's quick tongue, his gift, is what tripped him into spreading slander.
- Never assume you are safe from the devil's whispers; even a devoted defender of the Prophet ﷺ fell into a grievous error.
- Beware of being a hasty "social commentator" who repeats rumors before verifying them.
- Redemption is possible even after grave sins; Hassan sought forgiveness and was forgiven by Allah, the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr, and Aisha.
- Aisha's example shows extraordinary character — defending and praising those who had wronged her.
- What is done for Allah endures, while what is done for worldly gain perishes — none of Hassan's paid poetry for tyrants survives, only what he composed for the Prophet ﷺ.
- People can return from serious mistakes; the companions had the spiritual maturity to honor a person's virtue rather than reduce them to their worst moment.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Hassan's description of the Prophet ﷺ: "My eye has never seen anything more beautiful than you, and no woman has ever given birth to one more beautiful than you. You were created without any flaw — as if you were created exactly as you wished."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Hassan: "Respond, and the angel (Jibril) will support you."
- The Prophet's dua: "O Allah, support him with the Holy Spirit (Jibril)."
- From his poem (in Sahih Muslim): "You mocked Muhammad ﷺ and I responded on his behalf, and I expect from Allah a reward... May my father, my mother, and my honor be sacrificed for Muhammad ﷺ... The Messenger of Allah is amongst us, and the Holy Spirit has no match."
- His poem praising Aisha: "A chaste, pious woman who never backbites anyone, who starves herself from the meat of the unsuspecting."
- Aisha's defense of Hassan: "Do not say anything bad about him — he used to defend the Prophet ﷺ. I swear by Allah, I hope Allah will enter him into Jannah for the lines he said defending the Prophet ﷺ against Abu Sufyan."
