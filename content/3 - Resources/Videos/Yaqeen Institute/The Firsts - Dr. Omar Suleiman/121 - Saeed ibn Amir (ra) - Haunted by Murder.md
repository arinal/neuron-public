# Episode 121 — Saeed ibn Amir al-Jumahi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 65 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Rmk8HCP0o2I) · ⏱ 1:00:27

**Who they were:** A noble young man of Quraysh from Banu Abd Shams who witnessed the crucifixion of Khubayb ibn Adi (ra), embraced Islam out of conviction before the conquest of Mecca, and became renowned for his asceticism (zuhd), integrity, and his role as Umar ibn al-Khattab's beloved governor of Hims.

## Summary
Dr. Omar opens by exploring one of the most complex realities of the early community: people who committed horrific acts — including killing companions — before accepting Islam and repenting while the Prophet ﷺ was still alive. He asks the listener to imagine the dual burden: that of the murderer carrying guilt over whether Allah could forgive him, and that of the families of martyrs who had to forgive and pray beside those who killed their loved ones. He frames this through the hadith of the man who killed a hundred people yet was forgiven, and the hadith of Allah laughing at the murderer and his victim meeting together in Paradise.

He illustrates this with Jabbar ibn Salama, an elderly companion with a single narration: he was the man who speared Amir ibn Fuhayra (ra) — the Prophet's hijra guide — at Bi'r Ma'unah. As Amir died he cried "Fuztu wallah!" ("I have succeeded, by Allah!"). Baffled at how a dying man could claim success, Jabbar learned it referred to martyrdom; this conviction led him to Islam and to making his own hijra to Medina.

The main figure is Saeed ibn Amir al-Jumahi (ra), from the proud, wealthy, militarily-leading tribe of Banu Abd Shams. His uncle was Uqbah ibn Abi Mu'ayt, a staunch enemy who once dumped camel entrails on the praying Prophet ﷺ. Saeed's path to Islam began at the public crucifixion of Khubayb ibn Adi (ra) at Tan'im. He watched Khubayb pray two calm rak'ahs, declare he would not wish even a thorn to touch the Prophet ﷺ in his place, and curse the mob with a powerful du'a. Shaken, Saeed publicly disowned Quraysh and their idolatry and made his way to Medina to embrace Islam — notably before Khaybar and the conquest of Mecca, at the community's most vulnerable moment, a mark of his sincerity.

In Medina Saeed distinguished himself through extreme zuhd, abandoning the wealth of his elite origins and living like the people of the Suffa. He fought at Khaybar and beyond, gained the favor of the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr, and especially Umar (ra), with whom he shared a relationship of mutual sincere advice. When Umar became Khalifa, Saeed gave him a profound counsel: fear Allah concerning the people and never fear the people concerning Allah; let deeds match words; judge justly; love for the people what you love for yourself; and never fear the blame of a blamer in the way of Allah.

Umar repeatedly tried to give Saeed wealth and appointed him governor of Hims despite Saeed's pleas to be left to worship. Saeed lived so poorly that his name appeared among the registry of the poor, moving Umar to tears. He distributed Umar's gifts of gold to those needier than himself. When Umar visited Hims, the people raised four "complaints," each of which revealed Saeed's piety: he came out late (baking his own bread for lack of a servant), did not answer them at night (reserved for worship of his Lord), took one day off monthly (washing his only garment), and fainted in gatherings. The last was the heart of the episode: Saeed blacked out whenever he recalled witnessing Khubayb's death without helping him, fearing Allah would never forgive him. Umar embraced him, praising Allah for not disappointing his good opinion of him. Saeed died around 19 AH in Hims during Umar's caliphate.

## Key Moments
- Jabbar ibn Salama's account of spearing Amir ibn Fuhayra (ra), who died crying "Fuztu wallah!" — a phrase that ultimately led Jabbar to Islam.
- Saeed witnessing the crucifixion of Khubayb ibn Adi (ra), including Khubayb's two composed rak'ahs and his refusal to wish the Prophet ﷺ in his place.
- Saeed publicly disowning Quraysh, their idolatry and oppression, then making hijra to Medina before Khaybar and the conquest of Mecca.
- His embrace of zuhd despite coming from the wealthy, elite Banu Abd Shams, becoming one of the poor companions devoted to worship.
- His powerful advice to Umar (ra) upon Umar becoming Khalifa, reducing Umar to tears.
- Umar appointing him governor of Hims against Saeed's protests, and Saeed appearing on the registry of the poor, moving Umar to weep.
- Saeed and his wife giving away Umar's gifts of gold rather than letting "the dunya enter and ruin the akhirah."
- The "four complaints" of the people of Hims, each unveiling his hidden piety — culminating in his fainting from the trauma and guilt of Khubayb's death.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The mercy of Allah and the door of repentance are open even for the gravest sins after shirk — the shedding of blood — as shown by those who killed companions yet became companions themselves.
- Sincere faith is tested by timing and circumstance: Saeed's choice to embrace Islam at the community's weakest moment proved the purity of his conviction.
- True leadership is service, justice, and accountability — Umar's checks on his governors and Saeed's refusal of wealth and comfort model uncorrupted authority.
- Detachment from the dunya (zuhd) frees a person; Saeed feared that material gifts would "ruin his akhirah" more than he feared poverty.
- Consistency between word and deed, and fearing only Allah rather than people, are the foundations of righteous authority.
- A lasting sense of accountability — even fear that one is not forgiven — can become a driving force for continual good deeds and sincerity.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Qur'an: "Allafa bayna qulubikum fa'asbahtum bi ni'matihi ikhwana" — "He brought your hearts together so that by His favour you became brothers."
- Khubayb (ra): "I would not want, by Allah, for Muhammad ﷺ to be pricked by a thorn while I am safe with my family and my health."
- Amir ibn Fuhayra (ra), as he died: "Fuztu wallah" — "I have succeeded, by Allah."
- Saeed's advice to Umar: "I advise you to fear Allah concerning the people, and never fear the people concerning Allah... and take the truth wherever it leads, and do not fear in the way of Allah the blame of a blamer."
- Hadith (Tabarani): The poor Muslims will be lined up like pigeons on the Day of Judgment and told "Stand for the reckoning," and will say "By Allah, we left nothing behind to be held accountable for"; Allah will say "My servants have spoken the truth," and they will enter Paradise before others.
- Saeed on receiving gold: "Something of this world has entered to ruin my akhirah and brought fitnah into my house."
