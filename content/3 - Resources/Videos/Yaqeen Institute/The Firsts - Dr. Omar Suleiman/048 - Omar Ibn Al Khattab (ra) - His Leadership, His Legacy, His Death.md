# Episode 48 — Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 92 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ICcOOBsjVGE) · ⏱ 1:27:51

**Who they were:** Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), the second Caliph of Islam and first to bear the title *ameer al-mu'mineen* (Commander of the Believers), under whose decade-long leadership Islam spread across most of the lands that are majority-Muslim today. This final episode focuses on his model of just leadership and the circumstances of his martyrdom.

## Summary
Umar (ra) was the first leader called *ameer al-mu'mineen*, a title coined under his rule (Abu Bakr having been titled "khalifah of the Prophet ﷺ"). What made him worthy of leadership was that he never felt worthy of it. In his first sermon he refused to stand where the Prophet ﷺ or Abu Bakr had stood, descending to the lowest step, and prayed: "O Allah, I am weak so make me strong, I am harsh so make me gentle, I am stingy so make me generous." His rule was defined by justice — a tyrant feared Umar, but no one feared tyranny from him.

He carried an intense fear of being held accountable before Allah for the ummah, even for a camel that looked overworked or a sheep dying on the shore of the Euphrates. When he asked Salman al-Farsi (ra) whether he was a king or a khalifa, Salman replied that taking even a single dirham wrongly would make him a king — and Umar wept. He vowed that no widow of Iraq would need anyone but him. Despite being among the *muhaddathun* (divinely inspired) and having the Qur'an descend in agreement with his views, he ruled by *shura*, consulting the first Muslims, the people of Badr, the scholars of Qur'an, and especially the youth. He honored Ahlul Bayt — appointing Ali (ra) over Jerusalem, ruling against Ali in a dispute yet kissing his forehead and praying never to live in a land without him — and favored Usama ibn Zayd and al-Husayn with special love.

Umar grew the system of *awqaf* (endowments), donating his Khaybar garden for Allah's sake and inspiring the companions to build a network caring for travelers, the sick, orphans and widows. Living with fourteen patches in his garment, he consulted the companions about what he was entitled to from the treasury, allowing himself only two meals, seasonal clothing, and a mount for Hajj, "for I am only one of the Muslims." During the famine year (*Aam ar-Ramada*), he refused meat, milk and butter until the people could eat, addressing his rumbling stomach: "By Allah, you will not be full until the children of the Muslims are full."

His night patrols became legendary. He secretly cooked and cleaned for years for a blind, disabled old woman without revealing he was the khalifa; he brought his wife Umm Kulthum bint Ali to deliver a bedouin woman's baby while he cooked; and he derived lasting policy from what he witnessed — exempting the elderly People of the Book from jizya after finding an old blind Jewish beggar, equalizing the stipend for nursing children after a mother weaned her child early, and limiting military deployments to four months after overhearing a lonely wife's poetry. The most famous patrol led to a pious girl who refused to dilute milk because "the Lord of Umar sees us"; Umar married her to his son Asim, and from their line came Umar ibn Abdul Aziz, the first *mujaddid* of the ummah.

As his end neared, Hudhayfa (ra) told him he was the closed door holding back the great *fitan*, and that the door would be broken — not opened — meaning he would be killed, after which the trials could never again be shut. Umar made two seemingly impossible duas: for martyrdom in Allah's cause, in the city of the Prophet ﷺ, and not at the hands of anyone who had prostrated to Allah. All three were granted: Abu Lu'lu'a al-Majusi (Fayruz), a Persian craftsman enraged over a commission dispute, stabbed him during Fajr while he recited Surah Yusuf. Umar instructed Abdurrahman ibn Awf to complete the prayer, appointed a six-man council for succession, refused to nominate his own son, and arranged the payment of his debts.

In his final hours Umar's first question on waking was "Did the people pray?" — declaring there is no share of Islam for one who abandons salah — and he completed his own Fajr. He sent his son to Aisha (ra), humbly as "Umar" rather than as Commander of the Believers, to ask permission to be buried beside the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr. She granted it, weeping more for Umar than for her own father because he was the door between the ummah and the fitna. Buried with his head to Abu Bakr's shoulder, Umar was eulogized by Ali (ra): "I would not love to meet Allah with anyone's deeds more than yours."

## Key Moments
- His inaugural khutbah, descending to the lowest step and praying to be made strong, gentle, and generous.
- Salman al-Farsi's measure of a king versus a khalifa, which moved Umar to tears.
- The famine year, when he refused meat and dairy and rebuked his own hungry stomach.
- Years of secretly serving a blind old woman, discovered only when Talha followed him.
- Bringing Umm Kulthum bint Ali to deliver a bedouin's baby while he himself cooked.
- Policy born from patrols: exempting the elderly People of the Book from jizya, equalizing children's stipends, and capping deployments at four months.
- The pious milk-seller's daughter — "the Lord of Umar sees us" — ancestor of Umar ibn Abdul Aziz.
- His martyrdom at Fajr by Abu Lu'lu'a, and his first concern upon waking: "Did the people pray?"

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True leadership flows from humility; Umar's worthiness came precisely from never feeling worthy.
- A leader is a shepherd answerable for the flock — "How can I be a shepherd if I am not touched by what touches my flock?"
- Justice and *shura* must include all groups: elders, scholars, and especially the uplifted youth.
- Caring for the vulnerable — widows, orphans, the poor, the elderly — is the substance, not the slogan, of governance.
- Privacy is sacred: even in patrolling, "do not spy" (*wa la tajassasu*) restrained Umar.
- Salah is the threshold of Islam — "there is no share of Islam for one who abandons the prayer."
- Avoiding nepotism: Umar excluded his own son and his relative Saeed ibn Zayd from succession.
- Standing before Allah humbles every rank: "put my face in the dirt, that Allah may have mercy on me."

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "O Allah, I am weak so make me strong, I am harsh so make me gentle, I am stingy so make me generous." — Umar's first dua as khalifa.
- "For the one who fears the standing before his Lord are two gardens." (Qur'an) — his most-recited verse.
- "Rumble or do not, by Allah you will not be full until the children of the Muslims are full."
- "If Umar does not see us, the Lord of Umar sees us." — the milk-seller's daughter.
- "And do not spy on one another." (*wa la tajassasu*) — Abdurrahman ibn Awf's reminder during a patrol.
- "The command of Allah is a decree determined." (Qur'an) — Umar's words after being stabbed.
- Ali (ra): "I would not love to meet Allah with anyone's deeds more than yours; I always heard the Prophet ﷺ say, 'I, Abu Bakr, and Umar came / entered / left.'"
- "If this face belongs to the Fire, you do not want it in your lap; if it belongs to Jannah, its pillow is softer than your thigh." — Umar to his son Abdullah.
