# Episode 55 — Atika bint Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 55 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GO6iVdobkhE) · ⏱ 17:35

**Who they were:** Atika bint Zayd (ra) was the daughter of Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl — the hanif who followed the way of Ibrahim (as) before the revelation — and the sister of Sa'eed ibn Zayd. She is most remembered as a woman of beauty, eloquence, poetry, devotion, and noble character whose five husbands were all martyred (shuhada).

## Summary
Dr. Omar opens by explaining her name, Atika, which carries the meaning of one who wears much perfume and was a common name among the Arabs. He notes the Prophet ﷺ said at Hunayn, "I am the son of the Atikas of Sulaym and the son of the Fatimahs of Quraysh," referencing the many women named Atika in his lineage.

Atika was the daughter of Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl, the man the Prophet ﷺ said would be raised on the Day of Judgment as an entire nation by himself. Zayd rejected idolatry, alcohol, and female infanticide, rescuing girls destined to be buried and raising them in his own home. He left behind only two children: Sa'eed ibn Zayd, one of the ten promised Paradise, and Atika. She was known for her beauty, eloquence and poetry, her worship and devotion (as narrated by Ibn Kathir), and her complete, well-rounded character.

Her first husband was Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr as-Siddiq (ra) — the son of Abu Bakr who relayed news from Mecca during the Hijrah. Abdullah fell so deeply in love with her that he withdrew from serving alongside the Prophet ﷺ and from the masjid. Abu Bakr, wanting his family to be one of service, pushed him to divorce her, prompting Abdullah to compose love poetry vowing he would never forget her. Abu Bakr relented and let him take her back, asking only that he be active in service. Abdullah was later struck by an arrow at the siege of Ta'if and died of the wound in Madinah as a martyr.

Atika mourned him with an oath that her eyes would never stop weeping and her skin would never touch sweet scents. She then married Zayd ibn al-Khattab (ra), brother of Umar, who was martyred at the Battle of Yamama. When she again vowed never to marry, Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) reminded her not to forbid what Allah made lawful, and proposed to her. Aisha (ra) visited her on her wedding night and gently reworded her old poem to free her of her oath, signaling it was permissible to marry again.

Dr. Omar reflects on the striking lineage: both sons of al-Khattab (Zayd and Umar) married a daughter of Zayd ibn Amr, while al-Khattab's daughter Fatima married Zayd's son Sa'eed — a testament to Allah's wisdom, since al-Khattab had persecuted Zayd to stop the spread of faith, yet his own children carried that faith forward. Umar too was martyred. Atika then married az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra), one of the ten promised Paradise, who was martyred while praying after withdrawing from the Battle of al-Jamal.

With four martyred husbands, people did not see her as an ill omen; rather they said, "Whoever wants martyrdom, let him marry Atika." Her fifth husband was al-Husayn ibn Ali (ra), though she died before his martyrdom. She lived about 72 years and passed away during the caliphate of Mu'awiyah, remembered for her eloquence, poetry, knowledge, worship, and character.

## Key Moments
- Explanation of the name Atika and the Prophet's ﷺ words at Hunayn: "I am the son of the Atikas of Sulaym and the Fatimahs of Quraysh."
- Her father Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl rejected idolatry, alcohol, and female infanticide, and rescued infant girls from being buried.
- Her first husband Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr loved her so excessively that Abu Bakr urged divorce; Abdullah wrote poetry and was allowed to reconcile, later dying a martyr from a Ta'if arrow.
- She married Zayd ibn al-Khattab (ra), martyred at Yamama, then Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), also martyred.
- Aisha (ra) reworded Atika's mourning poem on her wedding night to release her from her oath against remarriage.
- She married az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra), martyred while praying after the Battle of al-Jamal.
- People said: "Whoever desires martyrdom, let him marry Atika," given that all her husbands died as shuhada.
- Her fifth husband was al-Husayn ibn Ali (ra), though she died before his martyrdom at about 72 years of age.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Love between spouses is natural and good, but excessive attachment that pulls one away from service to Allah and the community is unhealthy — balance is needed.
- Dr. Omar gives a strong disclaimer: Abu Bakr's intervention was a unique situation and is not a license for parents or in-laws to force divorce.
- Widows, especially of martyrs, remarrying is honorable in Islam; one should not forbid what Allah has made lawful, as Umar reminded her.
- Allah's wisdom can turn the very efforts to suppress faith into a means of spreading it — al-Khattab's persecution of Zayd ended with his own children carrying Islam forward.
- A righteous upbringing bears lasting fruit: Zayd ibn Amr's desire for faithful children was realized through both Sa'eed and Atika.
- Hardship and repeated loss can be a sign of nearness to Allah, not a bad omen — her home was a place through which great and beloved companions passed.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ at Hunayn: "I am the son of the Atikas of Sulaym and the son of the Fatimahs of Quraysh."
- Umar (ra) to Atika: "Do not make haram for yourself what Allah has made halal."
- Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr's poem (paraphrased): "I will never forget you so long as the sun rises or any star or bird crosses the sky… and I do not see that someone like me would divorce someone like her."
- The people's saying: "Whoever wants to be a martyr, let him marry Atika."
