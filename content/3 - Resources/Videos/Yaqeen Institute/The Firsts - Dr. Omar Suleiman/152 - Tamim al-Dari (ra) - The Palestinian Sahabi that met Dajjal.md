# Episode 152 — Tamim ibn Aws al-Dari (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 34 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9NDtpX-t6xE) · ⏱ 55:59

**Who they were:** Tamim al-Dari (ra) was the famed Palestinian companion — a former Christian scholar, merchant, and truth-seeker from the region of Hebron — best known for the narration in which he and his shipwrecked companions encountered the Dajjal chained on a remote island, and for being the man who first lit oil lamps in the Prophet's ﷺ mosque.

## Summary
Tamim al-Dari (ra) was an Arab Christian living in Palestine (al-Sham) under Roman rule. A literate, scholarly, and well-travelled merchant who led caravans, he encountered Meccan traders and grew curious about the new prophet arising among the descendants of Ibrahim — a prophet who affirmed Jesus, the Torah, and the Gospel. Pursuing the truth, he consulted a learned rabbi and a Christian priest, both of whom confirmed that a prophet was due to come and unite the fractured people of the book. Even a righteous jinn in the valleys near his village told the local people to stop seeking refuge in jinn and turn to Allah, affirming that a messenger had been sent.

Tamim journeyed to Medina in the Year of the Delegations with ten men, including his brother Abu Hind. On the way, a famous incident occurred: lost at sea for a month, he and thirty men landed on an unknown island where a strange hairy beast (al-Jassasa) directed them to a monastery. There they found a giant man in chains who questioned them about the date palms of Bisan, the water of Lake Tiberias (Galilee), and the unlettered Prophet — then revealed himself as the Dajjal, who would one day roam the entire earth in forty days save Mecca and Medina. When Tamim related this to the Prophet ﷺ, the Prophet gathered the people, struck his staff, and declared that it confirmed everything he had told them about the Dajjal.

Upon arrival, Tamim brought gifts — a horse, a cloak, and (out of ignorance) wine, which the Prophet ﷺ gently used as a teaching moment about its prohibition. He also brought five slaves, whom he freed for the sake of Allah at the Prophet's wish. His most beautiful contribution, however, was lighting the mosque with oil lamps from the olive-rich land of Palestine, earning the Prophet's prayer that Allah grant him light on the Day of Judgment. One of his freed slaves was named Siraj ("lamp") for tending them.

Despite catching the Prophet ﷺ only in his final year, Tamim rose swiftly in righteousness and scholarship. He devoted himself intensely to the Qur'an — said to be among the fastest to memorize it and known to complete it in a single night, weeping over a single verse. He became renowned for sincerity, charity, and helpfulness, teaching new converts and the repentant. The Prophet ﷺ honored him by saying that had he an unmarried daughter, he would have given her to him.

During the fitna, Tamim left Medina and returned to Palestine, where Islam had now spread. The Prophet ﷺ had earlier granted him a portion of land in Bethlehem as an endowment (waqf), confirmed by Umar (ra), which his descendants maintain to this day. Tamim died forty years after the Hijra; his neglected grave lies within an Israeli settlement in occupied Palestine, a fitting reminder, Dr. Omar notes, that the land was long occupied and that its people remain.

## Key Moments
- A righteous jinn in the valleys near his home told the people to seek refuge in Allah, not jinn, affirming a messenger had come.
- Lost at sea for a month, Tamim and thirty men landed on an island and met al-Jassasa, leading them to the chained Dajjal.
- The Dajjal questioned them about the date palms of Bisan, Lake Tiberias, and the unlettered Prophet — all confirmed by signs visible today.
- The Prophet ﷺ gathered the companions, struck his staff, and declared the story confirmed his own teachings about the Dajjal.
- Tamim brought a horse, cloak, and wine; the Prophet ﷺ taught that wine was now prohibited rather than rebuking him.
- He freed his five slaves for the sake of Allah and lit the Prophet's mosque with oil lamps from Palestine.
- He rebuked a man who asked how much Qur'an he prayed at night, preferring three rak'ahs of witr in secret over a public boast.
- The Prophet ﷺ allotted him land in Bethlehem as a waqf, which his descendants preserve to this day.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True sincerity (ikhlas) means concealing one's worship — Tamim refused to disclose how much he prayed or recited at night.
- Recognition of the truth is not enough; one must be willing to endure its consequences, as Tamim, Salman al-Farisi, and Abdullah ibn Salam did.
- The narrations of the Dajjal are matters of faith, not scientific expeditions — accept them at face value; no companion went searching for the island.
- Giving da'wah to someone makes you the closest person to them in life and death — Tamim used his scholarship of scripture to call the people of the book.
- Disciplining the self through voluntary oaths (e.g., making up missed worship) can guard against future negligence.
- Beautifying the house of Allah, even with something as simple as lamps, can earn light on the Day of Judgment.
