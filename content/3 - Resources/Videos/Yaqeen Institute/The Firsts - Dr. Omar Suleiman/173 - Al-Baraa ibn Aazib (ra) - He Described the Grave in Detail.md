# Episode 173 — Al-Baraa ibn Aazib (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 13 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aFFoEQahGxI) · ⏱ 1:07:41

**Who they were:** A young companion of the Ansar from the tribe of Banu Harithah in Medina, Al-Baraa ibn Aazib (ra) became one of the most prolific narrators of hadith — transmitting over 300 narrations — and is especially renowned for his detailed descriptions of the Prophet ﷺ and his famous narration about the punishment and reward of the grave.

## Summary
Al-Baraa ibn Aazib (ra) came from a family steeped in faith: his father, mother, and several uncles were all companions. His father narrated only one significant hadith, while one uncle was martyred at the Battle of Yamamah and another, Abu Burdah, was a trusted confidant of the Prophet ﷺ. Al-Baraa attached himself to Musab ibn Umayr after the latter brought Islam to Medina, and by the time the Prophet ﷺ arrived, the young Al-Baraa had already memorized many of the short suras of the Qur'an. As a ten-year-old, he witnessed the unmatched joy of the people of Medina when the Prophet ﷺ finally arrived, recalling he had never seen them happier about anything.

Through his father's interaction with Abu Bakr (ra) — who purchased a saddle and asked the boy to deliver it — Al-Baraa transmitted the long, detailed Bukhari narration of the Hijra: the journey by night, finding shade beneath a rock, the milk from the shepherd, and the pursuit by Suraqah ibn Malik whose horse sank into the ground. He also described his first close encounters with the companions, including seeing Abu Bakr tenderly kiss his fevered daughter Aisha on the cheek upon reaching Medina.

Al-Baraa is among the foremost narrators of the *shama'il* — the physical description and conduct of the Prophet ﷺ. He testified that the Prophet's face was brighter than the full moon, that he was of modest-to-tall height with hair reaching his shoulders, and that he never saw anyone more handsome than the Prophet ﷺ in a red Yemeni garment. He also recorded how the Prophet ﷺ structured Medinan society through seven commands and several prohibitions.

Though turned away from Badr alongside Ibn Umar (ra) for being too young, Al-Baraa was fascinated by the battles and recorded precise details about Badr's numbers and events. He eventually fought in fifteen battles. He vividly narrated the Battle of the Trench (Khandaq), describing the Prophet ﷺ covered in dirt, digging like ten men, and chanting the verses of Abdullah ibn Rawahah to boost morale. He also narrated the courage of the Prophet ﷺ at Hunayn, when many fled but the Prophet ﷺ dismounted and marched forward declaring, "I am the Prophet in truth, I am the descendant of Abdul-Muttalib."

Al-Baraa narrated numerous hadith on the virtues of Medina and the Ansar, supplications for travel and sleep, the manners of prayer, and the Prophet's love for Hasan and Husayn. His most famous narration is the lengthy hadith describing in detail what happens in the grave — the questioning, the reward, and the punishment. He lived into his 70s–80s, fought in later conquests including Tustar against the Persian Empire, served as a governor, taught the sunnah to his children, and passed away in Iraq, having clung to righteousness long after the Prophet ﷺ.

## Key Moments
- As a 10-year-old, eagerly awaiting the Prophet's arrival in Medina, asking each arriving companion (Musab, Ibn Umm Maktum, Ammar, Bilal, Sa'd) when the Prophet ﷺ was coming.
- Transmitting the detailed Hijra narration from Abu Bakr (ra), prompted by his father's request before delivering the purchased saddle.
- Witnessing Abu Bakr (ra) kissing his ill daughter Aisha (ra) on the cheek — his first close encounter with the family of the companions.
- Asking the Prophet ﷺ about the meaning of the handshake, learning that two Muslims who shake hands and praise Allah are forgiven before parting.
- Describing the Prophet ﷺ at the Trench, covered head to toe in mud, digging tirelessly and chanting poetry to raise the companions' morale.
- Recording the Prophet's bravery at Hunayn, remaining at his side when others fled, and the Prophet ﷺ dismounting to advance on foot.
- Witnessing the miracle at Hudaybiyyah when the Prophet ﷺ spat into the drying well and the water surged forth for all the people and animals.
- Crying years later over missing Fajr prayer during the Battle of Tustar, when victory came between dawn and sunrise.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Visiting the sick — even non-relatives — is among the most neglected communal sunnahs that builds a cohesive society.
- The Prophet ﷺ led from the front, getting his hands dirty alongside his companions and boosting morale, modeling true leadership.
- Precision in transmitting and preserving sacred knowledge matters — Al-Baraa was trusted because he narrated with exactness.
- Supplications carry specific blessings tied to their precise wording, as shown when the Prophet ﷺ corrected Al-Baraa's recitation of the bedtime dua.
- Misusing or twisting Qur'anic verses to justify shortcomings (as with those who avoided spending in Allah's path) is a long-standing danger to guard against.
- The grief of the companions over a single missed prayer in the midst of battle should shame our casual delay of our own prayers.
- Righteousness is a lifelong commitment — even great companions warned that the struggle did not end after the Prophet ﷺ passed.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "No two Muslims meet one another and shake hands and then praise Allah and seek forgiveness from Allah, except that they are forgiven before they part ways."
- Al-Baraa describing the Prophet ﷺ: "His face was as bright as the full moon... I have never seen anything more beautiful than the Prophet ﷺ that day."
- The Prophet ﷺ commanded seven things, including visiting the sick, following funerals, responding to the one who sneezes, supporting the weak, aiding the oppressed, spreading salam, and helping fulfill oaths.
- At Hunayn the Prophet ﷺ declared: "I am the Prophet in truth, I am the descendant of Abdul-Muttalib."
- The verses chanted at the Trench: "O Allah, had it not been for You, we would not have been guided, nor given charity, nor prayed. So send tranquility upon us and make our feet firm when we meet our enemies."
- The bedtime supplication: "O Allah, I submit myself to You, turn my face to You, entrust my affairs to You... I believe in Your Book which You revealed and in Your Prophet whom You sent" — with the Prophet ﷺ correcting "messenger" to "Prophet."
