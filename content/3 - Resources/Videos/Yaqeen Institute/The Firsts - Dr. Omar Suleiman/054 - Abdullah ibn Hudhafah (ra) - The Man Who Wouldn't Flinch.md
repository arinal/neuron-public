# Episode 54 — Abdullah ibn Hudhafah As-Sahmi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 54 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QaqVGgn1e-0) · ⏱ 15:52

**Who they were:** An early companion of the Prophet ﷺ, brother of Khunais (ra), brought to Islam as a young man and famed for standing firm before the two greatest tyrants of his day — Kisra of Persia and Heraclius of the Byzantines — embodying the Prophet's words that the greatest jihad is "a word of truth in the face of a tyrant."

## Summary
Abdullah ibn Hudhafah As-Sahmi (ra) embraced Islam early through his older brother Khunais (ra) and made the migration (hijrah) with him. Too young to fight at Badr or Uhud, he did not get the chance to display his devotion until later in life. The two incidents he is most famous for both involve speaking and standing for truth in the face of tyrants, fulfilling the Prophet ﷺ's teaching that the greatest striving is "a word of truth in the face of a tyrant."

The first incident took place during the Prophet ﷺ's lifetime, when Abdullah was chosen to carry the letter calling Kisra, emperor of Persia, to Islam. He insisted on delivering the dangerous letter directly into Kisra's hand, as commanded. Kisra, enraged by the letter inviting him to accept Islam, tore it up and threatened both the messenger and the Prophet ﷺ. When Abdullah returned and reported this, the Prophet ﷺ supplicated, "May Allah tear up his kingdom the way he tore up that letter" — and the Persian empire indeed collapsed into internal assassination and disarray.

The more famous incident occurred years later, during the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), in the campaigns against the Byzantines. Abdullah was captured, and Heraclius asked for the most noble and senior captive to be brought to him. Heraclius tried to break him: he offered him half his kingdom to become Christian; Abdullah refused, saying he would not abandon Islam even for the entire Roman and Arab empires combined.

Heraclius then sent the most beautiful woman to seduce him in his cell, but Abdullah turned away from her in every direction, completely unmoved — she declared he was "like a statue made of stone." He was then starved and deprived of water for three days, after which pork and wine were brought to him; though it would have been permissible under necessity, he refused so as not to give the emperor the satisfaction of mocking a companion of the Messenger ﷺ.

Heraclius had him strung up for crucifixion while warriors threw spears and arrows that deliberately missed him — still he did not flinch. Finally, Heraclius had a Muslim prisoner thrown into boiling oil before Abdullah's eyes, his flesh burning instantly. When Abdullah was lifted to be thrown in next, he wept — but explained he cried only because he wished he had a hundred souls to sacrifice for Allah, not out of fear. Defeated, Heraclius asked only that Abdullah kiss his forehead in exchange for freedom; Abdullah negotiated the release of all 300 Muslim captives before doing so. Back in Medina, Umar (ra) kissed Abdullah's forehead and ordered all the Muslims to do likewise. Abdullah ibn Hudhafah (ra) continued striving in Allah's path until he died in Egypt in the year 33 AH, during the caliphate of Uthman (ra).

## Key Moments
- Embraced Islam as a young man through his brother Khunais (ra) and migrated with him.
- Chosen by the Prophet ﷺ to deliver the letter inviting Kisra of Persia to Islam, hand-to-hand.
- Kisra tore up the letter; the Prophet ﷺ prayed, "May Allah tear up his kingdom," and Persia collapsed.
- Captured by the Byzantines and brought before Heraclius as the noblest of the prisoners.
- Refused the offer of half (and even the whole) Roman empire to renounce Islam.
- Resisted seduction, three days of starvation, and the temptation of pork and wine.
- Stood unflinching before mock execution by spears and arrows, and the threat of boiling oil.
- Negotiated the release of all 300 Muslim captives in exchange for kissing Heraclius's forehead; honored by Umar (ra) upon his return.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The greatest jihad is to speak a word of truth in the face of a tyrant, as the Prophet ﷺ taught.
- True faith (iman) and certainty (yaqeen) cultivated in the company of the Prophet ﷺ made Abdullah unbreakable.
- Even when something is permissible out of necessity, a believer may forgo it to protect the honor of the religion.
- Humility and self-honor are for the sake of Allah alone — Abdullah set aside his ego to free fellow Muslims.
- Endurance under trial reflects a heart fixed on martyrdom and the reward of Allah, not fear of death.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "The greatest jihad is a word of truth in the face of a tyrant."
- Abdullah (ra) to Heraclius: "If you gave me your whole empire and everything the Arabs possess, I would not renounce my religion of Islam even for the blink of an eye."
- Abdullah (ra), when weeping before the boiling oil: "I wished I had a hundred souls, each one to be sacrificed for the sake of Allah."
- The Prophet ﷺ upon Kisra tearing the letter: "May Allah tear up his kingdom the way he tore up that letter."
