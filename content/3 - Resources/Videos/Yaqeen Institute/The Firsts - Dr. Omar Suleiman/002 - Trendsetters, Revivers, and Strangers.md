# Episode 2 — Trendsetters, Revivers, and Strangers

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 2 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vJH3-g-h3ak) · ⏱ 41:40

**Who they were:** This is a framing lecture rather than a biography of a single companion. Dr. Omar Suleiman lays out the conceptual foundation for the entire series by defining five Qur'anic and prophetic categories of "firsts" that believers can aspire to embody.

## Summary
Dr. Omar opens the series by explaining its purpose: studying history not merely to admire great figures, but to be inspired to make history ourselves. Rather than proceeding chronologically, the series will examine companions and groups specifically through the lens of being "firsts" — the first muadhin (Bilal ra), the first messenger (Nuh ﷺ), the first muhajirun, and the categories of firsts found in the Qur'an and Sunnah that we can still aspire to join.

The first category is **as-sabiqun** (the forerunners), described in the Qur'an as those who rush and compete in good. They are people of *uluww al-himmah* (high ambition), self-motivated and quick to respond. Allah places this small group neither among the people of the right nor the left, but directly under His shade. Scholars explain the doubling of "as-sabiqun, as-sabiqun" in two ways: those who rush to good and those who rush away from evil, and those who, because they rushed to Allah in this life, will be rushed to Him in the Hereafter.

The second category is the **trendsetters**, illustrated by the hadith of Jabir ibn Abdullah (ra): when impoverished Bedouins arrived in Medina, the Prophet ﷺ urged charity but saw the companions hesitate, and his face showed disappointment until one of the Ansar brought a purse of silver, prompting others to follow until his sadness turned to joy. The Prophet ﷺ taught that whoever introduces a good practice already established in Islam earns its reward and the reward of all who follow it, undiminished.

The third category is the **strangers (al-ghuraba)** — those who hold to unpopular truths and rectify what people have corrupted, even when this alienates them. Islam began strange and will return strange, with glad tidings for the strangers. The fourth category is the **revivers (al-mujaddidun)**, sent at the turn of every century to revive what is lost of the religion, exemplified by Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz who revived justice. The fifth is the **distinguished (al-muhsinun)**, who excel in quality rather than quantity of deeds, becoming strangers even among the pious.

## Key Moments
- Dr. Omar frames the series as a roughly three-year journey studying companions only in the context of their being "firsts."
- Explanation of the three groups on the Day of Judgment: people of the right, people of the left, and the small group of forerunners directly under Allah's shade.
- The hadith of Jabir (ra): the Bedouins in need, the companions' hesitation, and the Ansari man whose silver purse set off a wave of giving.
- The Prophet's ﷺ teaching that one who starts a good practice (*man sanna fi al-Islam sunnatan hasanatan*) gains its reward plus that of all who follow.
- The hadith "Be in this world as though you were a stranger or a wayfarer," narrated by Abdullah ibn Umar (ra).
- Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz cited as the only universally agreed-upon mujaddid, and his two excellent rak'ahs of night prayer that outweighed others' lengthy worship.
- Ibn al-Qayyim's chain: Muslims become strangers among humanity, the pious become strangers among Muslims, and the muhsinun become strangers even among the pious.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Reading the ahadith about distinguished people should awaken aspiration to join them, not just admiration of them.
- Hesitation (*taraddud*) in responding to good is condemned; the lag time before we act on goodness matters spiritually.
- Setting a good example, guiding others to good, or calling people to good all earn ongoing reward without diminishing the doer's own.
- Holding to unpopular truth and abstaining from popularized sins — even among Muslims — is the mark of the strangers.
- Revival (*tajdeed*) operates on both the macro scale and the micro scale; reviving even a small forgotten sunnah in one's own context is a form of it.
- Excellence (*ihsan*) is about quality, not quantity — "the best in deeds," not "the most" — and one cannot be a true muhsin without first attaining taqwa across all areas, including the tongue.

- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever introduces a good practice in Islam will have its reward and the reward of all who act upon it after him, without their rewards being diminished in any way."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever guides others to an act of goodness will have a reward similar to that of its doer."

- The Prophet ﷺ: "Allah sends to this ummah at the turn of every century one who revives its religion for it."
- "لِيَبْلُوَكُمْ أَيُّكُمْ أَحْسَنُ عَمَلًا" — "That He may test which of you is best in deed" (not most in deed).
