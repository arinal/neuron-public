# Episode 102 — Aisha bint Abi Bakr (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 84 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nXXJWS3inuk) · ⏱ 1:08:26

**Who they were:** Aisha bint Abi Bakr (ra), the beloved wife of the Prophet ﷺ and daughter of his closest companion Abu Bakr (ra), known as the most beloved person to the Prophet ﷺ and a foremost scholar who narrated over two thousand hadith illuminating his private and domestic life.

## Summary
This episode — the second of four in the series on Aisha (ra) — focuses on the love story between the Prophet ﷺ and Aisha (ra), and the dynamics of their marriage. Dr. Omar opens with the famous narration of Amr ibn al-As, who, having embraced Islam late after years of opposition, was treated with such warmth by the Prophet ﷺ that he believed he might be the most beloved person to him. When he asked outright, the Prophet ﷺ answered without hesitation: "Aisha." When pressed about men, he said "her father" (using "Abuha" to keep the connection to Aisha), then Umar, then others. The Prophet ﷺ had no shame in declaring his love for her publicly.

Their love, well known throughout the community, created tensions among the wives. The Ansar would save their gifts for the nights the Prophet ﷺ spent with Aisha, prompting the other wives to send delegations to complain. Umm Salama, Fatima, and finally Zaynab each approached the Prophet ﷺ. To Umm Salama he replied that revelation only came to him while he was with Aisha among his wives; to Fatima he simply asked, "Don't you love what I love?"; and when Zaynab argued harshly and belittled Aisha, the Prophet ﷺ permitted Aisha to respond, afterward remarking that she was truly the daughter of Abu Bakr in her eloquence. Throughout, the Prophet ﷺ was equitable yet honest, praying, "O Allah, this is my division in what I control; do not blame me for what You control" — referring to the inclination of his heart.

Dr. Omar then surveys the rich body of narrations describing the Prophet's tenderness: he entered home smiling and joking, sewed his own clothes and mended his shoes, held Aisha's hand and faced her fully when speaking, drank from the same spot on the cup as her, and ate from the same place on a bone. Aisha used these narrations partly to distinguish Islam's allowances from the strict Jewish customs in Medina — affirming, for example, that the Prophet ﷺ would kiss his wives without breaking ablution or his fast. Even during i'tikaf in the last ten nights of Ramadan, he would lean his head into her room so she could comb and oil his hair.

The Prophet ﷺ continually affirmed his love. In the long hadith of Umm Zar'a, he listened attentively to Aisha's account of eleven women describing their husbands, then told her, "I am to you like Abu Zar'a was to Umm Zar'a, except I will never divorce you." When she asked about his love, he likened it to a knotted rope that remained tight. He let her rest her cheek on his as they watched the Abyssinians' spear display in the masjid, allowing her to gauge her standing with him. He could even tell her moods — knowing she swore "by the Lord of Muhammad" when content and "by the Lord of Ibrahim" when upset.

The episode also explores Aisha's jealousy as an expression of her love — most famously when she broke a dish sent by another wife, and the Prophet ﷺ calmly told the companions, "Your mother got jealous." Yet her one red line was Khadija (ra); when Aisha disparaged her, the Prophet ﷺ grew visibly upset and defended Khadija's memory. The lecture closes with intimate nighttime narrations: Aisha reaching out to find him in prayer or sujud, following him secretly to the Baqi cemetery where he prayed for the deceased, and the most amazing thing she ever witnessed — the night he wept until the ground was soaked, reciting the verse of Surah Al-Imran about the signs of creation, asking, "Should I not be a grateful servant?"

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Treating people with sincere goodness — as the Prophet ﷺ did even with former enemies — can win hearts profoundly.
- A righteous home is built not on material wealth but on love, character, and the presence of revelation.
- The Prophet ﷺ modeled marital tenderness: attentiveness, joking, eye contact, holding hands, sharing food and drink.
- Justice in what one controls combined with honesty about the heart's inclinations — "do not blame me for what You control."
- Jealousy can stem from deep love, but it must not cross into disrespecting others' rightful honor.
- Aisha's response — "I love your closeness, but I love even more what makes you happy" — shows love that prioritizes the beloved's wellbeing and worship.
- Even any expression resembling a curse from the Prophet ﷺ against a believer becomes mercy and purification for them.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "I am to you like Abu Zar'a was to Umm Zar'a, except I will never divorce you." — the Prophet ﷺ to Aisha.
- "Your mother got jealous." — the Prophet ﷺ after Aisha broke the dish.
- "My eyes sleep but my heart never sleeps." — the Prophet ﷺ.
- "O Allah, I am human; sometimes I get upset as people get upset; so if I ever say something against a believer, make it a source of mercy and purification for them." — the Prophet's ﷺ supplication.
- "O Allah, forgive what proceeds of her sins and what is yet to come, that which is private and that which is public... I swear this is the du'a I make for my ummah in every prayer." — the Prophet ﷺ to Aisha.
- "Should I not be a grateful servant?" — the Prophet ﷺ, when asked why he wept in prayer after being forgiven.
- Qur'an 3:190–191: "Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth and the alternation of the night and the day are signs for those of understanding..."
