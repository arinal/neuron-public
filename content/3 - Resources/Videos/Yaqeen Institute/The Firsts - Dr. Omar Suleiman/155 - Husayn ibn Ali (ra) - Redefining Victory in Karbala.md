# Episode 155 — Husayn ibn Ali (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 31 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KdOrOsp41H4) · ⏱ 1:27:31

**Who they were:** Husayn ibn Ali (ra) was the younger grandson of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, son of Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra) and Fatima (ra), and — together with his brother Hasan — one of the "two masters of the youth of Paradise." He is most remembered for his martyrdom at Karbala in his stand against the tyranny of Yazid.

## Summary
Dr. Omar frames this biography not as a journey into the distant past but as a lens on the present: a study of personal conviction in the face of corruption, and of how Allah places victory even in apparent defeat. He stresses the duty to love the family of the Prophet ﷺ for the Prophet's sake, recalling how the Prophet ﷺ would light up at the sight of his grandsons, calling Hasan and Husayn his "two sweet basil" of this world and the masters of the youth of Paradise.

Husayn (ra) was born in the fourth year after Hijra, less than a year after Hasan. The Prophet ﷺ named him Husayn, a unique form of the name, and showed him tireless affection — playing, wrestling, smelling and kissing his grandsons. Yet alongside this joy came grief: the angels foretold the Prophet ﷺ that a group of his own community would one day kill Husayn, and he was even shown the red soil of the land where the martyrdom would occur. The Prophet ﷺ carried the weight of this foreknowledge while still remaining optimistic and never paralyzed by what was to come.

After the Prophet's death, Hasan and Husayn grew into honored young men, deeply loved by the companions. They stood beside their father Ali during the trials of his caliphate and fought in his battles. When Hasan abdicated and reconciled the ummah under Mu'awiyah, Husayn — though not initially happy — trusted his brother's wisdom, put his head down, and even fought in the campaigns toward Constantinople. He upheld justice firmly, once invoking the Hilf al-Fudul pact against a governor of Medina in a property dispute.

When Mu'awiyah broke precedent by appointing his son Yazid as successor, Husayn, along with Abdullah ibn Zubayr and others, refused the pledge of allegiance. Both fled Medina to Mecca. From Iraq, Husayn began receiving overwhelming letters — two bags full — from the people of Kufa pledging support and begging him to come lead them. He sent his cousin Muslim ibn Aqil to assess the situation; 12,000 came out enthusiastically to receive Muslim.

Despite the desperate pleas of senior companions — Abdullah ibn Abbas, Abdullah ibn Umar, and others who feared he would meet his father's fate — Husayn set out for Iraq, convinced the support was genuine. But the new governor Ubaydullah ibn Ziyad crushed the Kufan revolt; Muslim ibn Aqil was betrayed, abandoned, and executed. By the time Husayn neared Iraq with fewer than 100 followers (only 73 fighting men, mostly his own family), the political ground had collapsed: "their hearts are with you but their swords are with Yazid." At Karbala, surrounded by 5,000 soldiers under Umar ibn Sa'd, deprived of water, Husayn refused to surrender to the tyrant on humiliating terms. After offering his companions freedom to leave — which they refused — he spent his last night in prayer. In the ensuing massacre his sons and relatives fell around him; Husayn himself was martyred with 34 wounds and beheaded, his head paraded and desecrated. Dr. Omar closes on the redefinition of victory: though his body was killed, his soul was reunited with the Prophet ﷺ, and his courage became an eternal legacy against tyranny.

## Key Moments
- The Prophet ﷺ named the newborn Husayn and called him and Hasan his "two sweet basil," smelling and kissing them, and declaring them masters of the youth of Paradise.
- The angel foretold the Prophet ﷺ that his community would kill Husayn, and brought him the red soil of Karbala — leaving the Prophet ﷺ tossing in grief at night.
- Husayn refused to pledge allegiance to Yazid and left Medina for Mecca rather than legitimize an unjust succession.
- The people of Kufa sent some two bags full of letters pledging support; Husayn sent Muslim ibn Aqil to verify, and 12,000 came out to receive him.
- Ibn Abbas, Ibn Umar, and the mother of the believers pleaded with Husayn not to go to Iraq, fearing he would meet his father's fate.
- The Kufan support evaporated under intimidation; Muslim ibn Aqil was betrayed, captured, and executed on the day of Arafah.
- Husayn refused to surrender on Ibn Ziyad's degrading terms, offering instead to return, meet Yazid directly, or go to a remote land.
- At Karbala, after his sons and family fell, Husayn was martyred with 34 wounds and beheaded; his head was later desecrated and carried to Yazid.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Those who crumble under the fitna of tribulation often failed first to conquer the fitna of comfort and desire — illustrated by the contrast between Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (who shunned power) and his son Umar (who led the army against Husayn for the sake of position).
- The love of power and rank is a spiritual poison the Prophet ﷺ warned against; pursuing position at all costs leads to disastrous ends.
- Tribalism — sometimes wearing new flags — lies at the root of the ummah's destruction; the brotherhood of Islam is meant to transcend it.
- When corruption takes over, "the courageous shine and the cowards are exposed," and even hypocrites are unmasked.
- Victory can be redefined: even in martyrdom there is triumph — they may kill the body but cannot kill the soul, while tyrants lose their own salvation.
- Do not numb yourself to great injustices (the bloodshed of the ummah) while consuming yourself with minor disputes — a lesson drawn for the present day.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "How can I not love them when they are my two sweet basil in this world?"
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever loves me, let him love Husayn."
- The Prophet ﷺ on Husayn's crying: "Don't you know that his crying hurts me?"
- Hasan to Husayn: "I wish I had some of that strength of your heart," and Husayn wishing for Hasan's eloquence.
- Husayn (ra): "I'd rather be martyred and my blood shed outside of Mecca than open up the Haram to become a place of bloodshed."
- A Kufan to Husayn: "Their hearts are with you, but their swords are with Yazid."
- Husayn's du'a at Karbala: "O Allah, You are my strength in every distress and my hope in every trial."
- A companion seeing the desecration of Husayn's head: "I saw the Prophet ﷺ kiss those same lips."
- Husayn's rebuke to a man asking about the impurity of a mosquito's blood: "You ask me about the blood of a mosquito while you allowed the blood of the son of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ to be shed?"
