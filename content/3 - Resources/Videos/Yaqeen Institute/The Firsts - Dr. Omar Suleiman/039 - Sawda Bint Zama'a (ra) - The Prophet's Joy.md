# Episode 39 — Sawda bint Zam'a (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 39 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KzlHreolLu8) · ⏱ 43:52

**Who they were:** Sawda bint Zam'a (ra) was the second wife of the Prophet ﷺ and the first woman he married after Khadija (ra). Known for her warmth, humor, and boundless generosity, she brought joy to the Prophet ﷺ during the most sorrowful period of his life.

## Summary
Sawda bint Zam'a (ra) is one of the most overlooked Mothers of the Believers, despite her remarkable closeness to the Prophet ﷺ. She came from powerful clans in both Mecca and Medina — her father Zam'a ibn Qais was related to Khadija (ra), and her mother's lineage connected her to the Prophet ﷺ through Abdul Muttalib. These tribal ties shielded her from the worst physical persecution after she embraced Islam. About five years older than the Prophet ﷺ, she was described as tall and of large physical presence.

Sawda and her husband Sakran ibn Amr — brother of the famous Quraysh negotiator Suhail ibn Amr — were among the earliest Muslims who accepted Islam at Dar al-Arqam and were disowned by their family. They migrated to Abyssinia, where they had a son, Abd al-Rahman, who would later become a great warrior and die a martyr at the Battle of Jalula during Umar's caliphate. In Abyssinia, Sawda saw two dreams — the Prophet ﷺ kneeling beside her, and the moon descending into her home — which her husband Sakran interpreted as signs that he would soon die and the Prophet ﷺ would marry her. Sakran did pass away, leaving Sawda a widow in her fifties, a convert in a strange land with a blind, elderly father and young son.

After returning to Mecca as something of an outcast, Sawda was matched with the Prophet ﷺ by the community's matchmaker, Khawla bint Hakim (ra), during the Year of Grief (Aam al-Huzn), after the deaths of Khadija (ra) and Abu Talib and the rejection at Ta'if. Khawla proposed both Aisha (ra) and Sawda — Sawda for her loving, humorous, non-confrontational nature that could bring joy and care into the Prophet's grieving household. Her brother Abd ibn Zam'a (then still a disbeliever) protested the marriage at the Ka'bah, an act he deeply regretted after becoming Muslim. Her mahr was 400 dirhams.

For three years, Sawda was the Prophet's only wife — present during al-Isra wal-Mi'raj, accompanying the family in the Hijrah to Medina, and the first to have her home among the hujurat. When Aisha (ra) came of age, Sawda welcomed her as a younger sister, and Aisha came to regard Sawda as a role model she most wished to emulate.

Sawda was famed for two qualities: bringing joy and laughter to the Prophet ﷺ, and her overflowing charity. Aisha (ra) said she was the most generous of people, giving away in charity anything that came to her without even looking at it. In her later years, no longer interested in intimacy, she gave her allotted night to Aisha — not seeking divorce, but desiring above all to be resurrected as a wife of the Prophet ﷺ on the Day of Judgment.

She lived an intensely private life after the Prophet's death, never making Hajj again, never involving herself in political disputes, and narrating only five hadith. So withdrawn was she that even the year of her death is disputed (somewhere between 644 and 674 CE). She asked for her room to be merged with Aisha's after her death — generous to the very end.

## Key Moments
- Embraced Islam early at Dar al-Arqam with her husband Sakran ibn Amr; together they migrated to Abyssinia where their son Abd al-Rahman was born.
- In Abyssinia she saw two prophetic dreams which her husband interpreted as foretelling his death and her future marriage to the Prophet ﷺ.
- Matched with the Prophet ﷺ by Khawla bint Hakim during the Year of Grief to bring joy and care into his household.
- Her brother Abd ibn Zam'a publicly protested the marriage at the Ka'bah — a deed he later regretted upon accepting Islam.
- Was the Prophet's sole wife for three years, present during al-Isra wal-Mi'raj and the Hijrah to Medina.
- Mistakenly encouraged the captive Suhail ibn Amr (her former brother-in-law) to have fought rather than surrender at Badr; the Prophet ﷺ gently corrected her and smiled to reassure her.
- The verse of hijab (in Surat al-Ahzab) was revealed in connection with an incident involving Sawda being recognized by Umar (ra).
- In old age, she gave her night to Aisha (ra) so she could remain a wife of the Prophet ﷺ on the Day of Judgment.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A cheerful, joy-bringing spirit is itself a great gift; Sawda's humor lifted the Prophet ﷺ during his darkest period and reminds us of the lighter, human side of the Sahaba.
- True generosity gives freely without attachment — Sawda gave away whatever came to her, even without looking at it.
- Living a life of obscurity and devotion can be as noble as a public one; Sawda's quiet, private worship was beloved to Allah.
- Selflessness in relationships: Sawda nurtured a loving bond with Aisha (ra) rather than rivalry, and surrendered her own night to please the Prophet ﷺ.
- Sincere longing for the Hereafter — Sawda valued her status as the Prophet's wife in Jannah above worldly comfort.
- The kind treatment of the prisoners at Badr left such an impression that many later embraced Islam.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Sawda (ra), making the Prophet ﷺ laugh: "O Messenger of Allah, I prayed behind you last night and you took so long in your ruku' that I had to hold my nose for fear that blood would drip from it" — and the Prophet ﷺ laughed until his back teeth were visible.
- Sawda (ra): "But I wish to be resurrected on the Day of Judgment as one of your wives" — when offering her night to Aisha (ra).
- Aisha (ra): "I never saw a woman I loved more to be in her place and follow her example than Sawda."
- Abd ibn Zam'a, after becoming Muslim: "What a silly person I was, putting dirt on my head [in protest] that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ married my sister Sawda."
- Ibn Abbas (ra), upon hearing of her death, fell into prostration, saying no sign (ayah) is greater than the passing of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ.
