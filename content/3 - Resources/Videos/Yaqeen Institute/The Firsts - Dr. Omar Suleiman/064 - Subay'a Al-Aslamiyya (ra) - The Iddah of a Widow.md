# Episode 64 — Subay'a bint al-Harith al-Aslamiyya (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 64 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2mxqQGFKfM) · ⏱ 15:02

**Who they were:** An early female companion and one of the recognized jurists (fuqaha) among the Sahaba, whose case clarified the ruling on the waiting period (iddah) of a pregnant widow.

## Summary
Subay'a bint al-Harith al-Aslamiyya (ra) was among the early scholars of the companions. The fuqaha of Medina and Kufa studied with her and narrated from her, most prominently Abdullah ibn Umar (ra). Little is known of her early history beyond that she was one of the first to embrace Islam alongside her husband, Sa'd ibn Khawla (ra).

Sa'd ibn Khawla (ra) was, according to some biographers, a Persian man from Yemen and an ally (halif) of the tribe of Amr ibn Lu'ayy, counted among the weak and downtrodden (du'afa) of Mecca. Some scholars include him among those whom Allah commanded the Prophet ﷺ not to turn away — "those who call upon their Lord morning and evening, seeking His face" — when the elites pressured the Prophet ﷺ to expel the vulnerable believers. Persecuted in Mecca, the couple made hijrah to Abyssinia and were among the very first to migrate to Medina, staying with the Ansari companion Kulthum ibn al-Hadim (ra).

Sa'd (ra) was only 25 at the Battle of Badr, indicating they were a young couple. He fought in all the battles alongside the Prophet ﷺ. His death is narrated within the famous hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas's illness in Bukhari: the Prophet ﷺ prayed that his companions' hijrah be completed and that they not be turned back on their heels — meaning he disliked that those who migrated would die in Mecca. Yet the narration laments, "But the poor Sa'd ibn Khawla," for he died in Mecca during Hajjatul Wada', the Farewell Pilgrimage — a noble death nonetheless.

When Sa'd (ra) died, Subay'a (ra) was pregnant and gave birth just two weeks later. Her case became the deciding precedent on the iddah of a pregnant widow: does she wait the full four months and ten days, or does her iddah end at the birth of her child? The companions returned to her case to establish that the iddah ends with the delivery — the shorter of the two terms.

After giving birth, Subay'a (ra) prepared herself for remarriage while still in postnatal bleeding (nifas). Two men proposed: a younger man whom she intended to accept, and an older man (kahal, above 33) named Abu Sanabil (ra). Abu Sanabil sought to delay her, suggesting her iddah was not yet complete. She went to the Prophet ﷺ, who confirmed her iddah had ended with the birth. She ultimately married Abu Sanabil (ra).

## Key Moments
- Subay'a (ra) and her husband Sa'd ibn Khawla (ra) were among the first couples to embrace Islam and made both the hijrah to Abyssinia and to Medina.
- They stayed with the Ansari Kulthum ibn al-Hadim (ra), reflecting how early they reached Medina.
- Sa'd ibn Khawla (ra) fought at Badr and the other battles, but died in Mecca during the Farewell Pilgrimage, saddening the Prophet ﷺ.
- Subay'a (ra) gave birth just two weeks after her husband's death.
- Her case established that a pregnant widow's iddah ends upon giving birth, not after the full four months and ten days.
- After two proposals, she married the older suitor, Abu Sanabil (ra), after the Prophet ﷺ confirmed her iddah was complete.
- She narrated the hadith about the virtue of dying in Medina, transmitted from her by Ibn Umar (ra).

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A pregnant widow's waiting period (iddah) ends at childbirth — Subay'a's case is the legal precedent for this ruling.
- Remarrying soon after the loss of a spouse is permissible and should not be looked down upon; companions varied — some remarried quickly, some never did, without it reflecting on their love or righteousness.
- The "weak" and downtrodden of society are significant in the sight of Allah; victory often comes through such devoted believers.
- Women were among the recognized scholars and jurists of the companions, teaching even prominent figures like Ibn Umar (ra).
- It is recommended to aspire to live, die, and be buried in Medina, and to make dua for it, as Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) did.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "O Allah, complete the hijrah for my companions and do not turn them back on their heels." (from the hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas's illness, Bukhari)
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever among you can die in Medina, then let them do so, for I will intercede for whoever dies in Medina." (narrated by Ibn Umar from Subay'a, ra)
- Qur'an (referenced): "Do not turn away those who call upon their Lord morning and evening, seeking His face."
