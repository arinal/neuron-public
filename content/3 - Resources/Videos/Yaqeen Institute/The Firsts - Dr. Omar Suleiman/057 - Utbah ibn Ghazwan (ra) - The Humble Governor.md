# Episode 57 — Utbah ibn Ghazwan (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 57 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mgQRrz0Ermk) · ⏱ 16:18

**Who they were:** Utbah ibn Ghazwan (ra) was among the earliest Muslims — by his own account the seventh to embrace Islam — a brave field commander who conquered Basra and became its humble governor, famous for his asceticism and refusal to be seduced by worldly power.

## Summary
Utbah ibn Ghazwan (ra) belongs to the last set of companions in the series: warriors who narrated no hadith and lived largely outside social life, yet were entrusted by the Prophet ﷺ with critical missions. Utbah was the son of Ghazwan ibn al-Harith, from the small tribe of Banu Mazin. He was not from Quraysh himself but became a *halif* (pledged ally) of Banu Nawfal, the tribe of Mut'im ibn Adi. Tall, handsome, strong, eloquent and described as having light upon his face, he embraced Islam around the age of thirty, roughly ten years younger than the Prophet ﷺ. By his own narration he was the seventh person to accept Islam, or at least among the first ten or fifteen.

His early life is sparsely documented because, as Dr. Omar notes, Utbah's biography "speaks through the battlefield." He made three hijras — twice to Abyssinia and once to Medina. His sisters also embraced Islam: Fakhita bint al-Harith later married Uthman ibn Affan (ra), and his youngest sister Basra (with a *sin*) later married Abu Hurayrah (ra), making Utbah a brother-in-law to both.

In Medina the Prophet ﷺ paired him in brotherhood with the great Ansari warrior Abu Dujana (ra) — a sign of his similar character and imposing presence. Utbah fought bravely at Badr, Uhud, Khandaq and all the major battles, was appointed to special raids for his expertise, and later fought in the wars against the apostates under Abu Bakr (ra).

His most famous chapter came under Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), who dispatched him against the Persians with a small but seasoned battalion (300–2000 men, many from Banu Thaqif). He made massive gains and, with Umar's permission, conquered the strategic Persian city of Al-Ubullah. From this came the city of Basra — one of the "two Iraqs" alongside Kufa, and a future fountainhead of Islamic scholarship and civilization. Umar appointed Utbah its governor for six months.

Despite his authority, Utbah was deeply uncomfortable with the sudden wealth and palaces falling into Muslim hands. Rather than build a home, he pitched a tent. He longed to return to the field and begged Umar to relieve him of governorship, but Umar insisted he remain, refusing to be left as the only companion holding such a post. Saddened, Utbah mounted his camel in Medina and prayed, "O Allah, do not return me to it." Allah answered his dua: on the way home to Iraq, this man who had commanded great armies fell from his camel and passed away.

## Key Moments
- Embraced Islam very early — by his own narration the seventh Muslim after the Prophet ﷺ.
- Made three hijras: twice to Abyssinia and once to Medina.
- Paired in brotherhood with Abu Dujana (ra), the great Ansari warrior.
- Fought at Badr, Uhud, Khandaq, all major battles, and the wars of apostasy under Abu Bakr (ra).
- Conquered the strategic Persian city of Al-Ubullah, from which the city of Basra arose.
- Appointed governor of Basra by Umar (ra), yet pitched a tent instead of taking a palace.
- Delivered his famous khutbah recalling the hardship of the early Muslims (his only narrated tradition).
- Begged Umar to relieve him of office; prayed to never return to it, and died falling from his camel on the way home.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True leadership in Islam is a trust (amanah), not a prize — the companions disliked positions of power even as they shouldered them faithfully.
- Utbah's tent amid palaces models detachment from dunya at the very moment wealth becomes available.
- His prayer — to seek refuge from being "great in my own eyes and small in the sight of Allah" — is a timeless warning against self-importance.
- A "silent soldier" who narrated nothing may still leave an immense legacy; the scholarship and civilization of Basra flowed from his conquest.
- The contrast with how people today fight and corrupt themselves over power highlights the purity of these early Muslims' intentions.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "I found myself as the seventh among seven who had been with Allah's Messenger ﷺ… the only thing we had to eat were the leaves of a wild tree, until our gums were ruptured." (Sahih Muslim, Kitab al-Zuhd; also Ibn Majah)
- "I seek refuge in Allah from being great in my own eyes and small in the sight of Allah." (*A'udhu billahi an akuna azeeman 'inda nafsi saghiran 'inda Allah*)
- "O Allah, do not return me to it." (*Allahumma la taruddani ilayha*) — his dua to be freed from governorship, which Allah answered.
- The Prophet ﷺ said the *halif* (ally) of a people is considered one of them.
