# Episode 51 — Mus'ab ibn Umair (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 51 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AV6_YDffZLY) · ⏱ 1:17:48

**Who they were:** A close companion of the Prophet ﷺ who gave up immense wealth and luxury for Islam, became the first ambassador (safeer) of Islam sent to Yathrib (Medina), and was martyred at Uhud as the flag bearer. He is remembered as "the man who gave it all."

## Summary
Mus'ab ibn Umair (ra) came from Banu Abd al-Dar, one of the richest and smallest tribes of Quraysh. The only child of his doting mother Khunnas bint Malik, he inherited a fortune said to rival nearly half the wealth of all Mecca. He was renowned as the most handsome, best-dressed, and most fragrant young man in the city — a trendsetter whose perfume Meccans would imitate. Despite his privilege, he was never arrogant or cruel, exemplifying the Prophet's ﷺ saying that the best of people in Jahiliyyah are the best in Islam.

Hearing of the secret gatherings at Dar al-Arqam, Mus'ab (ra) slipped in quietly one night and embraced Islam after hearing the Prophet ﷺ speak of Paradise and the Hellfire. He kept his faith hidden until Uthman ibn Talha discovered him praying and informed his mother. She imprisoned him in the house, depriving him of food and comfort but never striking him. When the Muslims migrated to Abyssinia, Mus'ab (ra) escaped and joined them, marrying Hamna bint Jahsh. Returning to Mecca on a false rumor that Quraysh had accepted Islam, he faced his mother again; she offered him back his wealth if he recanted, but he refused, walking away from everything — destined to be Mecca's richest boy, leaving without a single dirham.

When six young men from Yathrib accepted Islam during Hajj and asked for a teacher, the Prophet ﷺ chose Mus'ab (ra) to be Islam's first ambassador to the city. Hosted by As'ad ibn Zurarah (ra), Mus'ab disarmed the hostile chiefs Usaid ibn Hudayr and Sa'd ibn Mu'adh with his calm wisdom and beautiful recitation, winning them and Sa'd ibn Ubadah to Islam. Within a year he had converted much of Yathrib and led the believers as their imam, returning to the Prophet ﷺ with around 70 Ansar for the Second Pledge of Aqaba. He made the people fall so deeply in love with the Prophet ﷺ that they could not wait for his arrival.

Back in Medina, Mus'ab (ra) lived in poverty so stark that the Prophet ﷺ wept seeing him in a single patched garment, recalling the luxury he once had. The Prophet ﷺ used this moment to foretell the worldly blessings the ummah would later enjoy, and told them their present state of hardship and worship was better. Mus'ab (ra) carried the Muslim banner at both Badr and Uhud.

At Uhud, after the archers abandoned their post and the tide turned, Mus'ab (ra) saw the enemy advancing on the Prophet ﷺ. He drew them toward himself, raising the flag and shouting takbir. Abdullah ibn Qami'ah severed his right hand; he took the flag in his left; that too was cut; he held it with his arms until a spear killed him, his body bearing over 70 wounds. He so resembled the Prophet ﷺ that some thought the Prophet himself had fallen. When buried, his single cloth could not cover his whole body, so the Prophet ﷺ ordered grass placed over his feet. Companions like Khabbab and Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf wept for years afterward, remembering that Mus'ab (ra) gave everything and received his reward only in the Hereafter.

## Key Moments
- Born into immense wealth as the only child of Khunnas bint Malik; the trendsetting "king" of Mecca's youth, yet humble in character.
- Secretly accepted Islam at Dar al-Arqam after hearing the Prophet ﷺ describe Paradise and Hellfire.
- Discovered by Uthman ibn Talha and imprisoned by his mother, who deprived but never beat him.
- Escaped to Abyssinia, married Hamna bint Jahsh, and later renounced his entire inheritance rather than abandon his faith.
- Chosen as Islam's first ambassador to Yathrib; converted its leading chiefs and much of the city within a year.
- Disarmed the spear-wielding Usaid ibn Hudayr with calm kindness and Qur'an, then won over Sa'd ibn Mu'adh and Sa'd ibn Ubadah.
- Served as flag bearer at both Badr and Uhud.
- Martyred at Uhud after both hands were severed, drawing the enemy away from the Prophet ﷺ; buried beside Hamza (ra) in a cloth too short to cover him.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- "Allah has purchased from the believers their lives and their wealth in exchange for Paradise" — Mus'ab (ra) embodies this verse, trading total worldly privilege for the everlasting reward.
- The truth has consequences: deep faith demands sacrifice — of comfort, status, wealth, and even family ties.
- Every Muslim is an ambassador of the Prophet ﷺ; Mus'ab made people love the Prophet ﷺ through his own character before they ever met him.
- Good character and gentle wisdom (as with Usaid ibn Hudayr) can disarm hostility and open hearts more effectively than force.
- Worldly hardship endured for Allah can be a higher state than later ease and blessing.
- Visiting the martyrs of Uhud and sending salam to them is a living sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Mus'ab (ra) to his mother: "Love of Muhammad ﷺ, O my mother, is greater than my love for you... through him Allah brought me from darkness to light."
- The Prophet ﷺ, seeing Mus'ab in his poverty, foretold: "How will it be when you wake in one fine garment and sleep in another... and adorn your homes as the Ka'bah is covered?"
- Qur'an recited by Mus'ab at Uhud (3:144): "Muhammad is no more than a messenger; messengers have passed away before him. If he were to die or be killed, would you turn back on your heels?"
- The Prophet ﷺ over the martyrs (33:23): "Among the believers are men true to what they pledged to Allah..." and: "I bear witness that you are the martyrs before Allah on the Day of Judgment."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Visit them, come to them and give salam to them. By the One in whose hand is my soul, no Muslim gives them salam until the Day of Judgment except that they return it to him."
- Imam al-Bayhaqi's reflection: what man could meet Allah with the whole of Medina in his scale (mizan) — as Mus'ab (ra) does?
