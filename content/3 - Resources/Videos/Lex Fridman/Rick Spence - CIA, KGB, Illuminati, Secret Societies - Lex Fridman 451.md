# Rick Spence — CIA, KGB, Illuminati, Secret Societies, Cults & Conspiracies | Lex Fridman #451

> A 3.5-hour talk with **Rick Spence**, a historian who studies spies, secret societies, cults, the occult, and famous crimes. He's careful to separate **what we know** from **what we merely believe** — and reminds us that history is mostly *opinions*, not certainties. Below is the whole thing in plain English.

## The big picture in 60 seconds

- **Spies win by infiltration, not James Bond stunts.** The Russians have been the best at this for over a century — planting agents inside enemy groups and quietly steering them.
- People betray their country for four reasons, remembered as **"MICE": Money, Ideology, Coercion (blackmail), and Ego.** Ego is often the biggest.
- Powerful people love **rituals and secret clubs** (Bohemian Grove, Bilderberg, the Illuminati). The rituals mostly create a feeling of belonging and importance — but secrecy also attracts big egos and hides real influence.
- Many famous conspiracy theories are **partly plausible because the raw ingredients are real** (pedophile rings, blackmail, secret societies, cults all genuinely exist) — even when the specific theory is false.
- The Nazi party grew out of a tiny **occult/nationalist secret society** and was built as a "**counter-communism**" — copying the enemy rather than just opposing it.
- The **"Protocols of the Elders of Zion"** (the most damaging antisemitic hoax ever) is a fake — Spence traces its murky origins and explains *why fakes spread*: they tell people a story they already want to believe.
- His summary of human nature: *"Most people, most of the time, are polite, cooperative and kind — until they're not."* And the moment they flip is unpredictable.

## Spies & intelligence agencies

### The Russians set the standard
- Going back to the Tsar's secret police (the **Okhrana**), then the Soviet **Cheka/NKVD/KGB**, Russia has been remarkably consistent at spying — mostly by **infiltrating** enemy groups and recruiting informers.
- Their specialty: the **agent provocateur** — plant your own person inside a rebel group, push them into leadership, then goad them into actions you can crush.
- By 1917 they'd infiltrated *every* revolutionary party so thoroughly they practically knew every decision in advance. Spence's provocative aside: it's not crazy to wonder whether Lenin himself was, wittingly or not, useful to the secret police — he split the Marxists into warring factions, which is exactly what the police wanted.
- **Who actually overthrew the Tsar?** Not the revolutionaries — a political coup inside parliament did (Feb 1917). Lenin only took over 8 months *later*. And the old secret police simply **rebranded into the new Soviet one** — same professionals, new boss ("you do it for whoever pays you").

### How you recruit a traitor — "MICE"
- **M**oney — greed (e.g. CIA traitor Aldrich Ames needed cash for an expensive wife).
- **I**deology — true believers (1930s Communists) *or* disillusioned ex-believers ("no anti-communist like an ex-communist").
- **C**oercion — blackmail (gambling debts, secrets). A classic trick: threaten to spread a rumor that the person is *already* a snitch — now their own comrades will kill them, so they have no choice but to cooperate.
- **E**go — the thrill of secretly outsmarting the powerful people you pretend to serve. Spence thinks ego was the real driver for famous spy Kim Philby.

### CIA vs KGB, and the dark stuff
- Key structural difference: the KGB did **both** foreign spying *and* internal security in one agency; the US **split** it (CIA abroad, FBI at home) — and the two have always been rivals.
- The job requires being **willing to lie and to kill** — normal moral rules are suspended.
- **MK-Ultra:** a real CIA mind-control program (1950s–70s). Whether or not they could actually erase/implant memories or create "switchable" assassin personalities, **the point is they believed they could and tried** — running drug and hypnosis experiments on people *without consent*. When scrutiny came in the 1970s, they destroyed the records — and learned to **fund such work through private cutouts** for deniability.

### Epstein
- Spence's read: whether or not Epstein *intended* blackmail, filming powerful men with young girls **creates blackmail material** — which is enormously valuable to *someone*. There's a long history of exactly this (he cites a 1930s Berlin occultist who filmed VIPs for blackmail). So Epstein was either running his own blackmail operation or being used as a front for one.

## Secret clubs, rituals & the occult

### Bohemian Grove & the Bilderbergs
- **Bohemian Grove** is a *place* (a redwood forest) where the **Bohemian Club** — an old rich-men's club — holds a summer camp with silly skits, robes, and a giant owl statue ("Cremation of Care" ritual).
- Is it sinister? Spence's honest answer: probably not *human sacrifice* (as Alex Jones claimed), but it's genuinely a **ritual** — and rituals build group solidarity. More importantly, the club's "Lakeside Talks" are a real venue where **political candidates get vetted** (Nixon realized he was being auditioned there in 1968).
- The deeper question with any such group: **who's actually running it?** The point of secrecy is you can never quite tell.

### What "the occult" and "magic" really mean
- "Occult" just means **hidden** — the idea that the visible world is a tiny slice of a bigger reality.
- Crowley's definition of magic: *"causing change in conformity with will"* — basically **mind over matter / willpower + ritual.** Spence's everyday example: a **high-school pep rally** is a magic ritual — a crowd in costume chanting for their team to win and the enemy to lose. "Manifesting" is the same idea repackaged.
- Whether or not magic is *real*, what matters historically is that **people believe it is** and act on it — and crowds sharing a belief can genuinely reshape reality.

### Cults
- Difference between a cult and a religion (only half-joking): *"in a cult, the person at the top knows it's a scam; in a religion, that person is dead."* Tell-tale sign of a cult: **excessive reverence for the leader.**
- The engine underneath most of this is a basic human need: **the desire to belong** — which repeatedly gets people into trouble.

## How the Nazis actually started

- They grew out of the tiny **Thule Society** in Munich (1918) — an **occult + racist nationalist** study group led by a shady figure, "von Sebottendorff," who had connections to Ottoman intrigue, Freemasonry, and gun-running.
- Its funder was the **German Army**, which was terrified of Germany going communist like Russia. So they created the **German Workers' Party** not as *anti*-communism but as **counter-communism** — a copy that steals the enemy's appeal ("socialist," "workers'") but redirects it toward nationalism. That party became the **Nazi party**.
- **Hitler was an Army propagandist** sent in to take it over — trained in public speaking, kept on the payroll, and slotted into leadership.
- **Where did antisemitism plug in?** They framed *both* capitalism and communism as secretly "Jewish." Spence explains the roots: after 1800s "Jewish emancipation," Jews became visible in banking, law, medicine, *and* left-wing politics — so people blamed them for all the disruptive changes of the industrial age. (He also dislikes the word "antisemitism" — it's a euphemism invented in the 1870s to make plain "Jew-hatred" sound scientific.)

## The "Protocols of the Elders of Zion" (the great hoax)

- The most influential antisemitic forgery ever — fake "meeting notes" of Jews and Freemasons plotting world domination.
- Spence debunks the popular origin story (that the Okhrana wrote it in Paris in 1904) — it actually appeared earlier, serialized in an obscure Russian right-wing newspaper in **1903**, and is clumsily stitched together from an old French satire.
- His own theory: the raw material came from **1890s France**, which was awash in conspiracy panics (the Dreyfus affair, financial scandals, and the "Taxil hoax" about satanic Freemasons — which its own author later publicly admitted he **made up** to mock gullible believers).
- **Why fakes spread** — his "**turd on a plate**" metaphor: once the thing exists, people stop asking where it came from and just react to it (today we'd say it "goes viral"). It survived debunking because it **told a story people wanted to believe** — a simple enemy to blame for a confusing, painful world.
- On scapegoating in general: antisemitism (and all group-hatred) treats a wildly diverse group as a **single, unified mass** — and people who hold these views constantly make exceptions for the actual individuals they meet, because they're not really reacting to real people at all.

## Famous crimes

### Charles Manson
- Manson was an unremarkable petty criminal until ~33, then suddenly became a cult "mastermind." Spence finds that leap suspicious and suspects Manson was a **protected police/narcotics informant** (he repeatedly dodged parole violations and arrests).
- **Manson didn't personally kill the Tate–LaBianca victims** — he ordered it. The official "Helter Skelter race-war" motive never made sense.
- Spence's simpler theory: it started with the drug-deal murder of **Gary Hinman**; when an associate (Bobby Beausoleil) got arrested for it, the later killings may have been **copycat murders staged to get him off** ("he couldn't have done it — look, it's still happening").
- How did a scrawny guy control everyone? A mix of **LSD, sex, and preying on young people from broken homes** who craved a family — again, the pull of belonging.

### The Zodiac Killer
- Never caught. Spence isn't even sure it was **one person** — the early murders targeted couples, the last (a cab driver) was totally different.
- The killer claimed he was "collecting slaves for the afterlife." A neat clue Spence highlights: the killer's spooky "wheel of death" card was **copied from a 1951 Western comic book cover** — so he was drawing from pop culture.
- Toying with the press (like Jack the Ripper) is a common psychopath pattern.

### Are the killers connected?
- The Zodiac, Son of Sam, and the "Monster of Florence" all had a **satanic-cult angle** attached. Spence's balanced take: either psychopaths independently think alike, or there's some shared inspiration — and since satanic cults, ritual murders, and serial killers *all genuinely exist*, "never say something is too crazy for people to do."

## The Illuminati & the nature of secret societies

- The real Illuminati was founded **May 1, 1776** by a Bavarian professor, **Adam Weishaupt**. Goal: replace all religions and governments with a single Illuminati-run "new world order." It was banned repeatedly but the **brand never died** — because "Illuminati" just means "the enlightened ones," so anyone can claim it.
- Weishaupt's recruiting insight: *"nothing is more attractive than a concealed mystery"* — keep the inner secrets hidden and people will want in. (He also cynically planned to lure women with promises of emancipation he never intended to deliver.)
- **Spence's definition of a secret society:** the *existence* usually isn't secret — *what they do* is. They're **self-selecting** (they pick you), require **initiation/tests**, and demand **oaths of secrecy**. By that definition, **intelligence agencies are basically secret societies** — same recruitment, same oaths, same "we guard special knowledge" mindset.
- On real power today: the most powerful secret society is *"probably the one you don't know about."* But he **doesn't** believe a single small group secretly runs everything — "if they did, it would run more efficiently." What worries him more is **many rival groups who each think they're in charge.**

## Wisdom worth keeping

- *"Always be clear about what you know and what you believe."* We know very little for certain; we act mostly on beliefs we were told — and those beliefs, true or not, drive our actions (as the Nazis tragically showed).
- Beautiful utopian ideas ("from each according to his ability...") have repeatedly led to enormous suffering — the law of unintended consequences.
- His closing note of hope: **most people are cooperative and kind most of the time**, and you usually get back what you put in.
