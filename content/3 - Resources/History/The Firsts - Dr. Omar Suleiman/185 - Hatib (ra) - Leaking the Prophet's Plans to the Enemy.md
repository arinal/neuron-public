# Episode 185 — Hatib ibn Abi Balta'a (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 1 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yDEBQZvvr1c) · ⏱ 46:41

**Who they were:** Hatib ibn Abi Balta'a (ra) was an early Muslim, a veteran of Badr, an elite archer, and the ambassador the Prophet ﷺ sent to Al-Muqawqis of Egypt. He is most famous — and most misunderstood — for leaking the Prophet's ﷺ plans to the leaders of Mecca before its conquest, a mistake the Prophet ﷺ forgave.

## Summary
Dr. Omar opens a new theme of the series: praised, early companions who nonetheless made catastrophic mistakes after years of devoted service — yet were given a path of redemption rather than being "cancelled." Hatib (ra) is the first of these. A foreigner from the tribe of Lakhm in Yemen, he settled in Mecca without tribal roots or protective structure, making his life precarious. He was handsome, well-built with distinctively large, thick-fingered hands, literate, a poet, an elite horseman and archer, a trustworthy businessman, and above all known for his *wafa* (loyalty) — ironic given the one incident he is remembered for.

He embraced Islam early in Mecca, apparently keeping it quiet and escaping heavy persecution. He migrated to Medina with his freed servant Saad, and the Prophet ﷺ paired him in brotherhood with a companion of Masjid Quba. As an elite archer he attended every single battle — Badr, Uhud, Khandaq and all the rest — yet, fitting the quiet defensive role of an archer, no battlefield tales survive. Dr. Omar notes the beauty of Allah concealing the identity of the 40 archers who descended prematurely at Uhud, forgiving them entirely.

Hatib was a loyal confidant of the Prophet ﷺ: he was sent to carry the Prophet's ﷺ marriage proposal to Umm Salamah (ra), reassuring her about her daughter and her jealousy. As ambassador to Egypt, he brilliantly answered Al-Muqawqis's challenges — comparing the Prophet ﷺ to Jesus who did not call down destruction on his people, and warning the ruler with the example of Pharaoh. Al-Muqawqis declined Islam but sent gifts, including Maria and Sirin, whom Hatib invited to Islam on the journey to Medina.

Then came the fall. As the Prophet ﷺ secretly prepared to march on Mecca, Hatib, fearing for his defenseless family there, wrote a letter to Quraysh disclosing the plans, sent with a woman. Revelation exposed it; the Prophet ﷺ dispatched Ali, Az-Zubair and Al-Miqdad, who intercepted her and retrieved the letter hidden in her braided hair. Confronted, Hatib swore he acted not out of disbelief or hypocrisy but only to win protection for his relatives, knowing Allah would grant the Prophet ﷺ victory regardless.

Umar (ra) wished to strike him down as a hypocrite, but the Prophet ﷺ affirmed Hatib spoke the truth and reminded Umar that he had witnessed Badr — about whom Allah said, "Do as you wish, for I have forgiven you." The verse forbidding taking enemies as allies was revealed. The Prophet ﷺ neither flogged, boycotted, nor humiliated him; he forgave him, kept him close, and took him along to the conquest of Mecca. Hatib (ra) died around 30 AH in the time of Uthman, his legacy preserved by his son Abdur Rahman, a trustworthy hadith narrator.

## Key Moments
- A Yemeni foreigner without tribal protection, Hatib embraced Islam early and preceded his own tribe.
- An elite archer who attended every battle alongside the Prophet ﷺ, playing the crucial, unsung defensive role.
- A weak-chained but character-fitting narration has him avenging the wounded Prophet ﷺ at Uhud against Utbah ibn Abi Waqqas.
- He carried the Prophet's ﷺ marriage proposal to Umm Salamah (ra), conveying reassurances about her daughter and her jealousy.
- As ambassador to Egypt, he outwitted Al-Muqawqis with arguments from the story of Jesus and the example of Pharaoh.
- He gave da'wah to Maria and Sirin (ra) on the journey back to Medina, and both embraced Islam.
- He leaked the Prophet's ﷺ war plans to Quraysh in a letter intercepted by Ali, Az-Zubair and Al-Miqdad at Rawdat Khakh.
- The Prophet ﷺ forgave him on account of his presence at Badr, and the verse of Surah Al-Mumtahanah was revealed.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ judged Hatib by the entirety of his life, not his lowest moment — a mercy we often deny one another.
- We never know if we have earned our own "Badr moment"; we must live with *husn al-dhann* in Allah while never presuming forgiveness.
- The forgiveness of Hatib did not make the other companions of Badr complacent — Umar, Ali and others never "retired" from striving and fearing Allah.
- Even those who have harmed the Muslim community have a door of *tawbah* and should be welcomed through it, not frozen in their past.
- A catastrophic mistake by a righteous person does not erase a lifetime of sincerity and service.
- The one most wronged — the Prophet ﷺ himself — chose mercy, defending Hatib even afterward against those who wished him hellfire.

- Qur'an (Aal Imran, on Uhud): "Those of you who turned back on the day the two armies met — Satan only caused them to slip because of some of what they earned. But Allah has already forgiven them; indeed Allah is Forgiving and Forbearing."

- The Prophet ﷺ confronting him: "Ma hadha ya Hatib?" — "What is this, O Hatib?"
- The Prophet ﷺ to one who wished Hatib in the Fire: "You have lied. He will not enter the Fire, for he witnessed Badr and Hudaybiyyah."
- "Khiyarukum fil-Jahiliyyah khiyarukum fil-Islam idha faqihu" — "The best of you in the days of ignorance are the best of you in Islam, if they gain understanding."
