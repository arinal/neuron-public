# Episode 13 — The Plague of Amwas and the Sahaba

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 13 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=k8gVIyoXEUg) · ⏱ 1:00:10

**Who they were:** A special episode framed around the Plague of Amwas (Ta'un Amwas, 639 CE), which took the lives of hundreds of companions including Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah (ra) — "the trustworthy one (Ameen) of this Ummah" — and Mu'adh ibn Jabal (ra). Delivered during the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, it draws spiritual lessons from how the Sahaba responded to mass disease.

## Summary
Dr. Omar Suleiman departs from the usual format of "the great firsts" to address "the first plague," prompted by the spread of coronavirus and the wave of fiqh discussions about gathering, hajj, and Umrah. Rather than focus narrowly on jurisprudence, he approaches the topic spiritually, returning to the closest parallel the Sahaba experienced. He opens with the Prophet's ﷺ hadith that Allah granted two of his three requests — that this Ummah would never be entirely destroyed by natural disaster nor wiped out by an external enemy — but withheld the third, leaving internal division as something the Ummah must guard against itself. He stresses that despite this protection, the Prophet ﷺ turned to prayer and du'a at the slightest change in circumstances, modeling how to respond to signs of human vulnerability before Allah.

He explains the concept of ta'un (plague), citing the hadith that it was a punishment Allah sent upon whom He willed, yet made a mercy for the believers: anyone struck who stays patient in their place, knowing nothing strikes them except what Allah has decreed, receives the reward of a martyr (shaheed). Several hadith broaden the categories of martyrdom to include death by plague, stomach illness, drowning, being crushed, fire, pregnancy, and respiratory illness.

The Plague of Amwas was foretold by the Prophet ﷺ at the Battle of Tabuk, when he listed six signs before the Day of Judgment: his death, the conquest of Jerusalem, a mass plague that would fell people like sheep, an abundance of wealth, a fitna touching every household, and a betrayal by the Byzantines (Bani Asfar). The first three unfolded in rapid succession — the Prophet's ﷺ death (632/633), the conquest of Jerusalem (637), and the plague of Amwas (639) — explaining why the Sahaba felt the Hour was near.

During the caliphate of Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), the plague broke out in Amwas, a town about 25 miles from Jerusalem, killing some 25,000 people, including hundreds of Sahaba. Umar (ra) set out to be with his suffering people but, meeting Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah (ra) and consulting the companions, decided to turn back to protect the wider Ummah. When Abu Ubaidah asked, "Are you fleeing from the decree of Allah?" Umar replied, "We are fleeing from the decree of Allah to the decree of Allah" — establishing that tawakkul means taking precautions, not recklessness. Abdurrahman ibn Awf (ra) then settled the matter with the Prophet's ﷺ hadith establishing the principle of quarantine: do not enter a plague-stricken land, and if you are in one, do not leave it.

Umar (ra) tried to save Abu Ubaidah with an ambiguous summons to Medina, but Abu Ubaidah saw through it and refused to abandon his troops, accepting death as martyrdom. He died at around 58, appointing Mu'adh ibn Jabal (ra) as his successor. Mu'adh prayed that his own family receive the greatest share of this mercy — and his wife, sons, and daughters all died, followed by Mu'adh himself, then in his early thirties. Among the many companions who perished were Sharhabil ibn Hasanah, Yazid ibn Abi Sufyan, al-Fadl ibn Abbas, Suhail ibn Amr, and Abu Malik al-Ashari. Eventually Amr ibn al-As (ra) led the people to disperse into the mountains in small groups, and the plague subsided.

## Key Moments
- The Prophet ﷺ foretold the plague at the Battle of Tabuk as the third of six signs before the Day of Judgment, describing it as a death that would take people "like a plague runs through sheep."
- Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) consulted the companions and chose to turn back from Sham to protect the Ummah, exchanging with Abu Ubaidah the famous words about "fleeing from the decree of Allah to the decree of Allah."
- Abdurrahman ibn Awf (ra) narrated the prophetic hadith on quarantine, resolving the debate among the companions.
- Umar (ra) sent Abu Ubaidah an ambiguous letter hoping to save him; Abu Ubaidah laughed, understood the intent, and refused to leave his troops, accepting martyrdom.
- Abu Ubaidah (ra) died around age 58 after advising that the wisest of Adam's children are those most obedient to their Lord and most prepared for the journey; Umar wept, saying he would have appointed him caliph had he lived.
- Mu'adh ibn Jabal (ra) prayed his family receive the greatest share of the mercy, and lost his entire family; his final exchange with his dying son Abdurrahman was conducted entirely through Qur'anic verses.
- Suhail ibn Amr (ra) — once the Quraysh negotiator at Hudaybiyyah — and his whole family died in the plague after he had become one of the most trustworthy companions.
- Amr ibn al-As (ra) dispersed the people into small groups in the high grounds, after which the plague ended; he later led the conquest of Egypt.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Tawakkul (trust in Allah) means taking practical precautions and then relying on Allah — not recklessness or intentionally putting oneself in harm's way.
- The same affliction can be a punishment for some and a mercy and means of martyrdom for others; the outcome rests with Allah and one's intention and patience.
- Quarantine — not entering a plague-stricken land and not leaving it — is rooted in prophetic guidance centuries before its formal adoption in the 14th century.
- Righteousness does not exempt one from hardship: the most righteous, like Abu Ubaidah and Mu'adh, were taken by the plague.
- Following fiqh guidelines and public-health measures during a crisis is not abandoning patience; sabr is endurance of what befalls you, not causing harm to yourself or others.
- A sincere intention (niyyah) to perform hajj or Umrah is fully rewarded even if one is turned away — so intentions should be strengthened, not weakened, by such circumstances.
- The fragility of life and inevitability of death should end procrastination of good deeds and abandonment of sins, cultivating urgency in worship.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: a plague "was a punishment Allah would send upon whomever He willed, then Allah made it a mercy for the believers" — whoever stays patient in their place, knowing nothing strikes them except what Allah decreed, has the reward of a martyr.
- The Prophet ﷺ: "The martyrs are five: those who die of a plague, of a stomach illness, by drowning, by being crushed, and the one killed in the cause of Allah."
- The Prophet ﷺ on quarantine: "If you hear of a plague in a land, do not enter it; and if it breaks out in a land while you are in it, do not leave it."
- Abu Ubaidah (ra) to Umar: "O Commander of the Believers, are you fleeing from the decree of Allah?" — Umar (ra): "We are fleeing from the decree of Allah to the decree of Allah."
- Abu Ubaidah (ra), dying: "Allah has written death upon the children of Adam, so all of them will die. The wisest is the one most obedient to his Lord and who has done most to prepare for his journey."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Every Ummah has an Ameen (trustworthy one), and the Ameen of this Ummah is Abu Ubaidah."
- Mu'adh (ra) and his son's final exchange: the son recited "The truth is from your Lord, so be not among the doubters" (al-Baqarah), and Mu'adh replied "You will find me, God willing, among the patient" (as-Saffat).
- Umar (ra): "By Allah, if Abu Ubaidah were alive, I would have appointed him as my successor and met Allah in peace."
