# Episode 80 — Ubadah ibn al-Samit (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 80 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BQHGk6swoc8) · ⏱ 59:26

**Who they were:** A leader of the Khazraj tribe of Medina, one of the earliest Ansar to embrace Islam, a memorizer and scribe of the Qur'an, a Badri, and a great military commander — famously described by Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) as "a man worth a thousand men."

## Summary
Ubadah ibn al-Samit (ra) was a chieftain of the Khazraj from the city of Yathrib (Medina) and the son of a Khazraj leader. He was among the very first people of Medina to accept Islam, present at both pledges of Aqabah — the first (called Bay'at al-Nisa because it carried no military component) and the larger second pledge — and was appointed one of the twelve Nuqaba (leaders) for Medina. Through him his siblings, his first wife, and his son al-Walid embraced Islam. He fits into the family of Anas ibn Malik as the second husband of Umm Haram bint Milhan, whom he married after she lost her first husband and son at Uhud.

He is described as a strikingly tall, exceedingly black Yemeni man of imposing and beautiful presence, eloquent, knowledgeable, and a trusted negotiator of truces both before and after Islam. He accompanied the Prophet ﷺ in every battle, was a Badri, served as a scribe of revelation (Kutab al-Wahi), and was one of only five prominent Ansar who memorized the entire Qur'an — alongside Mu'adh ibn Jabal, Abu Ayyub al-Ansari, Abu Darda, and Ubayy ibn Ka'b. He combined rare qualities: a patient teacher of Ahl as-Suffa by day, a devoted worshipper at night, and a lion in battle.

In Medina he helped set up shelters for the migrants, facilitated the hijrah, negotiated treaties with the Jewish tribes, and served as a zakah collector — a role from which the Prophet's ﷺ warning about misusing charity left him so shaken that he never again wished to be placed in authority, though leadership was repeatedly thrust upon him. After the Prophet ﷺ passed, he served as a judge under Abu Bakr (ra) and as a military commander whose campaigns consistently ended in victory.

When Mu'awiyah requested teachers for the new Muslims of Ash-Sham, Umar (ra) gathered the five Ansari memorizers; Abu Ayyub (elderly) and Ubayy (ill) stayed, and Ubadah, Mu'adh, and Abu Darda went. Ubadah settled in Palestine with Umm Haram and founded a lasting culture of Qur'an memorization there, producing generations of huffaz whose chains trace back through him to the Prophet ﷺ. After a dispute with Mu'awiyah he returned to Medina, but Umar sent him back, declaring "May Allah disgrace a land that has neither you nor those like you," and uniquely made him answerable to no one but himself.

Ubadah played a major role in the conquest of Egypt under Amr ibn al-As (ra), where his famous confrontation with the Roman ruler al-Muqawqis displayed the moral revolution of Islam. He helped develop Fustat (Egypt's first Muslim capital) and built its first masjid, known today as Masjid Amr ibn al-As. He then led naval campaigns against the Roman base in Cyprus, where Umm Haram died as a martyr as the Prophet ﷺ had foretold. Retiring to Palestine, he taught Qur'an, narrated over a hundred hadith, and warned against the early stirrings of fitna against Uthman (ra). He died in Palestine a few years after his wife, and is buried in the Bab al-Rahma cemetery beside Masjid al-Aqsa.

## Key Moments
- Took both pledges of Aqabah and was appointed one of the twelve Nuqaba of Medina; narrated the foundational pledge upon six moral principles recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari.
- Warned by the Prophet ﷺ as a zakah collector that any dirham wrongly taken would testify against him on the Day of Judgment — leaving him trembling and ever-reluctant to hold authority.
- Married Umm Haram bint Milhan after Uhud and was praised by her for his excellent character toward her son.
- Umar (ra) called him "a man worth a thousand men" (bi alfi rajul), sending him to battle with the promise that victory would come at his hand.
- His confrontation with al-Muqawqis in Egypt: the ruler demanded "this black man" be removed, and the companions replied that Ubadah was their leader and best, "for black and white are the same to us — none is superior except by piety."
- Built Fustat's first masjid (Masjid Amr ibn al-As) and developed Egypt's first capital.
- Led the first naval expeditions in Islam against Cyprus, where Umm Haram was martyred upon landing, yet he still led the campaign to victory.
- On his deathbed he gathered everyone he had ever interacted with and asked them to take retribution for any wrong of his hand or tongue — and not one person had a complaint.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True leadership in Islam is measured by knowledge, piety (taqwa), and character — not by color, wealth, or worldly rank.
- Righteous people are a source of blessing and protection for the land they inhabit; Umar's words show how a community becomes vulnerable without them.
- Genuine piety produces fear of accountability even after a life of great accomplishment — Ubadah's deathbed concern was whether he had hurt anyone with his hand or tongue.
- The reluctance to seek power, combined with integrity, is precisely what makes one worthy of being entrusted with it.
- A single sincere teacher can launch a culture of learning that benefits generations, as Ubadah did with the Qur'an in Palestine.
- The testimony of faith (shahada) is the key to salvation — but a key with teeth, requiring effort; hope must never breed complacency.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The pledge of Aqabah: that we would worship none but Allah, not steal, not commit unlawful sexual behavior, not kill our children, not slander the innocent, and not disobey in what is good (Sahih al-Bukhari).
- "We pledged to hear and obey in ease and hardship, in eagerness and reluctance... and to stand firm upon the truth wherever we were, fearing not the blame of the blamers."
- The companions to al-Muqawqis: "Indeed black and white are the same to us; none is superior except by taqwa."
- Umar (ra): "May Allah disgrace a land that has neither you nor those like you" — and: "He is the ameer of himself; leave him alone."
- Ubadah's deathbed plea for forgiveness, ending with: "O Allah, bear witness" that none had a complaint against him.
- His final narrated hadith: "I heard the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say: Whoever bears witness that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah, Allah will forbid the Fire for him."
