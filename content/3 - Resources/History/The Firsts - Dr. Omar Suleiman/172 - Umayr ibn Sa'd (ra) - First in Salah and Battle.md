# Episode 172 — Umayr ibn Sa'd al-Ansari (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 14 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SuF1x3GrIEI) · ⏱ 31:34

**Who they were:** A young Ansari orphan of Medina raised in the shade of the Prophet ﷺ, famed for always being in the first row of prayer and battle, who later became the incorruptible governor of Damascus and Homs under Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra). Umar honored him with the title *Nasij wahdah* — "one of a kind," uniquely woven.

## Summary
Umayr ibn Sa'd (ra) was orphaned young, much like Zayd and Usamah. His biological father was actually a companion who attended Badr and died shortly after, fading from the records. His mother later married Julas ibn Suwayd, a wealthy, influential, and intelligent senior of Medina who raised Umayr with great love, becoming like a true father to him. Umayr embraced Islam around the age of ten and became known above all for constantly standing in the first row of salah, growing up attached to the masjid.

The defining incident of his youth came during the call to the expedition of Tabuk, the difficult campaign in which the hypocrites held back and made every excuse to avoid going out, while sincere believers gave everything they had. Umayr, too young to fight, urged his stepfather Julas to come forward and contribute as the sincere Muslims were doing. Julas instead uttered words of disbelief: that if Muhammad ﷺ is truthful, then "we are worse than donkeys" — revealing that he had only joined Islam to play both sides.

Umayr was placed in an agonizing position: Julas was the most beloved person to him after the Prophet ﷺ, yet to stay silent would be to betray his religion. He chose his faith, reporting the statement to the Prophet ﷺ. Julas denied everything, and it became word against word until Allah revealed verse 74 of Surah at-Tawbah, exposing the oath-swearing hypocrites and confirming Umayr's truthfulness. The Prophet ﷺ told him his ears were truthful and his Lord had confirmed what he said. Crucially, the verse left the door open for repentance — and Julas did repent, later weeping in gratitude that his stepson had saved him from hellfire.

This early integrity carried into Umayr's later life. Quiet and unassuming, he served in the armies of Abu Ubaydah throughout Syria and was stationed in Palestine (Filastin). Umar — who valued integrity above all in the wealthy, easily corrupting province of Syria — appointed him governor of Damascus, then moved him to Homs, a region of great riches. There Umayr delivered his famous sermon on Islam as a fortified wall whose strength lies in justice and whose gate is truth.

When a full year passed with no complaints and no money sent to the treasury, a curious Umar summoned him. Umayr walked the entire journey from Homs to Medina on foot — sunburnt, blistered, and nearly dead — carrying only a worn pouch, a bowl, a cup, and his spear. He explained that he had collected and distributed all the wealth among its rightful recipients. Umar reappointed him, and later tested him by sending money through al-Harith, instructing him to give it only if Umayr's circumstances were dire. They were: Umayr and his wife were living in such poverty that they gave their only bread to their guest. Yet Umayr refused the gift, sending it back with the message that he had no need of it. When pressed, he distributed it to the poor and especially the children of the martyrs, saying he had "sent it ahead" for himself for the Day of Judgment.

## Key Moments
- Orphaned young and raised lovingly by his stepfather Julas ibn Suwayd, a wealthy senior of Medina.
- Known throughout the Prophet's ﷺ lifetime for always standing in the first row of prayer.
- Urged Julas to support the Tabuk expedition; Julas responded with words of disbelief.
- Reported the statement to the Prophet ﷺ despite Julas being the most beloved person to him after the Prophet ﷺ.
- Surah at-Tawbah (9:74) was revealed confirming Umayr's truthfulness and exposing the hypocrites' false oaths.
- Julas later sincerely repented and thanked Umayr for saving him from hellfire.
- Appointed by Umar as governor of Damascus, then Homs, prized for his incorruptible integrity.
- Walked on foot from Homs to Medina, arriving exhausted, having distributed all wealth justly and kept nothing.
- Refused Umar's gift of money even in poverty, giving it instead to the poor and the children of the martyrs.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Loyalty to Allah and His Messenger ﷺ must come before even our most beloved relationships.
- "Whoever knows Allah in their youth will not sell their religion in their old age" — early integrity shapes a lifetime of character.
- True power and the strength of Islam lie in upholding justice and truth, not in force.
- Positions of authority and wealth are tests; the people of taqwa fear them and do not exploit them.
- Sincerity (ikhlas) is the opposite of hypocrisy — the believers turned every stone to give, while the hypocrites made every excuse.
- We are responsible for honoring the orphans and especially the children of the martyrs, a lesson urgently relevant today.
- The door of repentance remains open; exposure can be a mercy that saves a person from a worse end.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Qur'an, at-Tawbah 9:74: "They come to you bearing oaths... while they have said words of disbelief and have disbelieved after their Islam... and if they repent, that will be better for them."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Umayr: "Verily your ears were truthful in what they heard, and your Lord has confirmed what you have said."
- Julas after repenting: "May Allah reward Umayr for saving me from the hellfire."
- Umayr's sermon in Homs: "Islam is a fortified wall and a secure gate. The wall of Islam is its justice, and its gate is truth... the strength of Islam is not killing with the sword or striking with the whip, but to judge by truth and to take only with justice."
- Umayr on refusing the gift: "I sent it ahead for myself for the day when no children or wealth would benefit a person."
- Umar upon Umayr's passing: "If only I had more like Umayr to govern the affairs of the Muslims."
