# Episode 11 — Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 11 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tbPimlm0r2w) · ⏱ 59:03

**Who they were:** Abu Bakr (ra) was the closest companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the first adult man to embrace Islam, and the first Caliph. This episode focuses on his title As-Siddiq (the truthful one) and the elevated personal standard of goodness he set for himself.

## Summary
This is the second of three episodes on Abu Bakr (ra). Dr. Omar frames him as "sabbaqan li-kulli khayr" — the first to every good, as Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra) described him. This installment focuses on Abu Bakr's elevated standard of excellence; the next will address his irreplaceable status with the Prophet ﷺ.

Abu Bakr's defining title is As-Siddiq. Dr. Omar explains the Siddiq as a person who recognizes the truth, commits to it, and never wavers from it. The Siddiqun form a category between the prophets and the martyrs — and rank above the martyrs — because, judged by intentions, a Siddiq would willingly sacrifice his life for the truth at any moment, while embodying truthfulness across his entire life. Truthfulness for the Siddiq means alignment between speech and reality, and between words and deeds.

The title was given divinely on the tongue of the Prophet ﷺ. After the night journey (al-Isra wal-Mi'raj), Jibreel (as) told the Prophet ﷺ that Abu Bakr would believe him because he is a Siddiq. When the Quraysh rushed to Abu Bakr hoping to shake his faith, he replied, "If he said that, then he has spoken the truth," affirming that he already believed the Prophet ﷺ in things far greater — the revelation itself.

Abu Bakr's certainty was illustrated by his wager with Ubayy ibn Khalaf over the Qur'anic prophecy in Surah ar-Rum that the Romans, though crushingly defeated by the Persians, would be victorious within three to nine years. Abu Bakr staked first ten, then a hundred camels on this prediction, securing the bet on his family before Hijrah. The Romans triumphed in the year of Badr; Abu Bakr collected the hundred camels from Ubayy's inheritance after Ubayy was killed by the Prophet ﷺ at Uhud, and gave it all in charity.

His clarity of vision repeatedly matched the Prophet's ﷺ, even when no one else understood — at the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, where only Abu Bakr saw that Islam would thrive in peace, and at the Prophet's ﷺ death, when Abu Bakr calmly proclaimed, "Whoever used to worship Muhammad, Muhammad is dead; whoever worships Allah, Allah is ever-living."

The episode's central theme is Abu Bakr setting his own standards. Though guaranteed Paradise, he continually outdid himself — fasting, attending funerals, feeding the poor, and visiting the sick all in a single day, earning the Prophet's ﷺ words that such a person would be called from every gate of Paradise. As Caliph he kept milking the goats of widows and secretly served a blind elderly woman caring for orphans — cleaning, washing, grinding wheat, baking, and cooking — without ever revealing his name. Even Allah held him to a higher standard, revealing the verse asking him to forgive his cousin Mistah who had slandered Aisha (ra), to which Abu Bakr immediately responded, "Yes, I love that Allah forgive me," and resumed and increased his charity.

- Abu Bakr's response to the Quraysh about al-Isra wal-Mi'raj: "If he said it, then he has spoken the truth."
- His confident wager with Ubayy ibn Khalaf over the prophecy of Surah ar-Rum, fulfilled in the year of Badr.
- His unique vision at Hudaybiyyah and his composure proclaiming the truth at the Prophet's ﷺ death.
- The Prophet ﷺ praying Abu Bakr would be called from every gate of Paradise after he combined fasting, a funeral, feeding the poor, and visiting the sick in one day.
- Continuing to milk widows' goats even after becoming Caliph, refusing to let the title change his good character.
- Secretly serving a blind woman and her orphans daily, discovered by Umar (ra).
- Insisting a man strike him back after he shoved him while distributing charity, until Umar (ra) persuaded him to compensate the man instead.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A Siddiq recognizes truth, commits to it, and never wavers — with speech and deeds in full alignment.
- Heart and intellect are linked: a pure heart sharpens the ability to recognize truth.
- We are judged by intentions; a sincere intention can earn the reward of deeds we never had the chance to perform.
- Set your own elevated standards and beat your own goals rather than letting others define them for you.
- If you do public good, keep hidden good that only Allah knows — and let it be involved, demanding good.
- Leadership should not diminish humble service; "the leader of a people is their servant."
- True precedence in faith comes not from quantity of worship but from something settled in the heart — certainty in Allah's promise.
