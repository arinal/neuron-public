# Episode 29 — Umm Salama (Hind bint Abi Umayyah) (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 29 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Tw28oyUan74) · ⏱ 44:33

**Who they were:** Umm Salama (ra) — born Hind bint Abi Umayyah ibn al-Mughirah of the tribe of Banu Makhzum — was an early convert to Islam, wife of the companion Abu Salama (ra), and later one of the Mothers of the Believers as a wife of the Prophet ﷺ. This first part of her story focuses on her marriage to Abu Salama and the painful separation of their family.

## Summary
Umm Salama was the daughter of Abu Umayyah, the noble and generous tribal chief of Banu Makhzum — the same powerful tribe that produced Abu Jahl and Khalid ibn al-Walid (her first cousin). She preceded all her brothers (Amir, Abdullah, and al-Walid) to Islam, and was also the foster sister of Ammar ibn Yasir through breastfeeding. Her husband Abu Salama (Abdullah ibn Abdul Asad) was a maternal cousin and foster brother of the Prophet ﷺ, and one of the very earliest converts — reckoned the 11th person to accept Islam, with Umm Salama around the 14th or 15th.

Abu Salama and Umm Salama are considered the first family to migrate to Abyssinia, and are noted as among the first to make all three hijras (twice to Abyssinia, then to Medina). Much of what we know of the Abyssinian period — Najashi, Ja'far's address, and Az-Zubayr's mission — is preserved through Umm Salama's narrations. Their son Salama was born there in exile.

As they prepared to migrate from Mecca to Medina, Banu Makhzum intercepted the family. They forced Umm Salama to stay, sent Abu Salama on alone, and Abu Salama's clan then tore the baby away — dislocating his shoulder in the struggle. Umm Salama was left alone, weeping daily near the Kaaba for an entire year, until her relatives relented and let her go.

Reunited with her child, Umm Salama set out alone for Medina with only her camel and baby. At Tanim she met Uthman ibn Talha — a noble man, not yet Muslim, keeper of the keys of the Kaaba — who escorted her on foot the entire way to Quba, maintaining complete propriety, then turned back without asking any payment. Dr. Omar notes how Allah later repaid him: at the conquest of Mecca, the Prophet ﷺ entrusted the keys of the Kaaba to him and his descendants permanently.

In Medina the family flourished, having more children (Durra, Umar, and Zaynab in addition to Salama). Abu Salama fought at Badr and Uhud, where he was wounded; those wounds eventually claimed his life months later. Before this, he had joyfully taught Umm Salama a du'a he heard from the Prophet ﷺ for times of calamity — the very du'a she would recite at his death.

After Abu Salama's death, Umm Salama turned down proposals from Abu Bakr and Umar (ra), feeling none could equal her late husband — until the Prophet ﷺ himself proposed. She raised three concerns (jealousy, her age, her children), and the Prophet ﷺ addressed each, declaring her family to be his family. He later affirmed that she and her daughter Zaynab were of the Ahlul Bayt.

## Key Moments
- Umm Salama precedes her siblings to Islam; Abu Salama is among the first ~11 converts, before Dar al-Arqam.
- The family makes two hijras to Abyssinia and then to Medina, becoming the first to complete the three hijras; their son Salama is born in Abyssinia.
- Banu Makhzum forcibly separates the family: Abu Salama is sent to Medina alone, and the baby's shoulder is dislocated as both clans fight over the child.
- Umm Salama weeps near the Kaaba daily for a full year until released.
- Uthman ibn Talha (not yet Muslim) nobly escorts her on foot from Tanim to Quba, asking nothing in return.
- At the conquest of Mecca, the Prophet ﷺ permanently entrusts the keys of the Kaaba to Uthman ibn Talha and his descendants.
- Abu Salama teaches Umm Salama the du'a of calamity, then is wounded at Uhud and dies months later; the Prophet ﷺ closes his eyes and prays for him, leading his janazah with nine takbirat.
- Umm Salama declines Abu Bakr and Umar before accepting the Prophet ﷺ, who pledges her family will be his family.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Trials can be prolonged and severe; Umm Salama's year of separation and patient turning to Allah models steadfastness and growth through hardship.
- True nobility lies in character, not lineage — embodied by Uthman ibn Talha's selfless service and honored by the Prophet ﷺ entrusting him the keys of the Kaaba.
- The du'a of calamity — "Inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'un..." — opens the door to Allah replacing loss with something better.
- Be careful with one's words near death and tragedy, for the angels say Ameen to whatever is said.
- The Prophet ﷺ honored Umm Salama's children as his own, demonstrating compassion and full embrace of a blended family.
- A vast portion of Islamic history (the Abyssinia narrations) was preserved through a woman's testimony, highlighting the central role of the Sahabiyat in transmitting knowledge.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Du'a at calamity: "Inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'un. Allahumma ajirni fi musibati wa akhlif li khayran minha" — "To Allah we belong and to Allah we return. O Allah, compensate me for my tragedy and give me better than that which was taken away."
- Umm Salama on her journey: "I have no one but Allah and this child of mine."
- The Prophet ﷺ at Abu Salama's death: "Do not supplicate against yourselves except with good, for the angels say Ameen to whatever you say."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Umm Salama on her concerns: "As for your family, they are my family."
- The Prophet ﷺ on the Ahlul Bayt: "You and your daughter are from the people of the household (Ahlul Bayt)."
