# Episode 170 — Fatima bint Qays (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 16 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fd8XJRjQhTw) · ⏱ 16:54

**Who they were:** Fatima bint Qays (ra) was one of the earliest women emigrants (Muhajirat) to Medina and a companion whose personal experience of divorce and remarriage became a foundational reference point in Islamic jurisprudence, and who also preserved a famous hadith about the Dajjal.

## Summary
Fatima bint Qays (ra) was among the first to embrace Islam in Mecca, alongside her younger brother (about ten years her junior) and her first husband. She came from a powerful and noble tribe, and the books of history describe her as a woman of supreme beauty, intellect, and completeness — the "total package" of elite status. Her first husband, Abu Amr ibn Hafs, belonged to the powerful Makhzum clan (the same tribe of Abu Jahl), underscoring the prominence of her family connections. She made the hijra to Medina with the early refugees.

She is the narrator of a famous and lengthy hadith concerning Tamim al-Dari, who came from a far-away land and recounted to the Prophet ﷺ his encounter with a strange island and signs the Prophet ﷺ had spoken about (the narration of al-Jassasa, the Dajjal). She reported that she was sitting in the front row of the women when the Prophet ﷺ stood on the minbar and relayed Tamim's account.

Much of her significance, however, comes from her divorce. Her husband divorced her with an irrevocable, threefold divorce and provided her with almost nothing — only five containers of barley and five of wheat — with no maintenance, no housing, and no children to support. The Prophet ﷺ approved of that modest provision and instructed her about where to observe her ʿiddah: first in the house of Umm Sharik, but because that home had heavy foot traffic of visiting men, he moved her to the house of the blind companion Ibn Umm Maktum, where she would be secure. This case, occurring before the verses of hijab were revealed, became one of the earliest litigated divorce situations among the Sahaba.

After her ʿiddah, she received two proposals — from Muʿawiya and from Abu Jahm. She consulted the Prophet ﷺ, who advised her against both: Muʿawiya was poor (or would not spend on her), and Abu Jahm "does not put down his stick" (understood as a man who beats women). The Prophet ﷺ instead proposed that she marry Usama ibn Zayd (ra). She was initially reluctant due to the difference in social standing, but submitted, saying she heard and obeyed Allah and His Messenger ﷺ. The marriage proved beautiful and successful despite the class differences, and she said Allah honored and ennobled her through Usama.

She remained a respected reference on matters of divorce, ʿiddah, and post-divorce maintenance — though ʿUmar (ra) chose not to rule by her case, considering it possibly an error in her memory or an exception, preferring the general ruling he knew. Her standing in the community endured: after ʿUmar's assassination, the six companions of the shura convened in her home to deliberate over the choice of the next leader.

## Key Moments
- Embraced Islam early in Mecca with her brother and first husband, and emigrated to Medina among the first Muhajirat.
- Narrated the lengthy hadith of Tamim al-Dari and al-Jassasa (concerning the Dajjal), which she heard from the Prophet ﷺ on the minbar.
- Was divorced irrevocably and left with only five measures of barley and five of wheat, with no housing or maintenance.
- The Prophet ﷺ moved her ʿiddah from Umm Sharik's busy house to the home of the blind companion Ibn Umm Maktum for her security.
- Received proposals from Muʿawiya and Abu Jahm; the Prophet ﷺ warned her against both and recommended Usama ibn Zayd (ra).
- The hadith of the warning against Abu Jahm was ironically narrated by Abu Jahm's own son (Abu Bakr ibn Abi Jahm).
- Married Usama ibn Zayd (ra) in obedience to the Prophet ﷺ and found honor and nobility in the marriage.
- Her case became a touchstone in jurisprudence on divorce and maintenance, with ʿUmar (ra) declining to rule by it.
- After ʿUmar's assassination, the shura council met in her house to decide the next leadership.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ deliberately arranged marriages across social classes to break artificial barriers and transcend man-made divisions.
- It is permissible — indeed recommended — to warn someone of a real harm coming their way; this is a recognized exception to the prohibition of backbiting, not idle talk for entertainment.
- True nobility comes through obedience to Allah and His Messenger ﷺ, not through wealth or lineage, as Fatima discovered by accepting Usama.
- A person of modest standing in worldly terms (like Usama) can be a source of great honor; trust in the Prophet's ﷺ guidance over personal calculation.
- Even seemingly minor figures in the Sira can have an outsized impact, as Fatima's single life situation shaped enduring rulings on divorce and maintenance.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on her two suitors: of Muʿawiya, "he is a poor man who has no money," and of Abu Jahm, "he is a man who does not put down his stick" (i.e., who beats women) — "I advise you to avoid both of them."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Fatima: "Obeying Allah and obeying the Messenger is better for you."
- Fatima's response: "I hear and I obey Allah and His Messenger ﷺ, so marry me to him" — later saying Allah honored, ennobled, and raised her rank through Usama.
