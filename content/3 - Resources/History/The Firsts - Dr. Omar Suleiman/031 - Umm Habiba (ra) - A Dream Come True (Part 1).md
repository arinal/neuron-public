# Episode 31 — Umm Habiba (Ramla bint Abi Sufyan) (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 31 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=suH_xlIQpgY) · ⏱ 42:52

**Who they were:** Umm Habiba (ra), whose given name was Ramla bint Abi Sufyan, was one of the Mothers of the Believers (Ummahat al-Mu'mineen) and a wife of the Prophet ﷺ. She was the daughter of Abu Sufyan, one of the staunchest early opponents of Islam, yet she was among the earliest converts and migrated to Abyssinia.

## Summary
Umm Habiba (ra) was born around 595–596 CE, roughly 25 years younger than the Prophet ﷺ, into the powerful clan of Banu Umayyah — a rival of Banu Hashim and Banu Makhzum and home to many of Islam's fiercest enemies. Her father, Abu Sufyan (Sakhr ibn Harb), was a noble, wealthy, literate diplomat who would become the chief antagonist of the Muslims after Badr, while her mother, Safiyyah bint Abi al-Aas (paternal aunt of Uthman ibn Affan), is believed to have died before Islam. Umm Habiba was thus a first cousin of Uthman (ra) and likely the second person from Banu Umayyah to embrace Islam, preceding her entire family.

She was married to Ubaydullah ibn Jahsh, one of the four pre-Islamic Hanifs (alongside Waraqa ibn Nawfal, Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl, and Uthman ibn al-Huwayrith) who had abandoned idolatry. Ubaydullah, brother of Zaynab bint Jahsh, accepted Islam quickly, as did Umm Habiba, placing them among the first 15 or 16 Muslims. Facing persecution, the couple migrated to Abyssinia with the second, larger migration, with Umm Habiba pregnant. Her daughter Habiba was born there, giving Ramla the kunya by which she is known in the books of hadith.

In Abyssinia, Umm Habiba dreamt of her husband with a disfigured face, and soon learned that Ubaydullah had reverted to Christianity and fallen into alcohol addiction until his death. Dr. Omar cautions that while this account appears in nearly all books of Sira, its chains are not authenticated, so it should not be asserted with certainty; even as popularly told, it depicts a sad complacency rather than a sincere abandonment of faith. Left a widow in a foreign land with a young daughter, unable to return to Mecca without facing severe persecution, Umm Habiba was stranded.

She then had a second dream in which a caller addressed her as "Mother of the Believers." Shortly after, a proposal arrived: the Prophet ﷺ, having heard of her plight, sent a letter to Al-Najashi (As-hama, the Negus) asking him to act as his representative (wakil) in proposing marriage. Overjoyed, Umm Habiba gave away her jewelry to the servant girl Abraha who delivered the news.

Al-Najashi gathered the Muslims of Abyssinia in his palace for the nikah, praising Allah, testifying to the prophethood of Muhammad ﷺ, and giving a mahr of 400 gold dinars from his own wealth — the largest dowry of any of the Prophet's wives. Khalid ibn Sa'id (ra) served as Umm Habiba's wali. Al-Najashi then insisted on serving a wedding feast (walima), citing the sunnah of the Prophets. Umm Habiba would not actually join the Prophet ﷺ in Medina until seven years after the hijrah — six years after the wedding — and would be with him only three years before his passing.

## Key Moments
- Umm Habiba embraced Islam as likely the second convert from Banu Umayyah, preceding her father Abu Sufyan and her whole family.
- She and her husband Ubaydullah ibn Jahsh joined the second migration to Abyssinia, where she gave birth to her daughter Habiba.
- A troubling dream foreshadowed her husband's reversion to Christianity and reported death in alcohol addiction, leaving her a stranded widow.
- A dream in which she was called "Mother of the Believers" preceded the Prophet's proposal.
- The Prophet ﷺ chose Al-Najashi as his wakil for the marriage — an extraordinary honor — even though Ja'far ibn Abi Talib and other companions were present in Abyssinia.
- Al-Najashi paid a 400 gold dinar mahr from his own wealth and hosted a wedding feast, the most lavish of the Prophet's marriages, held in his absence.
- The servant girl Abraha, who delivered the proposal, became Muslim and asked only that her salam be conveyed to the Prophet ﷺ; years later he replied, "Wa alayha as-salam wa rahmatullahi wa barakatuh."
- When Abu Sufyan heard of the marriage he was pleased, saying the Prophet ﷺ was "a noble steed that cannot be rejected."

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Every companion had a unique struggle; Umm Habiba's was the pain of widowhood and exile while her own father led the opposition to Islam.
- The reported fate of Ubaydullah is a warning to guard one's faith and to value firmness (thabat) over slipping into complacency and addiction.
- Dr. Omar models scholarly caution: highly consequential Sira narrations lacking authenticated chains should not be affirmed with certainty.
- Love for the Prophet ﷺ can flourish across great distance — Al-Najashi and Abraha loved him deeply without ever meeting him.
- The angels still carry our salawat to the Prophet ﷺ, who responds to each one — a privilege available to every believer.
- Allah can turn enemies into allies; Umm Habiba's marriage helped soften Abu Sufyan's heart toward Islam.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Abraha's request: "If you find yourself in the presence of the Prophet ﷺ and you remember me, then give my salam to him, because I'll probably never get to meet him."
- The Prophet's reply on hearing Abraha's salam: "Wa alayha as-salam wa rahmatullahi wa barakatuh."
- Abu Sufyan on the marriage: "That is a noble steed that cannot be rejected."
- Qur'an (60:7), which Ibn Abbas (ra) connected to this marriage: "Perhaps Allah will make friendship between you and those whom you hold as enemies. And Allah has power over all things, and Allah is Most Forgiving, Most Merciful."
