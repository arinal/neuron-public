# Episode 125 — Khalid ibn al-Walid (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 61 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AJW7GB92h_I) · ⏱ 1:10:50

**Who they were:** Khalid ibn al-Walid (ra) was the companion honored by the Prophet ﷺ as "the Sword of Allah" (Sayfullah), regarded as one of the greatest military commanders in history, who fought roughly 100 campaigns and was never defeated.

## Summary
This is the second part of Khalid ibn al-Walid's biography, picking up after his embrace of Islam and his rise at the Battle of Mu'tah, where the Prophet ﷺ first called him "a sword from the swords of Allah." At the Conquest of Mecca (Fatah Mecca), the Prophet ﷺ divided his forces into four columns under Ali, Az-Zubair, Khalid, and Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah (ra). Khalid's battalion was ambushed by his brother-in-law Safwan ibn Umayya and his former best friend Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl; Khalid defeated them, and when the Prophet ﷺ learned the attack was not initiated by Khalid, he said, "Qada Allah khair" (Allah's judgment is better). The Prophet ﷺ then sent Khalid to destroy the idol Al-Uzza — a poetic reversal, since Khalid had once stood beside Abu Sufyan invoking Al-Uzza at Uhud.

The episode emphasizes Khalid's learning curve. His early mistakes came from being too quick to his sword, most notably at Banu Jadhimah, where he attacked a tribe that may have already accepted Islam (and with whom he carried a pre-Islamic grudge for killing his uncle and the father of Abdur-Rahman ibn Auf). The Prophet ﷺ raised his hands and said, "O Allah, I disavow myself of what Khalid has done," sent Ali to make compensation, yet grew Khalid through correction rather than breaking him. The Prophet ﷺ also taught Khalid protective du'as against the jinn and even sent him strands of his hair from the Farewell Hajj — a profound mark of honor that becomes significant later.

Abu Bakr (ra) was the first to appoint Khalid as commander, defending the choice by citing the Prophet's words that Khalid is "a sword of Allah unsheathed against the disbelievers." Khalid crushed the false prophet Musaylimah at the Battle of Yamamah during the Ridda (apostasy) wars, then turned toward the Persian Empire. In the Battle of the Chains (Dhat al-Salasil), he exhausted Hormuz's chained army through optical illusions and constant repositioning, then killed Hormuz personally in a duel, decimating the Persians. Subsequent victories followed at Dhat al-Nahar and the "Battle of the Eyes" at the fort of Anbar, where his archers blinded the enemy.

When the Roman front in Ash-Sham became difficult, Abu Bakr ordered Khalid to march from Iraq to Syria. Guided by Rafi' ibn Umayrah, Khalid chose an unprecedented waterless desert route of over 120 miles, trusting in Allah ("Tawakkalna ala Allah") and devising the tactic of watering camels and later sacrificing them for stored water. He united the Muslim battalions and won at Ajnadayn (30,000 Muslims vs. 90,000 Romans), displaying both military genius — favoring width over depth and meticulous scouting — and a fearless spirituality, walking among the enemy at night protected, as he said, by his dhikr.

During the siege of Damascus, Abu Bakr died and Umar appointed Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah as overall commander, replacing Khalid. Abu Ubaidah withheld the letter until the battle was won, then humbly informed Khalid, who responded, "We hear and we obey." Umar's reason was spiritual, not personal: a dangerous aura of invincibility had grown around Khalid, and Umar wanted the ummah to trust in Allah, not in a man. Khalid served loyally under Abu Ubaidah and helped achieve the decisive Battle of Yarmouk (636 CE). In his final years, unable to fight, Khalid fell in love with the Qur'an, reading it from Fajr to Dhuhr. He died in his bed in Homs around 642 CE, lamenting that a man covered in battle wounds should die "the way a camel dies" — and was reminded that the Sword of Allah would not be broken.

## Key Moments
- At Fatah Mecca, Khalid's column was ambushed by Safwan ibn Umayya and Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl; he defeated them, and the Prophet ﷺ accepted it as Allah's decree.
- The Prophet ﷺ sent Khalid to destroy the real idol Al-Uzza, where he confronted a devil and declared, "O Uzza, we have disbelieved in you... Allah has humiliated you."
- The Banu Jadhimah incident: Khalid's premature attack drew the Prophet's disavowal — "O Allah, I disavow myself of what Khalid has done" — and Ali was sent to compensate.
- The Prophet ﷺ gave Khalid strands of his hair at the Farewell Hajj, which Khalid kept in the front of his helmet — and recklessly searched for it when it fell at Yarmouk.
- Abu Bakr (ra) appointed Khalid to fight the Ridda wars; Khalid defeated Musaylimah al-Kazzab at the Battle of Yamamah.
- The Battle of the Chains: Khalid exhausted the chained Persian army through deception and slew their general Hormuz in single combat.
- The waterless desert march from Iraq to Ash-Sham, where the Muslims found a hidden spring and survived by sacrificing watered camels.
- Umar replaced Khalid with Abu Ubaidah; Khalid responded only with "We hear and we obey," and served loyally beneath him.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Victory comes from Allah, not from any individual; Umar removed Khalid precisely to protect the ummah's faith from believing triumph belonged to a man.
- Mistakes can be a path of growth — the Prophet ﷺ corrected and rectified Khalid's errors without breaking him.
- Converts coming late still required unlearning Jahiliyyah; even great companions had to purify lingering tribal grudges and reckless habits.
- True sincerity is shown in obedience: Khalid accepted demotion without creating fitna, proving his transformation from his pre-Islamic self.
- Genuine trust in Allah (tawakkul) can accompany careful planning — Khalid combined "Tawakkalna ala Allah" with relentless strategy and scouting.
- Dhikr is a believer's shield; Khalid feared no army, saying the dead cannot harm the living who remember Allah.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "Khalidun saifun min suyufillah" — "Khalid is a sword from the swords of Allah, unsheathed against the disbelievers." (the Prophet ﷺ, cited by Abu Bakr)
- "Do not curse my companions, for if one of you were to spend a mountain of Uhud in gold, you would not reach their rank, nor even a handful." (the Prophet ﷺ)
- "I am bringing you a people who love death as much as you love life." (Khalid's letter to Hormuz)
- "By Allah, we did not come out to you because of hunger... we are a people who drink blood, and we have learned there is no blood sweeter than the blood of the Romans." (Khalid to the Roman commander at Ajnadayn)
- "I have witnessed a hundred battles, and there is not a hand's span of my body without a wound from a sword, arrow, or spear — yet now I die on my bed the way a camel dies. May the eyes of the cowards never sleep." (Khalid on his deathbed)
- "Women have become incapable of giving birth to a man like Khalid." (Abu Bakr, ra)
- "The Sword of Allah will not be broken." (a companion comforting Khalid at his death)
