# Episode 130 — Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 56 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=el0mKPtxOVg) · ⏱ 58:05

**Who they were:** Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith (ra) was the Prophet ﷺ's paternal first cousin, foster brother, and childhood companion who became one of his fiercest enemies for some twenty years before embracing Islam near the conquest of Mecca and dying a deeply devoted, repentant worshipper.

## Summary
Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith (not to be confused with the more famous Abu Sufyan ibn Harb) was extraordinarily close to the Prophet ﷺ. His father al-Harith was the eldest son of Abdul-Muttalib — the same al-Harith who helped his father dig the well of Zamzam. Abu Sufyan was the Prophet ﷺ's paternal first cousin from Banu Hashim, his foster brother (both nursed by Halimah), and married into the family of Abu Talib (to Jumana, sister of Ali). He was born around the same year as the Prophet ﷺ and resembled him so strikingly that travelers in Syria recognized him as the Prophet's kin. The two were inseparable friends, exchanging gifts and attending one another's weddings; Abu Sufyan was also known for his eloquence, fine manners, and poetry praising the noble Arabs.

When revelation came, the Prophet ﷺ expected this brother to be among his first supporters. Instead, Abu Sufyan turned completely against him — out of envy, tribal rivalry, and resentment that the Prophet had "disrupted" the comfortable life of Banu Hashim in Mecca. For roughly two decades he attacked the Prophet ﷺ with both tongue and sword, fighting in every battle against the Muslims and becoming the chief poet ridiculing him. This caused the Prophet ﷺ profound personal pain. It was against this Abu Sufyan that Hassan ibn Thabit traded poetry, carefully shredding him while avoiding any insult that would also touch the Prophet ﷺ's own lineage and appearance.

By the time of the conquest of Mecca, all of Abu Sufyan's siblings (including Nawfal and Abdullah) and his own wife had inclined toward or embraced Islam. Realizing the cause was lost, and shamed by his wife Jumana for holding out so long, Abu Sufyan set out with his son Ja'far, his face wrapped for fear of being killed, to meet the Prophet ﷺ. When he uncovered his face, the Prophet ﷺ turned away from him repeatedly. Abu Bakr told him to leave, Umar was harsher, and a young Ansari youth rebuked him as "enemy of Allah." Through the intercession of his uncle al-Abbas — and alongside another repentant cousin, Abdullah ibn Abi Umayyah — he was eventually told he would be forgiven, but the Prophet ﷺ would not pretend the pain never happened.

After the Prophet ﷺ heard the shahada from him and told him to go teach his cousin wudu and salah, the wound began to heal slowly, but the Prophet ﷺ still did not smile at him. The turning point came at the Battle of Hunayn: when the army scattered, Abu Sufyan held the reins of the Prophet ﷺ's mule and fought at his side. The Prophet ﷺ asked who it was and, learning it was his brother, finally embraced him, saying he hoped Allah had given him as a replacement for Hamza, and granted him forgiveness and Paradise.

Reintegrated into Medina as the Prophet ﷺ's honored brother, Abu Sufyan transformed entirely. He fell in love with the Qur'an, became the first to enter the masjid and the last to leave, prayed constantly with tear-filled eyes, kept his gaze on his sandals, and spoke to almost no one. Aisha (ra), who never knew the old history, marveled at him until the Prophet ﷺ identified him as his cousin Abu Sufyan. He maintained this devotion through the caliphate of Abu Bakr and into that of Umar. While on Hajj, a barber's blade struck a cyst on his head, causing a fatal infection — so he is counted among the martyrs. Sensing death, he dug his own grave near the graves of the Prophet ﷺ's family and asked Allah to let him join the Prophet ﷺ and his brother. He died telling his weeping family not to cry, swearing he had not committed a single sin since accepting Islam, and was buried among the closest relatives of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

## Key Moments
- His father al-Harith helped Abdul-Muttalib dig the well of Zamzam — an image Dr. Omar uses to frame the beginning and end of his life.
- Despite being first cousin, foster brother, in-law, and near-lookalike of the Prophet ﷺ, he became his enemy and chief poet against him for about 20 years.
- Hassan ibn Thabit's poetic duels with him required carefully avoiding any insult that would also wound the Prophet ﷺ's lineage and likeness.
- At the conquest of Mecca, shamed by his wife Jumana, he came with his face wrapped to embrace Islam — fearing he might be killed.
- The Prophet ﷺ repeatedly turned away from him; Abu Bakr, Umar, and a young Ansari rebuked him before al-Abbas interceded.
- At the Battle of Hunayn he held the Prophet ﷺ's mule and fought at his side, prompting the long-awaited embrace and the words about replacing Hamza.
- In Medina he became a Qur'an-devoted worshipper — first into the masjid, last out, eyes always lowered.
- Sensing death after a Hajj infection, he dug his own grave near the Prophet ﷺ's family and was buried among the closest relatives.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Even the Prophet ﷺ experienced deep personal hurt and betrayal from one closest to him — a comfort to anyone harmed by family or friends.
- Forgiveness does not require pretending pain never existed; healing can take time, and acknowledgment matters.
- The Prophet ﷺ separated personal harm against himself from harm to the message of Islam, and prioritized the latter.
- Sibling rivalry and envy are an ancient tragedy — from Cain and Abel to Yusuf and his brothers — yet reconciliation carries great reward.
- A late and complicated beginning can still end in sincere devotion and a beautiful death; he sought to make up for every lost year.
- The Prophet ﷺ's mercy and forgiveness extended even to a man who had hurt him for two decades, eventually guaranteeing him Paradise.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ described him to Aisha (ra): "That's my brother Abu Sufyan — the first to enter the masjid and the last to leave it, and his eyes never leave his sandals."
- At Hunayn the Prophet ﷺ welcomed him, expressing hope that Allah had granted him "in place of Hamza" for the pain of Hamza's death.
- On his deathbed: "Do not cry over me. I swear by Allah that I have not sinned since the day I became a Muslim."
- Abu Sufyan's elegy for the Prophet ﷺ: that he spent the whole night weeping over "the greatest disaster to ever hit the face of the Earth," the saddest night in history being the night of the Prophet ﷺ's death.
