# Episode 35 — Asma bint Abi Bakr (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 35 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ZkDUeJdN3VM) · ⏱ 39:24

**Who they were:** Daughter of Abu Bakr as-Siddiq (ra), wife of Az-Zubair ibn al-Awam (ra), and mother of Abdullah ibn az-Zubair and the great scholar Urwa ibn az-Zubair. Known as *Dhat an-Nitaqayn* ("Possessor of Two Waist Belts") and remembered above all for her extraordinary courage.

## Summary
Asma bint Abi Bakr (ra) was born to Abu Bakr as-Siddiq (ra) and Qutayla bint Abdul Uzza, who never embraced Islam. Asma and her full brother Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr were the two children who would play a pivotal role during the Hijrah. Among the earliest converts — listed by Ibn Ishaq as the fifteenth person to accept Islam at the hands of her father — she was about eleven when revelation came. Little is recorded of her Meccan years beyond her devotion. Before the Hijrah she was married in Mecca to Az-Zubair ibn al-Awam (ra), and she was heavily pregnant when the migration took place.

During the Hijrah she carried food in secret to the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr (ra) in the cave of Thawr. Having nothing to tie the provisions, she tore her waist belt in two — for which the Prophet ﷺ gave her the title "Possessor of Two Belts" and promised her two belts in Paradise. When Abu Jahl came searching for the two men and demanded their location, Asma refused to speak; he struck her so violently that her earring fell, yet she would not betray them. Her grandfather Abu Quhafa (who only accepted Islam at the conquest of Mecca) lamented that the Prophet ﷺ had deprived her by leaving her behind, to which she replied that he had in fact left them much of immeasurable value.

Migrating to Quba, she gave birth to Abdullah, her firstborn, whom the Prophet ﷺ blessed. She and Az-Zubair would have eight children, including Abdullah, al-Mundhir, Asim, al-Muhajir, and the scholar Urwa, and daughters named Khadijah, Aisha, and Umm al-Hasan. Her life in Medina was one of relentless poverty and labor: tending Az-Zubair's horse and camel, kneading flour, and carrying date stones two miles on her head from the land the Prophet ﷺ had given him.

Asma was also a warrior, taking up a sword at the Battle of Yarmouk alongside the Muslim fighters against the Byzantines. She narrated numerous hadith — on hijab, ruqya, menstruation and other matters — learning also from her sister Aisha (ra), and she taught the fiqh of Hajj in Mecca for generations of pilgrims.

Her life's most harrowing chapter came at its end. Her son Abdullah ibn az-Zubair had declared the Caliphate in Mecca for over a decade and had rebuilt the Kaaba to the shape Ibrahim (as) originally built it. When the brutal tyrant al-Hajjaj besieged Mecca, the nearly hundred-year-old Asma counselled her son to keep his dignity rather than surrender to humiliation. After his martyrdom and crucifixion by the Kaaba, she confronted al-Hajjaj to his face, rebuking him as a liar and reminding him of the Prophet's ﷺ prophecy of two liars from Thaqif. She died in worship at about a hundred years of age.

## Key Moments
- Accepted Islam very early at the hands of her father Abu Bakr (ra), around the age of eleven.
- Tore her waist belt to bind provisions for the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr (ra) during the Hijrah, earning the title "Possessor of Two Belts" and the promise of two belts in Paradise.
- Stood up to Abu Jahl while pregnant and alone, refusing to reveal the hiding place even after he slapped her so hard her earring fell.
- Answered her grandfather Abu Quhafa that the Prophet ﷺ had "left plenty for us" — that which has no price.
- Lived in severe poverty, carrying date stones on her head; once declined the Prophet's ﷺ offer to ride his camel out of shyness and respect for Az-Zubair's gheerah.
- Fought with a sword at the Battle of Yarmouk against the Byzantines.
- Counselled her son Abdullah ibn az-Zubair before his martyrdom: "A dignified strike with a sword is more beloved than a humiliating lash of a whip."
- Confronted al-Hajjaj after her son's death, defending Abdullah's faith and condemning the tyrant fearlessly.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Courage was the hallmark of this entire family — Asma demonstrated it before Abu Jahl as a vulnerable young woman and before al-Hajjaj as a centenarian.
- True wealth lies in faith, not possessions: the noblest families lived in poverty and did not enrich themselves from the wealth of the Ummah.
- "The greatest jihad is a word of truth before a tyrant" — Asma embodied this twice across her long life.
- Give charity freely without counting: "Do not count, or else Allah will count what He gives to you."
- Maintain kindness and good relations with non-Muslim parents, as the Prophet ﷺ instructed Asma regarding her mother Qutayla.
- Dignity in death means dying upon truth; disgrace is to die upon falsehood, as she taught her son in his final hours.
- The hidden legacy of righteous mothers endures: through her son Urwa ibn az-Zubair, much of the seerah and hadith we possess reaches us.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ to Asma during the Hijrah: "Indeed, Allah has given you in exchange for this two belts in Paradise."
- Asma to Abu Quhafa: "He has left plenty for us" — that which cannot be priced.
- Qur'an: "Then on that Day you will surely be asked about pleasure (na'eem)." (102:8)
- The Prophet ﷺ to Asma: "Give and do not hoard, or else your sustenance will be hoarded," and "Do not count, or else Allah will count what He gives to you."
- Asma to her son Abdullah: "A dignified strike with a sword is more beloved than a humiliating lash of a whip," and "The sheep is not harmed by the skinning after the slaughter."
- Asma confronting al-Hajjaj: "You have lied, O enemy of Allah… the one you killed was engaged in Salah and Siyam… Rasulullah ﷺ said two liars will come from Thaqif, the last worse than the first — and you are him."
- Abdullah ibn Umar (ra), recalling both Abdullah ibn az-Zubair's birth and death amid cries of Allahu Akbar: "Those people were the best of all people; these people are the worst of all people."
