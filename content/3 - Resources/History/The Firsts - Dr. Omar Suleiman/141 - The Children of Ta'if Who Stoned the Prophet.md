# Episode 141 — The Children of Ta'if & Uthman ibn Abi al-As al-Thaqafi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 45 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xhErgn2AvxQ) · ⏱ 20:13

**Who they were:** This episode focuses not on a single companion but on the children of Ta'if — the same youths who, under their elders' orders, stoned the Prophet ﷺ on the worst day of his life — and how the Prophet's mercy and patience bore fruit through them, most notably in the young companion Uthman ibn Abi al-As al-Thaqafi (ra), whom the Prophet ﷺ appointed leader of his people.

## Summary
After being driven out and stoned at Ta'if, the Prophet ﷺ later described it as the worst day of his life. Yet when the angel of the mountains was sent by Allah and offered to crush the people of Ta'if between the two mountains, the Prophet ﷺ refused, saying he hoped that from their offspring there would come people who worship Allah alone and associate no partners with Him. Dr. Omar highlights this as one of the purest expressions of the Prophet's sincerity (ikhlas): his concern was never his own vindication, but that people would come to worship Allah.

That hope was realized. The children who once stoned him largely grew up to become Muslims when the delegation of Ta'if came to Medina after its siege, embraced Islam, and returned to make Ta'if a Muslim city. Most of these former children never met the Prophet ﷺ as believers — he passed away the following year — yet they carried the legacy forward, with descendants becoming scholars, narrators of hadith, and builders of civilization.

Dr. Omar relates a striking narration of a man who, as a child, witnessed the Prophet ﷺ enter Ta'if leaning on a stick or arrow and reciting Qur'an publicly. The recitation was so profound that he memorized it in his days of disbelief and never forgot it, later reciting it in Islam. When the people of Ta'if interrogated him about what he had heard, dismissing the Prophet ﷺ, they could not erase what had taken root in his heart.

The episode then turns to Uthman ibn Abi al-As, the youngest member of the Ta'if delegation. Evidence suggests he had secretly embraced Islam before the delegation arrived, concealing it from his people while quietly learning the Qur'an. Impressed by his sincerity, intellect, and purity of character, the Prophet ﷺ appointed this youth as the leader of his people over the older chiefs — so the youngest man arrived as a follower and left Medina as the leader.

Uthman went on to govern Ta'if, and later Bahrain and Oman during the reigns of Abu Bakr and Umar. He became a fierce warrior who led multiple expeditions against the Persian Empire, settled in Basra, transmitted numerous hadith, and taught there. Dr. Omar concludes that the people who once gave the Prophet ﷺ his hardest day became, by his mercy and du'a, a pipeline for spreading Islam — proof that the fruits of sincere dawah outlast the suffering of those who carry it.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True dawah is about Allah, not personal feelings — the Prophet ﷺ sought the worship of Allah even from those who wronged him most.
- Sincerity (ikhlas) means accepting hardship and humiliation for oneself so long as it produces good for others' guidance.
- Children taught to hate act against their natural fitrah; with patient nurturing there is always hope they can overcome it.
- See past abrasive adults and difficult audiences — "the hope is in the future."
- You never know who is listening; words of truth can take root and resurface years later.
- Youth and sincere character can outweigh age and status — the Prophet ﷺ elevated a young man to leadership over elders.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ to A'ishah, on the worst day of his life: "I encountered your people as they are, and the worst of it was the day of Aqabah."
- The angel of the mountains: "If you wish, I can crush them between the two mountains."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "No, for I hope that from their offspring there will be people who worship Allah and associate no partners with Him."
- The people of Ta'if to the witness: "We know our companion better than you; if we knew him to be what he claims, we would have followed him first."
