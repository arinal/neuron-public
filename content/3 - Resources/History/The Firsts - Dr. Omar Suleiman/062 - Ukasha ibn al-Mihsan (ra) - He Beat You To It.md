# Episode 62 — Ukasha ibn al-Mihsan al-Asadi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 62 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=E4svzfWqGxU) · ⏱ 13:36

**Who they were:** Ukasha ibn al-Mihsan al-Asadi (ra) was one of the earliest Meccan Muslims and a veteran of Badr, famed as the companion whose name became synonymous with being "first" — promised entry into Paradise without questioning or punishment.

## Summary
Ukasha (also pronounced Ukkasha) ibn al-Mihsan al-Asadi (ra) was an ally (halif) of Banu Abd al-Shams and one of the earliest Muslims of Mecca. His name became so associated with precedence that in Arabic poetry to call someone "an Ukasha" is to call them a forerunner, and some scholars held that seeing him in a dream signified that the dreamer would precede others toward good.

He is counted among those given the glad tidings (bishara) of Paradise — and not merely Paradise, but entry without any accountability (hisab) or punishment (azab). The famous narration of Abu Hurayra (ra) recounts the Prophet ﷺ describing a group of 70,000 from his Ummah whose faces would shine like the full moon and who would enter Jannah without reckoning. Ukasha rose and asked the Prophet ﷺ to pray that he be among them, and the Prophet ﷺ replied, "You are among them." When another man stood and made the same request, the Prophet ﷺ answered, "Ukasha has beaten you to it" (Sabaqaka biha Ukasha).

Dr. Omar stresses that this was not luck. Ukasha embraced Islam in his youth alongside his siblings, who were themselves remarkable: his sister Umm Qays bint Mihsan (ra), an early Muslim who lived past a hundred and narrated hadith; his brother Abu Sinan ibn Mihsan (ra), said by one narration to be the first to give the Pledge of Ridwan under the tree at Hudaybiyyah; and two further brothers, Amr ibn Mihsan and Wahb (ra), among the earliest Muslims.

Described as handsome, generous, and ever-smiling, Ukasha gave his all to whatever he undertook. The Prophet ﷺ appointed him early as commander of a battalion of forty men, in which he found great success. At the Battle of Badr his sword broke, and when he came to the Prophet ﷺ asking what to fight with, the Prophet ﷺ handed him a stick saying, "Fight with this." As Ukasha swung it, it became a shining, unmistakable sword in his hand — a miracle of the Prophet ﷺ. The companions called it the sword of aid, and Ukasha fought with it for the rest of his life.

Despite holding the highest guarantees — being a muhajir, a veteran of Badr, and promised Paradise without reckoning — Ukasha never grew complacent. He remained among the first to step forward whenever called. After the Prophet ﷺ passed away and Abu Bakr as-Siddiq (ra) summoned the companions, Ukasha was again among the first to come forward, fighting in the wars against the apostates (Ridda), where he was martyred in the campaign against Musaylima al-Kadhab.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- His glad tidings were earned through a lifetime of sincere effort (sidq al-niyyah), not luck — his actions matched his intention.
- When you ask Allah for something great, live your life in accordance with that request.
- Even with the highest guarantees, Ukasha never grew complacent but remained first to come forward.
- When you hear of a good deed or trait, be the one to lead the way — fill the void in your community and revive forgotten sunnahs.
- Trust and obedience to the Prophet ﷺ (taking the stick without question) brought about divine aid.
