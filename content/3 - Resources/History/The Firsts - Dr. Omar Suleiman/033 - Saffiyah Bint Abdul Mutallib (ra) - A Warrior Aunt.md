# Episode 33 — Safiyyah bint Abdul Muttalib (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 33 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3BDvOfirtSY) · ⏱ 34:35

**Who they were:** Safiyyah bint Abdul Muttalib (ra) was the paternal aunt of the Prophet ﷺ, the only one of his aunts known with certainty to have embraced Islam and feature prominently in the seerah. She was the devoted single mother of az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra) and a remarkably courageous warrior-woman.

## Summary
Dr. Omar opens by noting that this episode is an outlier in the series, since Safiyyah was not among the very first to accept Islam — but her son az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra) was, and his heroic character cannot be understood without first appreciating his extraordinary mother. He surveys the family of the Prophet ﷺ, listing his roughly twelve uncles (including Abu Talib, Hamza, al-Abbas, and Abu Lahab) and his six aunts (Safiyyah, Umm Hakim, Atika, Umayma, Arwa, and others). Of all the uncles and aunts, only Hamza, al-Abbas, and Safiyyah are known with certainty to have embraced Islam.

Safiyyah was born around 569–570 CE, the same age or a year older than the Prophet ﷺ, and grew up alongside him almost as a sister. She was especially close to her brother Hamza (ra), born within a year of her. Her marriages also bound her closer to the Prophet ﷺ: she first married al-Harith ibn Harb (brother of Abu Sufyan), then al-Awwam ibn Khuwaylid, the brother of Khadijah (ra), making her Khadijah's sister-in-law. With al-Awwam she had three sons — az-Zubayr, as-Sa'ib, and Abdul-Ka'ba (who died young).

When al-Awwam died, Safiyyah chose never to remarry and raised her sons as a single mother. Fearing her orphaned son az-Zubayr would be bullied or disadvantaged in that harsh society, she raised him to be strong, intelligent, and courageous — taking him out to learn the arts of war and enlisting his uncle Nawfal ibn Khuwaylid. When Nawfal accused her of disciplining az-Zubayr as if she hated him, she replied in poetry that she was rough with him only so he would grow strong, capable, and one day defeat armies and return victorious.

Safiyyah embraced Islam on the very day Hamza (ra) became Muslim — a turning point in the seerah. The young az-Zubayr accepted Islam at age 13, and Safiyyah at first resisted, even asking Nawfal to punish him, out of misguided fear for her orphaned son's safety rather than hatred for the Prophet ﷺ. When revelation came to warn his closest relatives, the Prophet ﷺ called out by name to Fatima and Safiyyah bint Abdul Muttalib, telling them he could give them of his wealth but could not protect them from Allah on the Day of Judgment.

Two episodes reveal her courage most. At Uhud, she advanced toward the battlefield with a spear, condemning the men for fleeing from the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet ﷺ sent az-Zubayr to stop her so she would not see the mutilated body of Hamza. She obeyed only when told the command came from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ — "I hear and obey" — then stood over Hamza's body deep into the night, repeating *inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'oon*, seeking forgiveness and reward for her loss. At the Battle of the Trench, while sheltering with the women, children, and elderly, she single-handedly killed an attacking man from Banu Qurayza with a pole and threw his body down, making the enemy believe an army defended the fortress. The Prophet ﷺ said, "That is Safiyyah" — a woman like no other. She died during the caliphate of Umar (ra), who prayed her funeral, and was buried in al-Baqi'.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A parent's character and tarbiyah leave a deep imprint — az-Zubayr's strength and courage flowed directly from his mother.
- Even sincere love can be misguided; Safiyyah (like other mothers) initially opposed Islam out of fear for her child, yet Allah commanded obedience to parents except in disobedience to Him.
- True taqwa is shown in immediate submission: Safiyyah halted instantly once she knew the command came from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.
- Grief can be borne with faith — patience, *inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'oon*, seeking forgiveness for the deceased, and entrusting one's reward to Allah.
- Courage and contribution are not limited by age or gender; Safiyyah defended the community when others froze.
- No one, however beloved, can shield another from accountability before Allah — only one's own faith and deeds avail.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ, when warning his relatives: "Ya Fatima... Ya Safiyyah... ask me whatever you want from my wealth, but I cannot avail you anything before Allah."
- Safiyyah's poetry: "Whoever claims that I hate my own child has lied; I discipline him only so he may grow strong... and one day defeat armies and return with the spoils."
- Safiyyah at Uhud, to the fleeing men: "Did you flee from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ?" — and upon being commanded to stop: "Sam'an wa ta'a" (I hear and obey).
- Over Hamza's body: "Inna lillahi wa inna ilayhi raji'oon... Allahumma ighfir lahu... Allahumma inni ahtasibuhu wa indak" (O Allah, I seek his reward with You).
- The Prophet ﷺ on hearing of her feat at the Trench: "Innaha Safiyyah" — that is Safiyyah.
