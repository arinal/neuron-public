# Episode 66 — Rayta bint Al-Harith ibn Jabalah (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 66 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K8S2LlulMRQ) · ⏱ 10:10

**Who they were:** Rayta bint Al-Harith ibn Jabalah (ra) was among the first 40–50 people to embrace Islam, an early emigrant to Abyssinia, who died of poisoning together with three of her children on the road from Abyssinia to Medina — counted among the martyrs (shuhada).

## Summary
Rayta bint Al-Harith ibn Jabalah (ra) and her husband Al-Harith ibn Khalid embraced Islam very early, numbering among the first 40 or 50 companions of the Prophet ﷺ. Dr. Omar notes that her name was common in the era — shared by Rayta bint Abi Talib (ra), the sister of Ali (ra) — and clarifies the tangle of similar names: she is not the sister of Subai'a, though she had a brother named Subai'a ibn al-Harith who also embraced Islam. Like many early Muslims, Rayta and her husband were a young, vulnerable couple, not from powerful tribes, and so were among those who migrated to Abyssinia under the leadership of Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (ra).

Childless when they left Mecca, the couple had four children while living in Abyssinia: Musa, Zainab, Aisha, and Fatima. When news of the hijrah to Medina reached them, they were among those who immediately set out from Abyssinia to join the Muslim community there.

The journey, undertaken with little provision, carried serious risk. As they neared Medina, the family drank from some water and went to sleep. Al-Harith ibn Khalid awoke to find his wife Rayta (ra) and three of his children — Musa, Zainab, and Aisha — all dead from the tainted water. He entered Medina having left Abyssinia with a wife and four children, arriving with only his daughter Fatima.

Dr. Omar reflects on the immense status of those who died "from hijrah to hijrah" — not merely on one migration, but between two. He connects their story to others like Khalid ibn Hizam (ra), the younger brother of Hakim ibn Hizam (ra) and nephew of Khadijah (ra), who was bitten by a snake and died while on his way to make hijrah. For all such people, Allah revealed the assurance that their reward is guaranteed by Him, even if death overtook them before reaching their destination.

## Key Moments
- Rayta (ra) and her husband Al-Harith ibn Khalid were among the first 40–50 to embrace Islam.
- The couple emigrated to Abyssinia under the leadership of Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (ra).
- All four of their children — Musa, Zainab, Aisha, and Fatima — were born in Abyssinia.
- Upon hearing of the hijrah to Medina, they immediately set out to join the companions.
- Near Medina, the family drank tainted water; Al-Harith awoke to find his wife and three children dead.
- He arrived in Medina with only his surviving daughter, Fatima.
- The story of Khalid ibn Hizam (ra), who died of a snakebite en route to hijrah, illustrates the same fate of dying on the path of migration.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A sincere intention to migrate or strive for Allah's sake secures its reward even if death intervenes before the goal is reached.
- Those who died "from hijrah to hijrah" hold an immense, almost inexpressible status with Allah.
- Tragic, sudden death — a recurring grief in the COVID era — can be reframed through the lens of these companions' martyrdom and reward.
- Many companions whose names are now obscure will stand high in Allah's sight; obscurity to people is no measure of rank with Allah.
- We should hold sincere intentions and trust that Allah forgives our shortcomings, whether from personal deficiency or circumstances beyond our control.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "And whoever leaves their home as a migrant to Allah and His Messenger, then death overtakes them — their reward is surely incumbent upon Allah, and Allah is ever Forgiving, Most Merciful." (Qur'an 4:100)
