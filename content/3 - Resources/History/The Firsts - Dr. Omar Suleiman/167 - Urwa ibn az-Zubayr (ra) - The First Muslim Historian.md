# Episode 167 — Urwa ibn az-Zubayr (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 19 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uCZtTBd1r_8) · ⏱ 1:06:47

**Who they were:** Son of az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra) and Asma bint Abi Bakr (ra), and nephew of Aisha (ra). A Tabi'i (not a companion), Urwa is remembered as the pioneer of Islamic history and the foremost early compiler of the Sirah — the bridge between the companions and the rest of the ummah.

## Summary
Urwa ibn az-Zubayr came from a legendary household. His father az-Zubayr named all of his children after martyrs (Abdullah, Mus'ab, al-Mundhir, and others), and Urwa grew up amid the chaos of the second fitna. His earliest memory was witnessing his heroic brother Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr under siege; when his brother slew an attacker, the young Urwa cried out in pride and was nearly killed himself. Born around 23 AH, he was too young to fight at battles like al-Jamal and consciously chose the path of scholarship rather than the sword that defined his warrior brother.

Dr. Omar frames Urwa against the political backdrop of the era: the death of Yazid, the massacre of Medina, the rise of Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr's caliphate over nearly the whole Muslim world (except Sham, held by the Umayyads), and the eventual clash between Abdullah and Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan. A famous narration recalls four youths — Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr, Mus'ab, Abd al-Malik, and Urwa — each declaring their lofty aspiration; Urwa said he sought only knowledge and to be away from conflict, and each got what he aimed for.

Urwa's defining advantage was his free access to the house of his aunt Aisha (ra). While others had to seek permission and sit behind a curtain, Urwa would enter freely and ask her about everything that happened between her and the Prophet ﷺ — even being shown the exact spot where the Prophet ﷺ would pray and sleep. From these intimate conversations he absorbed the bulk of the Sirah, memorizing and writing relentlessly. Four years before Aisha's death he was satisfied he had captured every hadith she possessed. He also married strategically to access more knowledge and learned from countless companions, becoming, in the words of az-Zuhri, "an ocean without bounds."

After the wars ended, Abd al-Malik ibn Marwan sought reconciliation with the family of az-Zubayr, returning to Urwa his father's sword (marked from Badr and Uhud). Urwa took no governing post but became the scholar Abd al-Malik wrote to with questions; these letters form the earliest written manuscripts of the Sirah, making Urwa the first to compile and write the biography of the Prophet ﷺ. He famously remarked that a word of humiliation he bore with patience may have earned him lasting honor with Allah.

His worship was extraordinary: he recited a quarter of the Quran daily from the mushaf by day and a quarter from memory by night, never abandoning this habit, and fasted every day except the two Eids. He gave generous charity, inviting the poor into his gardens to take freely — the opposite of the condemned garden-owners in the Quran. On a journey to Sham his leg developed gangrene; refusing wine as an anesthetic, he asked them to amputate it while he was in sujud, and barely made a sound. In the same journey his son was kicked and killed by a horse. Urwa responded with profound patience, thanking Allah that of four limbs and four sons He took only one of each. He spent his final years in Wadi al-Aqiq away from the fitna, and died fasting in 94 AH — "the year of the jurists."

## Key Moments
- As a child, witnessing his brother Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr kill an attacker during a siege, and crying out in pride — for which he was stripped and nearly beaten before they realized he was a child.
- The narration of four young men declaring their aspirations, each ultimately attaining what he sought — Urwa choosing knowledge.
- His free entry into Aisha's (ra) home, from which he gathered the intimate details of the Prophet's ﷺ life and most of the Sirah.
- The painful episode when Aisha vowed never to speak to Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr after his comment about her finances, eventually freeing 40 slaves to expiate her oath after reconciliation.
- Abd al-Malik returning az-Zubayr's sword to Urwa, who identified its marks from Badr and Uhud.
- His written correspondence with Abd al-Malik becoming the earliest manuscripts of the Sirah.
- The amputation of his leg during prayer in sujud, without anesthesia, and learning of his son's death on the same journey.
- His final day: fasting and choosing to wait to break his fast in Paradise, dying on a Friday in 94 AH.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Seek knowledge while young: "One day you will be the elders of your people — and what good is an ignorant old man?" Don't postpone your Islamic goals to old age.
- Aim high. The early generations dreamed of great things in their youth and pursued them, achieving what they set out to attain.
- There is honor in choosing the background role: Urwa's patience and dedication to scholarship preserved the Sirah for the entire ummah, a contribution greater than the sword.
- Patience and gratitude reframe calamity — gratitude for what remains rather than grief over what is lost.
- "Good deeds have siblings and sins have siblings": one righteous act draws others, as does one evil act.
- Generosity modeled on opening one's blessings to the poor, the opposite of hoarding wealth.
