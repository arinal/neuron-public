# Episode 46 — Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra): 10 Unique Virtues

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 46 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BI-LQkkDflA) · ⏱ 1:21:13

**Who they were:** Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), the second Caliph of Islam, titled al-Farooq ("the one who distinguishes truth from falsehood"). Though always ranked beneath Abu Bakr as-Siddiq (ra) in virtue, he is celebrated for a uniquely deep, just, and transformative leadership of the Muslim community.

## Summary
This second installment on Umar (ra) sets aside the chronology of his life to focus on ten distinguishing virtues, some unique to him and others in which he simply excelled. Dr. Omar frames Umar as an answer to the Prophet ﷺ's supplication and as a man whom Allah customized for the service of Islam, a single individual from whom an immense amount of good (khair) flowed.

The first virtues concern his nearness to truth and revelation. Umar (ra) was *muhaddath* — divinely inspired, as the Prophet ﷺ said angels would "speak to" such people. His sound intuition was the fruit of his sincerity and struggle against his own *nafs*. This is illustrated by his famous honesty — telling the Prophet ﷺ he did not yet love him more than himself, then returning the next day having corrected it ("Now, O Umar"). The Quran repeatedly descended in agreement with his opinions: the prohibition of alcohol, the verses of hijab, the captives of Badr, taking the Station of Ibrahim as a place of prayer, and not praying over the hypocrite Abdullah ibn Ubayy. He also pressed Abu Bakr to gather the Quran into a written mushaf.

The Prophet ﷺ saw more dreams about Umar than any other companion: the dream of garments representing religious commitment (Umar's dragged the ground), the cup of milk overflowing through the Prophet's fingers (interpreted as knowledge), the well from which Umar drew water until everyone and even the animals drank their fill, and the palace in Jannah from which the Prophet ﷺ turned away out of respect for Umar's sense of honor (*ghira*) — a story that moved Umar to tears at the thought of ever feeling jealousy toward the Prophet ﷺ.

Further virtues include Shaytan fleeing from him (because he was constantly in *dhikr*, never giving Shaytan room to whisper), his intense worship and tearful night prayer (Aisha said none prayed the night like him; he had "two tunnels" worn beneath his eyes from weeping), and his unmatched justice (*adl*) — accepting correction from a woman on the question of dowry, from Salman al-Farisi over a garment, from the judge Shurayh over a horse, and enforcing the law even against the son of the governor Amr ibn al-As and his own son Abdurrahman.

Dr. Omar then highlights Umar's mercy — surprising given his reputation for sternness — captured in his dua: "O Allah, I am weak, make me strong; I am harsh, make me lenient; I am stingy, make me generous." His humility (welcoming anyone who showed him his faults; shrinking whenever told *ittaqillah*, "fear Allah"), his asceticism (*zuhd*) and warnings against impulsive indulgence, and finally his profound self-accountability complete the ten. He coined the maxim "Hold yourselves accountable before you are held accountable," feared he might be the one excluded from Jannah, and even asked Hudhayfah whether he was among the hypocrites. The episode closes with Uthman's (ra) testimony that Umar's private self was even better than his public reputation.

## Key Moments
- The Prophet ﷺ declared, "If there were to be a prophet after me, it would be Umar," and that nations before had *muhaddath* (divinely-spoken-to) people — and Umar was such a one in this ummah.
- Umar's honest admission that he did not yet love the Prophet ﷺ more than himself, and the Prophet's affirmation "Now, O Umar" after Umar corrected it.
- The Quran descending to agree with Umar's opinions on alcohol, hijab, the captives of Badr, the Station of Ibrahim, and not praying over Abdullah ibn Ubayy.
- The Prophet ﷺ's dreams of Umar: the long garment (religion), milk overflowing (knowledge), the well, and the palace in Jannah he turned away from out of respect for Umar's *ghira*.
- The Prophet ﷺ telling Umar that Shaytan would take a different path rather than walk the same road as him.
- His justice: ruling against himself before the judge Shurayh over a broken-legged horse, then appointing Shurayh as a judge; enforcing retribution against the son of Amr ibn al-As; and publicly flogging his own son Abdurrahman.
- His asceticism shown in rebuking Jabir over a large bag of meat, and his refusal to consume all his good in this world.
- Uthman's (ra) testimony to Abu Bakr that Umar's private self was even better than what people saw publicly.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Sincerity purifies intuition: the more truthful one is in seeking Allah, the more one's instincts align with what is right — though intuition never overrides revelation.
- Constant remembrance of Allah (*dhikr*) is a fortress; if Shaytan finds no opening, he cannot even whisper.
- Do not claim "the stick of Umar" without "the tahajjud of Umar" — his harshness on others never exceeded his harshness on himself, and was free of ego.
- True strength is controlling oneself in anger, fulfilling trusts, and guarding people's honor — not having the loudest voice.
- Mercy and justice belong together; relaxing punishment in the presence of doubt is more beloved than punishing while uncertain.
- Self-awareness and welcoming correction are essential to spiritual growth; hold yourself accountable now, while it is easier.
- Balance hope and fear: fear that you could be the one who falls short, yet hope that Allah's mercy could single you out.

- Hurmuzan to Umar: "You ruled, so you were just; you were just, so you were secure; you were secure, so you slept."
- Uthman (ra): "By Allah, what is hidden of him is better than what is seen, and there is none among us like him."
