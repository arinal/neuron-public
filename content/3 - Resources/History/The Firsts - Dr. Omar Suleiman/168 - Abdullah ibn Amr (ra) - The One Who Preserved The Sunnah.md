# Episode 168 — Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 18 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AyG8dwLxMgY) · ⏱ 1:26:09

**Who they were:** The son of Amr ibn al-As (ra), Abdullah ibn Amr was the young companion who systematically wrote down the sayings of the Prophet ﷺ — compiling hundreds of hadith in his famous book *al-Sahifa al-Sadiqa* ("The Truthful Tablet") — and is remembered as one of the great preservers of the Sunnah.

## Summary
Abdullah ibn Amr (ra) was the fourth of the four young "Abdullahs" who effectively documented the religion for later generations. Because the Arabic spelling closely resembles that of Abdullah ibn Umar, he is often confused with him in translations, leaving him somewhat in the background despite an outsized contribution. He was born when his father Amr ibn al-As was only about eleven years old, so father and son were nearly of the same generation; Abdullah accompanied his father in nearly everything and embraced Islam roughly a year before both his parents, while still in his late twenties.

He was born into the proud, intelligent and wealthy clan of Banu Sahm. His birth name was al-As — named after his grandfather al-As ibn Wa'il, one of the most condemned mockers of the Prophet ﷺ, who had jeered that Muhammad ﷺ would be forgotten because he had no sons. In a striking reversal, it was al-As's grandson, renamed Abdullah ("servant of Allah") by the Prophet ﷺ, who would extend the mention of the Prophet ﷺ across centuries by preserving his words. Abdullah came into Islam carrying tribal arrogance and luxury he had to unlearn; the Prophet ﷺ even told him to burn his fancy saffron-dyed garments.

Feeling far behind his peers, Abdullah threw himself into the religion. He memorized the Qur'an in his first year, sat at the feet of the most knowledgeable companions — especially Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, from whom the Prophet ﷺ instructed people to take the Qur'an — and memorized over a thousand sayings of the Prophet ﷺ. Crucially, after seeking and receiving the Prophet's permission, he wrote down everything he heard, even when some of Quraysh discouraged him; the Prophet ﷺ reassured him that nothing but truth came from his mouth. The result was *al-Sahifa al-Sadiqa*, containing over 700 hadith recorded directly from the Prophet ﷺ. He also studied the previous scriptures (Torah) to understand the prophecies of the coming Prophet.

His defining personal struggle was overzealousness — going too far, too fast. He prayed all night, fasted every day, and on his wedding night recited the entire Qur'an in his two rak'ahs. The Prophet ﷺ repeatedly counseled moderation: fast like Dawud (alternate days), complete the Qur'an in no fewer than three days, and honor the rights of one's body, family, wife, and guests. Abdullah later lamented, "I wish I had taken the concessions of the Prophet ﷺ," realizing zeal cannot be sustained without balance.

A rare combination of scholar and warrior, Abdullah fought in many battles alongside his father — Yarmuk, the conquests of Syria, Palestine, and Egypt — often as a commander, and documented the campaigns even as he participated. He settled in Egypt (Fustat) where he taught extensively; his narrations, though largely transmitted away from Medina, were preserved and absorbed especially into Musnad Ahmad as well as Bukhari and Muslim. He hated fitna and avoided the civil strife, yet because the Prophet ﷺ commanded him to obey his father as long as he lived, he came out to the Battle of Siffin on his father's side — though he refused to throw a single arrow or raise his sword.

## Key Moments
- The Prophet ﷺ changed his name from al-As to Abdullah and praised his household: Abdullah, his father Amr, and his mother.
- He asked permission to write the Prophet's words; reassured by the Prophet ﷺ, he produced *al-Sahifa al-Sadiqa* with over 700 hadith, including the prophecy that Constantinople would be conquered before Rome.
- On his wedding night he recited the entire Qur'an in his two rak'ahs, prompting his wife to gently complain and the Prophet ﷺ to counsel moderation.
- The Prophet ﷺ prescribed the fast of Dawud and completing the Qur'an in no fewer than three days, telling him not to be "like the one who used to pray and then stopped."
- The story of the man repeatedly named by the Prophet ﷺ as "of the people of Jannah" — whose secret was sleeping each night with a heart free of malice and envy toward fellow Muslims.
- He patiently walked with a young man of Quraysh struggling with alcohol addiction, conveying both warning and hope of repentance.
- At Siffin he obeyed his father by attending but refused to fight; when Ammar ibn Yasir was killed he cried out that they were the transgressing group the Prophet ﷺ had foretold.
- His painful estrangement from and eventual reconciliation with al-Husayn (ra), who had asked him, "Why did you fight against my father?"

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Enter the religion with ease and grow gradually; overzealousness leads to burnout, and "do not make yourself hate the worship of Allah."
- Allah may guide the enemies of the truth or their children — every major opponent of the Prophet ﷺ became, through guidance, a means for Islam's spread.
- Internalizing the deen — purifying the heart of malice and envy — can outweigh extensive outward acts of worship.
- Worship must honor a balance of rights: your Lord, your body, your family, your spouse, and your guests all have a claim upon you.
- Preserving and holding fast to the authentic Sunnah is a sacred trust against those who seek to undermine it.
- Forgiveness can be complicated and slow even among the best of people; reconciliation often requires a sincere mediator and time.
- Obedience to parents and hatred of fitna shaped his choices, showing that good people can be caught in conflict while still seeking to do right.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ (pointing to his mouth): "Write, for by the One in whose Hand is my soul, nothing comes out of it except the truth."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever seeks a way other than my way is not from me" — after the three men vowed to pray all night, fast every day, and never marry.
- The man of Jannah: "I sleep with no malice in my heart toward any Muslim, and I envy no one for what Allah has given them."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Verily Allah does not take away knowledge by snatching it from people, but He takes it away with the death of the scholars" — confirmed by Abdullah to Aisha (ra).
- The Prophet ﷺ: "The Muslim is the one from whom other Muslims are safe regarding their tongue and hand; and the muhajir is the one who abandons what Allah has forbidden."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Obey your father as long as you live."
- His dying father Amr (ra) said the two whom the Prophet ﷺ loved most were Ibn Mas'ud and Ammar; and Abdullah was instructed to remain by his father's grave "for the time it takes to slaughter and distribute a camel."
