# Episode 74 — Usayd ibn Hudayr ibn Simak (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 74 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=71lD9mOgiM8) · ⏱ 49:01

**Who they were:** Usayd ibn Hudayr (ra), known as Abu Yahya, was one of the noble young leaders of the Ansar in Medina — the spokesperson (khatib) of the Ansar — and a man transformed by the Qur'an, famed for the beauty of his recitation and his deep love of God's Book.

## Summary
Usayd ibn Hudayr (ra) was the son of Al-Hudayr, the chief of the Aws tribe who was killed on the Day of Bu'ath, the devastating civil war between the Aws and Khazraj that wiped out Yathrib's elders and left young men in their twenties and thirties as leaders. Dr. Omar places Usayd within a group of five young men — none older than 32 — who, together with Mus'ab ibn Umayr, As'ad ibn Zurarah, Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, and Sa'd ibn Ubadah, set the entire stage for the Medina the Muslims would come to love.

Usayd was an exceptional personality: literate, eloquent, fluent in poetry, gifted with a sweet and beautiful voice, and known for his sharp intellect. He was also light-hearted and quick to joke, skilled at disarming hostility, handsome, and renowned as the most accurate spear-thrower in Yathrib. His conversion came when Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, hearing that his cousin As'ad ibn Zurarah was sitting with the Meccan teacher Mus'ab ibn Umayr "confusing the youth," sent Usayd with his spear to break it up. Mus'ab calmly invited him to simply sit and listen, offering to leave forever if Usayd disliked what he heard. Mus'ab recited Qur'an, and Usayd was overcome with emotion, declaring how noble and beautiful the words were, then immediately asking how to join. He performed ghusl, took the shahada, and prayed two rak'ahs.

Sa'd ibn Mu'adh recognized that Usayd had returned "with a face different than the one he left with" — echoing what Abu Jahl said of Utbah ibn Rabi'ah in Mecca, except that Usayd's heart embraced the truth while the Meccans' arrogance veiled them. Usayd's conversion opened the door: then Sa'd ibn Mu'adh embraced Islam, then Sa'd ibn Ubadah, then much of Medina. Usayd attended the Second Pledge of Aqaba and was appointed by the Prophet ﷺ as one of the twelve naqibs (chiefs), and upon the Hijrah was paired in brotherhood with Zayd ibn Harithah (ra).

Usayd was distinguished by worship and presence. People — and even the angels — gathered to hear his recitation; one night so many angels descended as he recited Surat al-Baqarah that his horse nearly trampled his son Yahya. He missed Badr and deeply regretted it, but proved his truthfulness at Uhud, where he stayed by the Prophet ﷺ and received seven major wounds. He was praised by the Prophet ﷺ ("What a man Usayd ibn Hudayr is"), by Abu Bakr, and by Aisha (ra), who named him among the three best of the Ansar.

Usayd's eloquence and diplomacy repeatedly calmed crises. He comforted Aisha and Abu Bakr after the necklace incident that brought the gift of tayammum; he reassured the Prophet ﷺ against the words of the hypocrite Abdullah ibn Ubayy; and most critically, at the Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah after the Prophet's death, his words — "the Prophet ﷺ was from the Muhajirun, so his khalifa will be from the Muhajirun, and we will be the Ansar of his khalifa as we were his Ansar" — united the community behind Abu Bakr. He died in Sha'ban, 20 years after the Hijrah, in debt of 4,000 dirhams; Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) led his janazah, wept for him, and personally sold his own possessions over three years to pay off the debt.

## Key Moments
- His father Al-Hudayr was chief of the Aws and was killed on the Day of Bu'ath.
- Sent by Sa'd ibn Mu'adh with his spear to drive off Mus'ab ibn Umayr, but instead sat, listened to the Qur'an, and embraced Islam on the spot.
- His conversion triggered a chain: Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, then Sa'd ibn Ubadah, then much of Medina entered Islam.
- Appointed one of the twelve naqibs at the Second Pledge of Aqaba; paired in brotherhood with Zayd ibn Harithah after the Hijrah.
- Angels descended as he recited Surat al-Baqarah at night, nearly causing his horse to trample his son Yahya.
- Missed Badr and regretted it deeply, then suffered seven wounds standing firm at Uhud.
- The Prophet ﷺ poked him while he joked before battle; when Usayd lightheartedly demanded "retaliation," the Prophet ﷺ lifted his shirt, and Usayd hugged and kissed his ribs.
- His decisive speech at the Saqifah of Banu Sa'idah united the Ansar behind the leadership of Abu Bakr (ra).

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Qur'an itself transforms hearts — Usayd came as a hostile enemy and left a believer simply by listening with presence and an open heart.
- Arrogance is a veil: the Meccans knew the Prophet ﷺ for 40 years yet rejected him, while the Ansar loved him before even meeting him, because their hearts were humble.
- True presence (ihsan) in worship: Usayd said he knew he was of the people of Jannah in three states — reciting/listening to the Qur'an, hearing the Prophet's khutbah, and praying over the deceased while imagining himself in their place.
- Truthfulness is proven by action — having sincerely regretted missing Badr, Usayd proved it by enduring seven wounds at Uhud.
- The seeds of fitna are always dormant; even these righteous men nearly came to blows during the slander incident — Shaytan could not separate them from the Qur'an, but could busy them with division.
- Selflessness over status: Usayd never sought position or wealth, died in debt, and used his eloquence to unite the ummah rather than claim power.
- Genuine brotherhood: Umar (ra) personally sold his possessions over three years to clear Usayd's debt rather than draw from the treasury.
