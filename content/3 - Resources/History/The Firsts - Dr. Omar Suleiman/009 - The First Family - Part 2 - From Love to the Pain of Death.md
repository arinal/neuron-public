# Episode 9 — Fatima bint Muhammad and Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 9 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zSa4kKwo_cs) · ⏱ 1:00:54

**Who they were:** Fatima (ra) was the beloved daughter of the Prophet ﷺ and his wife Khadija (ra), married to his cousin and ward Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra); together they formed the household (Ahl al-Bayt) and were parents of al-Hasan, al-Husayn, Zaynab, and Umm Kulthum (ra).

## Summary
This episode continues the story of the first family, tracing the marriage of Ali and Fatima (ra) from love through hardship to the pain of death. When the two asked the Prophet ﷺ which of them he loved more, he answered with perfect eloquence: "She is more beloved to me than you, but you are more precious to me than her" — an answer in which both could take pride. Their first difficulty was not poverty but the distance of their new home from the Prophet ﷺ. The companion Haritha ibn al-Nu'man, on whose land the Prophet's apartments stood, gladly gave land directly across from Aisha's quarters so Fatima could live close to her father, prompting the Prophet ﷺ to pray that Allah grant him a palace in Paradise.

Their poverty was severe. Ali developed back pain and Fatima's hands blistered from grinding and household labor, so she went to ask the Prophet ﷺ for a servant. Instead of a maid, he taught them the tasbih before sleep — SubhanAllah 33 times, Alhamdulillah 33 times, and Allahu Akbar 34 times — explaining he could not provide a servant while the people of Suffah and the orphans of Badr went without. Ali (ra) testified he never missed reciting it afterward and never again felt the need. Despite hardship, the family was famously generous; many scholars link the praise in Surat al-Insan (76:8–9) to their giving away their only food to a needy family.

The episode recounts moments of humanity: Ali once failed to wake for qiyam and answered that if Allah willed He would return their souls, prompting the Prophet ﷺ to recite "Man is ever argumentative." After a marital dispute, the Prophet ﷺ found Ali sleeping in the mosque covered in dust, gently brushed him off, and called him "Abu Turab" (father of dust), diffusing the situation with affection rather than anger. After the conquest of Mecca, when a proposal arose for Ali to also marry the daughter of Abu Jahl, the Prophet ﷺ refused to forbid the halal but declared, "Fatima is a part of me; what hurts her hurts me," honoring her grief without injustice.

Their love deepened over ten years, expressed even in Ali's tender love poetry to Fatima. They had four surviving children: al-Hasan (born during Uhud), al-Husayn, Zaynab, and Umm Kulthum, plus al-Muhassin who died at birth. The Prophet ﷺ named Hasan and Husayn himself, rejecting Ali's choice of "Harb" (war), and showered them with love — prolonging his prostration while Hasan climbed his back, gathering all four under his cloak, and praying for them constantly.

The final section captures loss. In his last illness, the Prophet ﷺ summoned his strength to greet Fatima, whispered to her that he would not survive (making her weep), then whispered that she would be the first of his family to follow him (making her smile). When he passed, Aisha's scream reached Fatima next door, and she cried out beautiful words about her father's nearness to his Lord and Paradise. Ali fell silent for days. Fatima fell ill almost immediately and died the third day of the first Ramadan after the Prophet ﷺ — only 27 years old — having arranged her modest night burial and asked Ali to marry Umama to care for the children. Ali led her funeral, buried her, and recited a poem of raw, unfiltered grief, an imagined conversation between himself and Fatima at her grave.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ modeled fairness and emotional intelligence — never belittling Ali, never letting his deep love for Fatima become injustice toward others.
- Remembrance of Allah (dhikr) brings strength and sufficiency greater than worldly help; spiritual provision outweighs material relief.
- True wealth in a household is love and presence; what we give our children of ourselves outlasts any material gift.
- Patience (sabr) does not mean feeling no pain — Fatima wept, Ali grieved openly — but saying only what pleases Allah and channeling pain into good.
- Leaders must care for the most vulnerable first; the Prophet ﷺ would not privilege his own family over the orphans and homeless.
- We are gathered with those we love: "You are with the one you love."
