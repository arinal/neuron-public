# Episode 139 — Abu Bakra (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 47 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ueQpEwW13BQ) · ⏱ 28:27

**Who they were:** A former slave from Ta'if who freed himself by escaping the besieged fortress, became "the freed slave of Allah and His Messenger ﷺ," and went on to be a prolific narrator of over 100 hadith and a founding scholar of Basra.

## Summary
In the 8th year after Hijra, after the conquest of Mecca and the defeat of the Hawazin tribes, the Prophet ﷺ laid siege to the mountainous city of Ta'if. Rather than launching a full-scale assault on the difficult terrain, he chose to withdraw to Medina — but first he announced that any enslaved person who left the forts of Ta'if and joined the Muslims would be free. Around 23 slaves seized this opportunity. Among them was a man whose dramatic escape would define his name.

This companion, originally named Nufay', lowered himself out of the fortress clinging to the wheel-and-pulley (bakra) used to draw water from a well. From that day he was known as **Abu Bakra** — a name so close to Abu Bakr that, to this day, readers of Bukhari and Muslim frequently confuse the two narrators. His mother, Sumayya, was an Abyssinian woman, and his master was named Harith. Though some attributed him to his master as "son of Harith," Abu Bakra refused this. He insisted: "I am Abu Bakra, the freed slave of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ" — freed both from slavery and from shirk.

When the delegation of Ta'if later accepted Islam, his former master came demanding him back. The Prophet ﷺ refused, declaring him "the freed slave of Allah and the freed slave of His Messenger" — granting Abu Bakra full inclusion in the society of Medina despite having no Meccan or Medinan lineage, no wealth, and a despised slave background. He became a complete member of the community and narrated 132 hadith.

Abu Bakra was known for his great enthusiasm and zeal. In one famous incident he entered the mosque, found the Prophet ﷺ already in rukū', and bowed at the door before walking forward to join the line so as not to miss the rak'ah. Instead of rebuking him, the Prophet ﷺ said, "May Allah increase you in eagerness — but do not do it again." Dr. Omar highlights this as a model of how to correct well-meaning mistakes: begin with encouragement and affirmation, then gently correct.

Abu Bakra's entire family became scholars and narrators — his sons (Abdullah, Abdurrahman, Muslim, and others) and his daughter Kabsha. His son became a governor in Persia, and his sister married the founder-companion of Basra. He himself settled in Basra as a leading scholar for nearly five decades, deliberately avoiding the fitna and civil strife among the Muslims, encouraging others to stay out of it as well.

In his old age, while entering a grave to bury his deceased wife, he was injured in the crowding — an injury that eventually led to his death. As he died surrounded by some twenty children and grandchildren, he forbade their loud weeping and said he welcomed death, for he saw a coming time of trial in which he could neither enjoin good nor forbid evil. When his children offered to bring a doctor, he replied, "Where is your doctor — let him push away death if he is truthful." He passed away in Basra in the year 52 AH, and per his will, his Medinan brother-in-Islam led his funeral prayer.

## Key Moments
- Escaped the besieged fortress of Ta'if by lowering himself on the wheel of a well, earning the name Abu Bakra.
- Refused to be attributed to his former master, insisting his identity was "the freed slave of Allah and His Messenger ﷺ."
- His former master came to reclaim him after Ta'if's conversion, but the Prophet ﷺ refused, calling him the freed slave of Allah.
- Bowed in rukū' at the mosque door to catch the rak'ah; the Prophet ﷺ encouraged his zeal before correcting him.
- Narrated 132 hadith, including the eclipse prayer, the warning against false testimony, and the prohibition of unequal exchange of gold and silver.
- His entire family — sons and daughter — became respected scholars and narrators of hadith.
- Avoided involvement in the fitna throughout his life and urged others to do the same.
- Was fatally injured while burying his wife and died in Basra in 52 AH, having served as a teacher there for decades.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Correct well-meaning mistakes with encouragement first, then gentle correction — harsh, immediate rebuke can alienate a person from Allah forever.
- Islam dissolved the racial, tribal, and economic hierarchies of the time; a despised former slave became an elite scholar whose whole family flourished.
- A person's worth is tied to their relationship with Allah and His Messenger ﷺ, not to lineage, wealth, or social class.
- The Prophet ﷺ saw potential and "fruits" even among peoples who had wronged him, choosing mercy over destruction.
- Owning one's humble beginnings with sincerity (Abu Bakra narrating his own correction) reflects integrity, not shame.
- Death is decreed; the believer can welcome it with contentment, especially to be spared trials of faith.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on Abu Bakra: "He is the freed slave of Allah and the freed slave of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Abu Bakra after bowing at the door: "May Allah increase you in eagerness, but do not do it again."
- On the greatest major sins: "Shall I not tell you of the worst of major sins?... associating partners with Allah and disobeying parents... and indeed, false testimony, false testimony, false testimony" — repeated until the companions wished he would stop.
- Abu Bakra's letter to his son, a governor: "Let not one of you judge between two people while he is angry."
- On his deathbed: "Where is your doctor? Let him push away death if he is truthful."
