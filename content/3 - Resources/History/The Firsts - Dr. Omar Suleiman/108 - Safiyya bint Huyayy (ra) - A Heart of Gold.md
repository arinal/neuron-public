# Episode 108 — Safiyya bint Huyayy (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 78 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fwDNboEel4c) · ⏱ 1:00:05

**Who they were:** Safiyya bint Huyayy (ra) was a wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and one of the Mothers of the Believers — the only one from a Jewish (and non-Arab) background, a descendant of the Prophets Harun and (by extension) Musa, known above all for her gentleness, empathy, and a "heart of gold."

## Summary
Safiyya (ra) was the daughter of Huyayy ibn Akhtab, chief of Banu Nadir, the most powerful Jewish tribe of Medina, and her mother Barra bint Samaw'al was of the royals of Banu Qurayza. Adored as a child by both her father and her uncle Abu Yasir, she carried noble lineage and was a descendant of Ibrahim and Harun (peace be upon them). Dr. Omar frames her marriage as one of three the Prophet ﷺ contracted with daughters of enemy chiefs, and notes the symbolism of his marrying into the line of Bani Isra'il.

As a girl she witnessed the moment her father and uncle returned from Quba after seeing the newly arrived Prophet ﷺ. Though they confirmed he was the awaited prophet, Huyayy declared, "I will be his enemy as long as I am alive" — a political rejection in contrast to the chief rabbi Abdullah ibn Salam (ra), who embraced Islam immediately. Huyayy went on to instigate treason during the Battle of Khandaq, helping turn the Arabs against the Prophet ﷺ from within.

Safiyya had been married twice before — first to Salam ibn Mishkam (divorced), then to the abusive Kinanah ibn Abi al-Huqayq. When she dreamed of the moon rising over Khaybar and falling into her lap, her husband struck her, accusing her of desiring "the king of Yathrib." Both her father and husband were killed at the Battle of Khaybar (7 AH). When she was taken captive, the companions urged the Prophet ﷺ that one so noble should be his. He gave her the choice: accept Islam and remain with him, or keep her religion and return free to her people. She declared her longing for Islam, was freed (her freedom serving as her mahr), and married him ﷺ.

The Prophet ﷺ treated her with great honor — observing the prescribed iddah, holding her walima of hais, dates and saweeq on the journey home, dressing her distinctively, and humbly offering his knee to help her mount the camel. On their wedding night they spoke all night; he asked about a green bruise still on her eye, learned of her husband's abuse, and gently explained why war with her tribe became unavoidable until all hard feelings left her heart.

Her life thereafter was marked by constant suspicion — companions who guarded the Prophet's tent with swords, women who blamed her when the camel stumbled, and co-wives who taunted her as "the Jewish woman." The Prophet ﷺ defended her repeatedly, comforting her with the words that her father is Harun, her uncle is Musa, and her husband is Muhammad ﷺ. After his death she lived under the care of Ali (ra), upheld kinship ties with her Jewish relatives, and showed remarkable mercy and forgiveness until her death in Medina, where she was buried in al-Baqi'.

## Key Moments
- As a child, she watched her father Huyayy and uncle Abu Yasir return defeated from Quba, confirm that Muhammad ﷺ was the awaited prophet, yet vow lifelong enmity against him.
- Her abusive husband Kinanah struck her over her dream of the moon rising over Khaybar — a dream that came true through her marriage to the Prophet ﷺ.
- After Khaybar, the Prophet ﷺ rebuked Bilal (ra) — "Has mercy been taken from you, O Bilal?" — for letting captive women pass by their fallen men.
- Given the choice between freedom with her religion or Islam with the Prophet ﷺ, she quoted the Qur'an to him and chose Islam; he freed her and married her.
- On their wedding night, the Prophet ﷺ asked about the bruise on her eye and apologized/explained the realities of the battle until her hard feelings dissolved.
- The "She is Safiyya" incident: the Prophet ﷺ stopped two Ansar to clarify the woman with him was his wife, teaching to leave no room for suspicion.
- During Hajjat al-Wada', when her camel bolted, the Prophet ﷺ held the entire army and wiped her tears with his own hand to console her.
- On his deathbed, when co-wives mocked her wish to bear his pain, the Prophet ﷺ testified: "By Allah, she is truthful."

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ judged people by their own conduct, not by rumors or lineage — refusing to act against Medina's tribes on mere suspicion, and embodying the principle "no soul bears the burden of another."
- True nobility lies in mercy: the Prophet's humanity in war (sparing families the sight of their dead, honoring captives) stood in stark moral contrast to his enemies.
- Safiyya's "superpower" was hilm and waqar — forbearance, dignity, empathy and a heart of gold, a quality beloved to the Prophet ﷺ.
- "She is Safiyya": one should clear ambiguity and protect one's reputation, leaving no opening for Shaytan's whispers — a lesson especially relevant in the age of social media.
- Islam's justice means maintaining kinship and mercy toward non-Muslim relatives; Safiyya kept ties with her Jewish family and willed part of her wealth to her brother.
- Excellence (ihsan) means responding to those who wrong you with pardon — as Safiyya freed the servant girl who slandered her to Umar (ra).

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "No bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another" (Qur'an) — quoted by Safiyya (ra) to the Prophet ﷺ.
- The Prophet ﷺ to Bilal: "Has mercy been taken from you, O Bilal — that you would let these women see their fallen men?"
- The Prophet ﷺ comforting her: "What can they say about you when your father is Harun, your uncle is Musa, and your husband is Muhammad?"
- The Prophet ﷺ about suspicion: "Indeed, Shaytan flows through a person the way blood flows through the veins."
- The Prophet ﷺ on his deathbed: "By Allah, she is truthful (Wallahi innaha la sadiqah)."
- Safiyya (ra) to Umar (ra): "I never loved the Sabbath after Allah gave me Jumu'ah in exchange... and as for the Jews, they are my kin, so I uphold ties with them."
