# Episode 145 — Ka'b ibn Zuhayr (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 41 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_mnddXGH8k8) · ⏱ 51:21

**Who they were:** Ka'b ibn Zuhayr (ra) was the master poet of early Islam, son of the legendary pre-Islamic poet Zuhayr ibn Abi Sulma. After years of writing hostile poetry against the Prophet ﷺ, he repented and composed the first *Burda* ("Banat Su'ad"), for which the Prophet ﷺ gave him his own mantle.

## Summary
Dr. Omar frames this episode within the broader theme of poets in the time of the Prophet ﷺ. Poetry was the media of the age, and poets were its most potent propagandists. Every accusation the Quraysh hurled at the Prophet ﷺ — that he was a poet, a sorcerer — was in reality a confession of the tactics they themselves employed. The nastiest poetry was used to dehumanize Muslims, incite war, and humiliate Muslim women, a pattern Dr. Omar draws parallels to in modern atrocities.

Ka'b's father, Zuhayr ibn Abi Sulma, was the supreme poet of the age — author of one of the seven hanging poems (the Mu'allaqat) displayed in the Ka'bah. His poem recounted the reconciliation of two warring tribes, and his work made him immensely wealthy and honored. Umar (ra) cited Zuhayr as the poet of all poets, praising his work because he spoke no foolishness, never betrayed logic, only praised people for what was truly in them, and only spoke what he knew — illustrating the permissibility of wholesome pre-Islamic poetry.

Zuhayr discouraged his son Ka'b from poetry, fearing he would either be corrupted or tarnish the family name, but Ka'b secretly mastered the craft and eventually earned his father's blessing. Zuhayr, who knew of an awaited prophet from the People of the Book, dreamt of a rope descending from the sky that he could not reach — interpreting it that he would die before this prophet came. He advised his sons to embrace this Mustafa if he appeared, and indeed died before the revelation.

Ka'b inherited his father's wealth and his throne as master poet. He and his younger brother Bujayr belonged to Banu Muzaynah, an outer tribe along the route of the Hijra. When Bujayr went to hear the Prophet ﷺ and embraced Islam, Ka'b was enraged, believing his brother had fallen for a cult of "illiterate, brainless" followers. He escalated his attacks, sending mocking poetry accusing Bujayr of drinking from Abu Bakr's intoxicating cup and abandoning his ancestral path. The Prophet ﷺ turned the insult into praise, noting that Bujayr was courageous enough to follow the truth even though his family was not upon it.

As Islam triumphed after Badr and the conquest of Mecca, the world closed in on Ka'b, who now had a death sentence for inciting war. Bujayr wrote to him, urging repentance and assuring him the Prophet ﷺ forgave those far worse — Abu Sufyan, Safwan, the people of Ta'if. Ka'b secretly entered Medina, stayed with an old friend, and approached the Prophet ﷺ at Fajr with his face wrapped. He cleverly asked whether the Prophet ﷺ would forgive Ka'b if he came repentant, and only after receiving the answer revealed himself and declared the shahada. An Ansari leapt to kill him, but the Prophet ﷺ ordered him left alone.

Ka'b then recited his masterpiece — the original *Burda*, "Banat Su'ad" — a 60-line poem whose first 40 lines speak of a woman (or, scholars debate, the days of ignorance that abandoned him) before turning to lavish praise of the Prophet ﷺ. He likened the Prophet ﷺ to an unsheathed Indian sword from whose light guidance is sought. In gratitude, the Prophet ﷺ removed his own mantle (burda) and placed it on him — both in the Arab tradition of rewarding poets and as a means of softening his heart. Ka'b became the master poet of Islam, refused to sell the mantle even to Mu'awiyah, and his descendants carried on the poetic legacy. Dr. Omar closes with Abdullah ibn al-Zib'ara, another fierce anti-Islam poet and rival of Hassan ibn Thabit, who likewise repented and was embraced by the Prophet ﷺ.

## Key Moments
- Ka'b's father Zuhayr ibn Abi Sulma authored one of the seven Mu'allaqat hung in the Ka'bah and was the supreme poet of the Jahiliyyah.
- Umar (ra) named Zuhayr the "poet of all poets" for four reasons: no foolishness, sound logic, honest praise, and speaking only what he knew.
- Zuhayr's dream of an unreachable rope from the sky, signifying he would die before the awaited prophet came — with the instruction to his sons to embrace him.
- Bujayr, Ka'b's younger brother, embraced Islam and wrote urging Ka'b to repent, citing the Prophet's ﷺ forgiveness of Abu Sufyan, Safwan, and others.
- Ka'b's hostile poetry mocking his brother for "drinking from Abu Bakr's cup" — which the Prophet ﷺ reframed as praise of Bujayr's courage.
- Ka'b's secret, dangerous entry into Medina and his clever indirect question before revealing himself and declaring the shahada at Fajr.
- The recitation of the first *Burda* ("Banat Su'ad"), after which the Prophet ﷺ gave Ka'b his own mantle.
- Ka'b being instructed to praise the Ansar after his initial poem praised only the Muhajirun, because an Ansari had attacked him.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Judge speech and art by its content and values, not merely its eloquence — Umar's criteria for Zuhayr's poetry illustrate what makes words wholesome.
- The mercy of the Prophet ﷺ extended even to warmongers and enemies who repented; no past sin barred sincere return to Islam.
- "Islam wipes away what came before it" — repentance erases the entire record of one's former hostility.
- Following the truth requires courage even when one's family and heritage are not upon it.
- The Prophet ﷺ softened hearts with generosity and honor, turning fierce opponents into devoted defenders.
- The Prophet ﷺ counseled patience over haste — listening fully before judgment, even toward a man under a death sentence.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Umar (ra) on Zuhayr's poetry: he liked it because "he doesn't speak foolishness... he doesn't betray logic... he doesn't praise anyone except with what is actually in them... he only speaks that which he knows."
- The Prophet ﷺ on Bujayr: acknowledging he was "right about one thing — he did not find his father or his mother upon this," reframing Ka'b's insult as praise.
- From the *Burda*: the Prophet ﷺ is "surely a sword from whose light is sought... one of the swords of Allah, an Indian blade that is unsheathed."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Abdullah ibn al-Zib'ara upon his repentance: "Islam does away with everything that came before it."
