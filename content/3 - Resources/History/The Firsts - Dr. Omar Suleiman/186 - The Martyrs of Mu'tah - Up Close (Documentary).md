# Episode 186 — The Martyrs of Mu'tah: Zayd ibn Harithah, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib & Abdullah ibn Rawahah (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 88 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xMEpkbxUHqU) · ⏱ 30:42

**Who they were:** The three appointed commanders of the Battle of Mu'tah — Zayd ibn Harithah (the beloved freedman and "son" of the Prophet ﷺ), Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (the Prophet's cousin and ambassador to Abyssinia), and Abdullah ibn Rawahah (the warrior-poet of the Prophet ﷺ) — all of whom were martyred in a single battle against an overwhelmingly larger Roman-allied army.

## Summary
After the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah and the conquest of Khaybar, the Muslims gained the strength to send messengers across the world calling people to Islam. The Prophet ﷺ dispatched emissaries to Rome, Persia, Abyssinia and beyond. One messenger, al-Harith, was sent toward Bilad al-Sham (Syria) and was stopped by the Ghassanid Arabs — Christian Arabs allied to Rome. Their chief crucified him, making him the only "messenger of the Messenger" ever murdered, a flagrant violation of the international law of the time that protected ambassadors. In response the Prophet ﷺ raised an army of 3,000 companions and sent it to Mu'tah.

He ﷺ appointed his commanders in succession: Zayd ibn Harithah first; if he fell, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib; and if he fell, Abdullah ibn Rawahah. When the army arrived they found themselves facing a force estimated between 150,000 and 200,000 fighters — including 100,000 Ghassanids and tens of thousands of professional Roman legionnaires. Faced with such impossible odds, some hesitated, wondering whether to send back for new instructions, but Abdullah ibn Rawahah urged them forward, reminding them that martyrdom was the very thing they came seeking.

Zayd ibn Harithah — abducted as a child, gifted eventually to the Prophet ﷺ, and so beloved that he chose to remain with him over returning to his own father — carried the banner first and was killed. The Prophet ﷺ, narrating the battle live from the minbar in Medina with his voice quivering, announced his death.

Ja'far ibn Abi Talib then took the banner. The first ambassador of Islam, who at around twenty years old had won the protection of the Negus (Najashi) of Abyssinia, he plunged into the army. When his right arm was severed he held the banner with his left; when his left was severed he clutched it between the stumps of his arms until he fell with over fifty (some say up to ninety) wounds across his body. The Prophet ﷺ said his arms were replaced with two wings flying in Paradise, and Ja'far's sons were thereafter called "the sons of the one with two wings."

Abdullah ibn Rawahah, the poet and devoted worshiper who had defended the Prophet ﷺ with his verse and fasted on the hottest days, took the banner third. Though he wept before the battle out of fear of the Fire and the verse that all must pass over it, he rallied the Muslims and rebuked his own soul for hesitating before Paradise, then drove into the enemy until he too was martyred. Finally Khalid ibn al-Walid — a relatively new Muslim fighting his first battle as a believer — took up the banner. Using a clever diversionary tactic to make 3,000 look like a far larger force, he struck the enemy center, inflicted heavy losses, broke nine swords in his hand, and brought the army back safely, earning the title "the Sword of Allah."

## Key Moments
- The murder/crucifixion of the Prophet's messenger al-Harith by the Ghassanid chief — an unprecedented violation that triggered the expedition.
- The Prophet ﷺ naming three commanders in succession before the army departed: Zayd, then Ja'far, then Abdullah ibn Rawahah.
- The Muslims arriving to find roughly 150,000–200,000 enemy fighters against only 3,000 — the most disproportionate battle they had ever faced.
- Zayd ibn Harithah, the freed slave who chose the Prophet ﷺ over his own father, falling first as commander.
- Ja'far losing both arms yet holding the banner aloft between his stumps until he died of over fifty wounds.
- The Prophet ﷺ narrating the entire battle from Medina by divine vision, his voice quivering and his face overcome with grief.
- The Prophet ﷺ visiting Ja'far's family, embracing and smelling his sons, then weeping as he delivered the news of his martyrdom.
- Khalid ibn al-Walid's diversion tactic and counterattack that turned the battle, allowing the small army to return victorious.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True love for the Prophet ﷺ means loving those whom he loved — and these three were among the closest people in the world to him.
- The body is nothing; the soul is what endures. The martyrs are alive with their Lord, in gardens far more beautiful than anything in this world.
- Faith is choosing what is right over comfort and belonging — as Zayd chose the Prophet ﷺ over freedom and family.
- Genuine courage is not the absence of fear: even Abdullah ibn Rawahah, promised Paradise, wept in fear of standing before Allah, yet pushed his soul forward.
- Sincere sacrifice in Allah's cause is honored beyond measure — Ja'far's lost arms were replaced with wings in Paradise.
- Victory ultimately comes from Allah; 3,000 prevailing over up to 200,000 was an observable miracle that restored the Muslims' confidence.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Qur'an: "Do not say of those who are killed in the way of Allah that they are dead; rather they are alive, being sustained and provided for."
- Abdullah ibn Rawahah to the hesitant army: "What you fear is exactly why you came here — a shahada. You cannot fear martyrdom."
- Abdullah rebuking his own soul: "O soul, why do I see you not liking Jannah? Why do I see you hating your entrance to Jannah? I will enter you into Jannah whether you like it or not."
- The Prophet ﷺ on Ja'far: that Allah had replaced his arms with two wings with which he flies in Paradise.
- The Prophet ﷺ to Ja'far after his return from Abyssinia: "I do not know with which I am happier — the conquest of Khaybar or the arrival of Ja'far," and that Ja'far most resembled the Prophet ﷺ in appearance and character.
