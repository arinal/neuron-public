# Episode 52 — Al-Shifa bint Abdullah al-Adawiyya (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 52 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tJZZ_IurOIE) · ⏱ 16:41

**Who they were:** Al-Shifa bint Abdullah (ra) was one of the earliest Muslim women, a rare literate woman of Mecca, a healer, calligrapher, and scholar who taught the Prophet's ﷺ wife Hafsa (ra), and a trusted advisor consulted by both the Prophet ﷺ and later Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) on matters of the marketplace.

## Summary
Al-Shifa bint Abdullah al-Qurashiyya al-Adawiyya was from the tribe of Banu Adi, the same clan as Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra). Her given name was actually Layla, but she earned the nickname "Al-Shifa" (the healer) because of her knowledge of medicine, and the name stayed with her for life. She married Abu Hathma ibn Hudhayfa and had two sons, Sulayman and Masruq (ra). Though her renown grew most after the Prophet's ﷺ death, she was among the very first Muslims, embracing Islam within the first three years of the call.

She was distinguished by her wisdom (hikmah) and by being among the roughly twenty to thirty literate people in Mecca — and one of the few literate women. She mastered reading, writing, medicine, and calligraphy, and she became a teacher of these skills. Hafsa (ra), the wife of the Prophet ﷺ, was her student, learning to read, write, and do calligraphy from her. Al-Shifa was also among the first women to make the hijrah to Madinah.

In Madinah her home was prominently situated between the masjid and the marketplace. The Prophet ﷺ would visit and consult her, particularly on matters of finance and the marketplace, and she kept a special izar (garment) and blanket and amenities ready for his visits, where he would rest and take a nap. So treasured were these items that they became the most precious part of her inheritance to her sons.

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Umar (ra), upon becoming khalifa, continued to honor her, frequently consulting her on the affairs and fiqh of the marketplace as he passed by on his way there. Some reports even suggest he placed her in charge of the marketplace. Her son Masruq was appointed an amir and was known for his knowledge, while her son Sulayman was a hafiz known for long night prayers.

Her lineage survived through Sulayman, whose two sons were named Abu Bakr and Uthman — both narrators of hadith. Through this chain, several of her narrations were preserved, including her famous account of how Umar (ra) came to be called Amir al-Mu'minin. She passed away during the caliphate of Umar (ra), who himself led her funeral prayer.

## Key Moments
- Her true name was Layla; "Al-Shifa" (the healer) was a nickname earned through her knowledge of medicine.
- One of only about 20–30 literate people in Mecca, and among the few literate women, she taught reading, writing, and calligraphy.
- She taught Hafsa (ra) to read and write — significant because the Qur'an was later gathered in Hafsa's home, partly due to her literacy.
- The Prophet ﷺ asked her to teach Hafsa the ruqya for ant bites just as she had taught her to write (narrated in Abu Dawud).
- Her home in Madinah sat between the masjid and the marketplace; she kept special amenities ready for the Prophet's ﷺ visits.
- When Umar (ra) asked why her son Sulayman missed Fajr in congregation, she explained he had prayed all night and fallen asleep — prompting Umar's famous statement valuing Fajr in jama'ah.
- She narrated the account of how the title "Amir al-Mu'minin" was first given to Umar (ra), through the visit of Labid ibn Rabi'a and Adi ibn Hatim.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Literacy and beneficial knowledge — including medicine and the practical fiqh of finance — are valued and elevated in Islam.
- Women played central scholarly and advisory roles in the earliest community; the Prophet ﷺ and Umar (ra) both sought Al-Shifa's counsel.
- The most precious legacy is not wealth but proximity to and reverence for the Prophet ﷺ — her dearest inheritance was the items he used in her home.
- Catching Fajr in congregation is immensely rewarded; the Prophet ﷺ said it is as if one prayed the entire night.
- Knowledge is preserved and transmitted through families and generations — her narrations reached us through her grandsons and ultimately Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ to Al-Shifa: "Will you not teach Hafsa the ruqya for ant bites the same way you taught her to write?" (Abu Dawud)
- Umar (ra): "For me to pray Fajr in congregation is more beloved to me than praying the entire night."
- The Prophet ﷺ on the best of deeds: "Belief in Allah, striving (jihad) in the path of Allah, and an accepted Hajj (hajj mabrur)."
- Al-Shifa describing Umar (ra): "When he spoke, he made himself heard; when he walked, he walked briskly; when he fed, he fed generously; and when he struck, he struck hard."
- On the title Amir al-Mu'minin, Amr ibn al-As (ra) said of Umar: "He is the commander (amir) and we are the believers (mu'minin)."
