# Episode 8 — Fatima bint Muhammad ﷺ (al-Zahra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 8 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RbwnRZ30TVE) · ⏱ 57:10

**Who they were:** Fatima (ra), the youngest and most beloved daughter of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and Khadija (ra), known as "Umm Abiha" (the mother of her father), and her marriage to Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra) — together forming the first household of Ahl al-Bayt.

## Summary
This episode brings Fatima (ra) into the story alongside Ali (ra), recapping that Ali's mother was Fatima bint Asad, the first Hashemite woman to embrace Islam and a mother-figure to the Prophet ﷺ. Khadija never breastfed Ali, which by Allah's wisdom later allowed his marriage to Fatima. Fatima was born around the year of revelation (c. 610), the last daughter of the Prophet ﷺ and Khadija, and was named after Khadija's mother, Fatima bint Zaidah. While "Khadija" means premature, "Fatima" derives from a term meaning a full-term, weaned child — and Abu Hurayra (ra) narrated that Allah weaned Fatima and her offspring from the Fire.

Dr. Omar lists the seven children of the Prophet ﷺ — Qasim, Zaynab, Ruqayya, Umm Kulthum, Fatima, Abdullah, and Ibrahim (from Maria) — noting that Fatima was the only one raised entirely in Islam. After Khadija's death (around the same time as Abu Talib's), the ten-year-old Fatima stepped into a maternal role, cleaning her father's wounds when he was beaten in public — earning her the title "Umm Abiha." In one of the most painful incidents, Uqba ibn Abi Mu'it, at Abu Jahl's challenge, dumped camel entrails on the Prophet's ﷺ back during prostration; young Fatima ran out crying and scraped the filth off her father, who then prayed against his oppressors and comforted her that "Allah will give victory to your father."

The Prophet ﷺ taught Fatima that she was the master of the women of her time, while clarifying that Maryam was the best of the women of past nations and Khadija the best of her own. Fatima was among the four women of perfected faith: Maryam, Asiya, Khadija, and Fatima. She migrated to Medina at thirteen and resembled the Prophet ﷺ so closely in look, walk, and manner that Aisha (ra) said no one resembled him more; father and daughter would each rise, kiss, and seat the other on entering a room.

After the Battle of Badr — a day of joy in victory but sorrow in the death of Ruqayya — the question of Fatima's marriage arose. Both Abu Bakr and Umar (ra) proposed and were gently turned away, signaling the Prophet ﷺ had someone in mind. Encouraged by family, an elderly servant, and the senior companions, the impoverished Ali (ra) finally summoned the courage to propose, though awe of the Prophet ﷺ left him speechless until the Prophet ﷺ himself asked, "Perhaps you came to ask for Fatima's hand?"

Ali's only possession was his shield, worth four dirhams; Uthman (ra) bought it for 400 dirhams and then gifted it back, earning a dua from the Prophet ﷺ. With around 480 dirhams as the mahr, the Prophet ﷺ directed two-thirds toward perfume and one-third toward furniture. The whole community joined in — Bilal, Umm Salama, Abu Bakr, Ammar ibn Yasir, Aisha — preparing a strikingly simple home and a humble wedding feast of barley, dates, and hayse. On the wedding night the Prophet ﷺ made wudu, poured water over Ali and Fatima, comforted his nervous daughter, and prayed: "O Allah, bless them, and what is between them, and what is upon them, and bless them in their offspring."

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet's ﷺ extraordinary fatherhood — standing, kissing, and honoring his daughter in a society that buried daughters alive — sets the standard for treating one's children.
- Accountability before Allah is individual: the Prophet ﷺ told even his beloved daughter that he could not protect her from God's reckoning, and that he would punish even Fatima were she to steal.
- The best mahr and weddings are the simplest; the marriage of the leader of the women of Paradise was 480 dirhams, and the mahr is a meaningful gift, not a price tag or insurance plan.
- Generosity and community: Uthman's quiet charity and the senior companions personally helping prepare the wedding reflect a society that rejoiced in righteous unions.
- Hardship can refine faith — Fatima's perfected iman is linked to her growing up amid persecution, never knowing the days of ease her sisters did.
- Modesty (haya) and the proper conduct of marriage: the woman's consent, including indicated approval through silence born of shyness, suffices.
