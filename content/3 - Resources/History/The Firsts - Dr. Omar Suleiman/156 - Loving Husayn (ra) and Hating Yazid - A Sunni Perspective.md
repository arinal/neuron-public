# Episode 156 — Al-Husayn ibn Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 30 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lNxB8zcdlho) · ⏱ 21:10

**Who they were:** Al-Husayn (ra) was the beloved grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, son of Ali (ra) and Fatima (ra), whose tragic martyrdom at Karbala is one of the greatest tragedies of Islamic history. This episode is a companion reflection clarifying the mainstream Sunni perspective on loving Husayn, hating Yazid, and commemorating Ashura.

## Summary
Dr. Omar opens by situating this episode within the current season of The Firsts, which revisits the young companions and the children of companions — including the family of the Prophet ﷺ — and builds out their stories from their vantage point. He notes that Karbala and the month of Muharram bring intense, heated, and often hateful emotions, and he deliberately avoids that tone, instead seeking the lessons of these noble lives. He ties Husayn's redefined notion of victory — that those killed in pursuit of truth and virtue for Allah's sake are triumphant — to the suffering of the people of Gaza witnessed over the preceding two years.

He then addresses three recurring questions. The **first** is whether Sunnis curse Yazid ibn Muawiyah, who cannot be fully extracted from complicity in the murder of Husayn and who reigned terror upon Medina and Mecca. Dr. Omar establishes that Yazid is no companion and no man of virtue; Imam Ahmad, asked by his son how anyone of faith could love Yazid, replied that no one who loves Allah could. Yet when asked why he did not curse him, Imam Ahmad answered, "When have you seen your father curse anybody?" — a restraint rooted in his own character, not in any virtue of Yazid.

He explains that scholars differed: some, like Ibn al-Jawzi, wrote treatises permitting the cursing of Yazid, while others refrained as a matter of safety — leaving open the slim possibility that he may have repented before death — without ever absolving him or minimizing the crime. The key point: one need not whitewash the crime nor curse to stand within mainstream Sunni scholarship.

The **second** issue is loving Husayn, which is part of iman. Even without his martyrdom, Muslims would love him because of the Prophet's ﷺ love for him — the way he would grab, kiss, and hold his grandson — and because sending peace upon the Prophet's family is part of daily salah. His tragic death only deepens that natural attachment to the heart.

The **third** issue is how to commemorate Ashura. Dr. Omar reconciles the fasting tied to the victory of Musa (as) over Pharaoh with the painful memory of Husayn's martyrdom. He recounts his father's memory of people in Medina who insulted Abu Bakr and Umar, and others who responded by insulting the Prophet's family — a transgression met with transgression, resulting in loss. The Sunni middle path, he argues, is to preserve **historical memory** without **devotional commemoration of death**: just as the companions kept tragedies like Uhud and the martyrdom of Hamza alive in their hearts without instituting annual mourning rituals, we honor Husayn through love and remembrance while guarding the creed and channeling emotions of love and hate in spiritually productive ways.

## Key Moments
- The episode frames Husayn's life within the season's focus on the young members of the Prophet's family and the children of companions.
- Imam Ahmad's exchange with his son: no one who loves Allah loves Yazid, yet Imam Ahmad refrained from cursing because he refrained from cursing anyone.
- The scholarly difference of opinion: some Sunni scholars (e.g., Ibn al-Jawzi) permitted cursing Yazid; most refrained as a precaution, none absolved him.
- The story of Dr. Omar's father witnessing people insult Abu Bakr and Umar at their graves, and others retaliating by insulting the Prophet's family.
- The reminder that loving the Prophet's family is woven into the daily salah and is essential to a complete iman.
- The companions weeping over their food and clothing as they remembered Hamza's body lacking even a garment to cover it — historical memory without ritual commemoration.
- Connecting the loneliness, betrayal, and gruesome killing of Husayn at Karbala to the suffering of the people of Gaza, including children named Hamza, Husayn, and Hassan.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Victory in Islam is redefined: those who die seeking truth and virtue for Allah are triumphant, while their killers face misery in this life and the next.
- One can hate the crime and complicity of Yazid without cursing him, and without minimizing the murder of Husayn — both fit within the Sunni mainstream.
- Loving the family of the Prophet ﷺ is a duty of faith, not contingent on Husayn's martyrdom; to lack it makes one's iman deficient.
- The Sunni balance is to maintain historical memory of tragedies while avoiding devotional commemoration of death, guarding the creed.
- Respond to transgression with truth, not with counter-transgression; meeting an insult with an insult only multiplies loss.
- Channel natural love and hate in spiritually productive ways that keep one within the boundaries of correct belief.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Imam Ahmad, when asked how some could love Yazid: "Does anyone who has faith love Yazid? Does anyone who loves Allah love Yazid?"
- Imam Ahmad, when asked why he did not curse Yazid: "When have you seen your father cursing anybody?"
- On the framework of true victory: "Our dead are in paradise; your dead are in hellfire."
- The Prophet ﷺ taught that Allah loves those who love his family, and commanded the ummah to love them for his sake and for the sake of Allah.
