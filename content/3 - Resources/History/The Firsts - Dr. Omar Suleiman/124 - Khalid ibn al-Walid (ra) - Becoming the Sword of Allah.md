# Episode 124 — Khalid ibn al-Walid ibn al-Mughirah al-Makhzumi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 62 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BIv71MsdFVs) · ⏱ 1:13:13

**Who they were:** Khalid ibn al-Walid (ra), known as Sayfullah ("the Sword of Allah"), was the maternal nephew of the Prophet's ﷺ wife Maymunah bint al-Harith and is regarded without dispute as the greatest general in the history of Islam after the Prophet ﷺ. Though he embraced Islam late, after years of fighting against the Muslims, he became one of their most devoted and effective commanders.

## Summary
Khalid came from Banu Makhzum, the great rival of Banu Hashim in generosity, status, and power within Mecca. His father, al-Walid ibn al-Mughirah, was the chief of Banu Makhzum, the wealthiest and one of the most eloquent men in Mecca, and a person whose conversion the Prophet ﷺ greatly desired. Al-Walid was nicknamed "al-Wahid" (the one/alone) because he would cover the cost of the Kaaba's kiswah by himself every other year. Despite hearing the Quran and privately admitting its power — declaring it was "not the words of man or jinn" — his arrogance prevented his belief.

Abu Jahl, al-Walid's nephew, manipulated him by playing on his pride, spreading rumors that he only visited the Prophet ﷺ for money. This pushed al-Walid to publicly slander the Prophet ﷺ, eventually coining the formula that the Quran was merely "sihrun yu'thar" (magic that overcomes people). Allah revealed verses in Surah al-Muddathir giving a play-by-play of al-Walid's scheming, and in Surah al-Qalam exposing him — including the revelation that he was a child of adultery, which his mother later confirmed. Al-Walid died from an infected foot wound after stepping on a spear, leaving vindictive final instructions concerning his blood money, debts owed to him, and revenge for his daughter. This was the father in whose household Khalid grew up.

Khalid himself was the youngest son, born physically imposing — described as resembling the twin of Umar ibn al-Khattab (his relative) in stature. He had no interest in trade and cared only for war, mastering archery, spear-throwing, horseback and camelback riding, swordsmanship, and military strategy beyond anyone of his age. His first major action against the Muslims came at Uhud, where as commander of the left flank he exploited the archers abandoning their mountain post, circled around, and devastated the Muslims — making him a hero among the Meccans. He also created diversions at Khandaq and was outmaneuvered by the Prophet ﷺ at Hudaybiyyah using his own diversion tactics, which began convincing him of the Prophet's ﷺ divine support.

Khalid's heart gradually softened through several signs: his brother al-Walid ibn al-Walid (a freed Badr captive who embraced Islam as a free man) wrote him letters of da'wah; he witnessed the prayer of fear during Hudaybiyyah; he saw the Prophet ﷺ divinely protected from every attempt against him; and he had a dream — interpreted by Abu Bakr (ra) — of moving from a barren, constricted land into a green, expansive meadow, signifying his journey from shirk to Islam. After being rebuffed by Safwan ibn Umayya and Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl, and confronted by Abu Sufyan, Khalid left Mecca for Medina in the 8th year of Hijrah, meeting Uthman ibn Talhah and Amr ibn al-As along the way. The Prophet ﷺ welcomed him warmly, prayed for his forgiveness, and from that day never preferred anyone over him.

Less than two weeks later came the Battle of Mu'tah, where Khalid went out as a foot soldier. After the three appointed commanders — Zayd ibn Harithah, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib, and Abdullah ibn Rawahah — were martyred, Thabit ibn Arqam offered Khalid the banner, which he humbly declined before the Muslims unanimously accepted him. With his diversion tactics he deceived the vastly larger Roman force, broke nine swords in combat, and led the 3,000 Muslims to safety. The Prophet ﷺ, watching via revelation from the minbar, declared him "a sword from the swords of Allah." Khalid spent his wealth on weapons and battle preparation rather than withholding it, and on his deathbed regretted having memorized only a few surahs because of his total dedication to jihad. Most poignantly, he asked Salim Mawla Abi Hudhayfah to recite the verses about his own father so he could memorize them — completing his faith despite the pain.

## Key Moments
- Al-Walid ibn al-Mughirah privately admits the Quran's power but, manipulated by Abu Jahl, coins the slander "sihrun yu'thar" — exposed by revelation in Surah al-Muddathir.
- Allah exposes al-Walid in Surah al-Qalam as a child of adultery, confirmed by his own mother.
- Khalid commands the left flank at Uhud and engineers the counterattack that turns the battle into a Muslim catastrophe.
- His brother al-Walid ibn al-Walid is ransomed for 4,000 dinars after Badr, then embraces Islam as a free man and begins years of da'wah to Khalid.
- Khalid is outsmarted by the Prophet ﷺ at Hudaybiyyah using his own diversion tactics — a sign to him of prophethood.
- Khalid's dream of moving from barren land to a green meadow, interpreted by Abu Bakr (ra) as his passage from shirk to Islam.
- Khalid, Uthman ibn Talhah, and Amr ibn al-As travel together to Medina and embrace Islam in Safar of year 8 AH — the last three to earn the status of Muhajireen.
- At Mu'tah, Khalid takes the banner after three commanders fall, breaks nine swords, and saves the army — earning the title Sayfullah.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Ulterior motives and arrogance can blind even the most intelligent person to the truth — al-Walid recognized the Quran's beauty yet refused it out of pride.
- "Islam wipes out everything that came before it" — the Prophet ﷺ accepted Khalid as if no enmity had ever existed, modeling complete forgiveness.
- The mercy shown to prisoners (the ihsan toward Badr captives) softened hearts and won converts like al-Walid ibn al-Walid.
- Sincerity (ikhlas) is shown by serving the cause with one's God-given strengths — Khalid spent his wealth on weapons and gave his whole self to the mission.
- Faith requires confronting hard truths: Khalid insisted on memorizing the painful verses about his own father to complete his Iman.
- Genuine humility: despite his genius, Khalid declined leadership at Mu'tah out of respect for the senior, longtime Muslims.
