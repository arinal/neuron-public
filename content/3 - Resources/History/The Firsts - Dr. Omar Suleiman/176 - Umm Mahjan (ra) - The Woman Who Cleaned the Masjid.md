# Episode 176 — Umm Mahjan (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 10 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-rHYYdac6Ks) · ⏱ 25:09

**Who they were:** A humble freed slave woman of African origin who lived in and cared for the Prophet's ﷺ masjid in Medina — cleaning and perfuming it — and whose simple, often unnoticed service earned her an extraordinary place of honor in the eyes of the Prophet ﷺ.

## Summary
This episode opens the first of three biographies of "the unnoticed" — the people who slip through the cracks of a community, whom others fail to greet or even notice, yet whom the Prophet ﷺ singled out for great honor. Dr. Omar frames these stories as lessons in how the Prophet ﷺ dealt with the most easily forgotten and exploited members of society.

Dr. Omar first establishes the virtue of cleaning the masjid. The Prophet ﷺ commanded the building of masjids in every locality and insisted they be kept clean, pure, and perfumed. So central was this that as Islam spread, the Sahaba would build the masjid before their own homes. The Prophet ﷺ himself cleaned the masjid by hand — even removing dried snot from his own pulpit — and was once so pleased when a woman scratched away a stain and perfumed the spot that he praised her deed publicly.

Almost nothing is known of Umm Mahjan beyond her first name; her biography is pieced together from several narrations. Her backstory, narrated by Aisha (ra), who enjoyed sitting and talking with her, revolves around a line of poetry she constantly hummed: "the day of the red scarf was an amazing miracle from the Lord, and verily He saved me from a disbelieving people." She had been a slave girl, likely from Abyssinia (Habasha), caring for the daughter of a powerful tribal chief. When a bird snatched the girl's precious red scarf during a journey, Umm Mahjan was accused of lying and theft, then stripped, beaten, and violated. Suddenly the bird swooped back and dropped the scarf in their midst — vindicating her. Her master, full of remorse, freed her. She later heard of the Prophet ﷺ, came to Medina, and embraced Islam, becoming not just a Muslim but one of the noble Sahaba.

In Medina she lived in a small hut/tent within the masjid, cleaning and perfuming it after the congregation departed. When she died one night, the companions, not wishing to disturb the Prophet ﷺ, quietly washed, prayed over, and buried her. The Prophet ﷺ noticed her absence, was upset that he had not been informed, and remarked that they had belittled her affair. He asked to be taken to her grave and prayed a separate janazah over her, declaring that the graves are full of darkness for their occupants and that Allah illuminates them through his prayer upon them.

Dr. Omar draws out the lessons: the virtue of cleaning the masjid, the right of every Muslim — even the unknown — to a proper janazah, and the reality that the most noble before Allah are the most pious, not the most prominent. He notes that overcoming ingrained tribalism and elitism took the Sahaba time, and that this woman's true rank was far above what her station suggested.

## Key Moments
- The Prophet ﷺ commanded building, maintaining, cleaning, and perfuming masjids; the Sahaba built masjids before their own homes.
- The Prophet ﷺ cleaned the masjid with his own hands, even removing dried mucus from his pulpit, and praised a woman who cleaned and perfumed a stain that had angered him.
- Contrast drawn with the wife of Abu Lahab, who dumped filth in the Prophet's ﷺ path and is called "the carrier of firewood" — royalty that burns in Hell — versus this humble woman who becomes royalty in Paradise.
- Umm Mahjan's backstory: falsely accused of stealing a red scarf taken by a bird, beaten and violated, then vindicated when the bird returned it, leading to her freedom.
- She came to Medina out of curiosity about the Prophet ﷺ, embraced Islam, and lived in a hut within the masjid.
- After her quiet night burial, the Prophet ﷺ noticed her absence, was distressed he wasn't told, and prayed a separate janazah at her grave.
- The Prophet ﷺ declared that his prayer turns the darkness of graves into light for their occupants.
- In a weaker narration, when asked which deed she found most beneficial, she answered from her grave: cleaning the masjid.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Cleaning the masjid and removing harmful or unclean things from it is a beloved, ennobling deed — the lowest branch of faith is removing harm from the road; how much more so from the houses of Allah.
- The Prophet ﷺ noticed and honored the people the community overlooked; true Islamic leadership sees the forgotten.
- Every Muslim, however unknown or humble, has a right to a proper janazah — if you didn't notice them in life, don't fail to notice them in death.
- Nobility before Allah is measured by taqwa (piety), not lineage, status, or wealth — a lesson the Sahaba themselves had to grow into.
- We never know the profound stories behind the quiet people in our masjids; Allah guides people through unexpected hardships to the best ends.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "The day of the red scarf was an amazing miracle from the Lord, and verily He saved me from a disbelieving people." — Umm Mahjan's recurring poem.
- The Prophet ﷺ at her grave: "Verily these graves are full of darkness for their occupants, and verily Allah gives light to them by my praying upon these people."
- "Verily the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most pious of you." (Qur'an 49:13)
- The Prophet ﷺ on removing harm: he saw a man "strolling in Jannah" because he removed something harmful from the road, and that removing harm is the lowest branch of faith.
