# Episode 182 — Abu Rafi al-Qibti (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 4 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QgxnWofZCRE) · ⏱ 29:27

**Who they were:** Abu Rafi (ra) was a Coptic Egyptian originally enslaved by al-Abbas (ra), later gifted to and freed by the Prophet ﷺ. A trusted member of the Prophet's household — carpenter, messenger, and servant — he was praised as one whose heart was pure, free of malice and envy.

## Summary
Dr. Omar continues the theme of the *mawali* (freed slaves) of the Prophet ﷺ, introducing Abu Rafi al-Qibti as part of what he calls "the family of the family of the Prophet ﷺ" — a household of freed slaves attached to the Prophet's immediate family. This episode sets the stage for the later coverage of Maria al-Qibtiyya (ra). Abu Rafi was originally Egyptian and Coptic; his name is variously reported as Ibrahim, Aslam, or Yasar, though scholars favor Aslam, while he is known throughout hadith literature by his kunya, Abu Rafi.

He was enslaved in the household of al-Abbas (ra) before Islam and served as a messenger carrying communications within the family. Quraysh once dispatched him to the Prophet ﷺ, and the moment he saw the Prophet ﷺ, faith was cast into his heart. He declared he could never return to Quraysh, but the Prophet ﷺ — refusing to break a covenant or imprison a messenger — told him to return and conceal his Islam until the time was right. Abu Rafi remained in Mecca, secretly practicing Islam alongside Umm al-Fadl (Lubaba) and the children of al-Abbas.

When news of the Muslim victory at Badr reached Mecca, Abu Rafi was sitting with Umm al-Fadl while Abu Lahab anxiously awaited word. As Abu Sufyan described how unseen white figures on great horses had struck down the Quraysh, Abu Rafi could not contain his joy and cried out that those were the angels. Abu Lahab beat him savagely until Umm al-Fadl struck Abu Lahab on the head with a tent pole — a wound from which Abu Lahab rotted and died so foully that his own family buried him by hurling water and stones from a distance.

Afterward Abu Rafi migrated to Medina as a Muhajir. The Prophet ﷺ freed him and married him to a freed woman named Salma (ra). He witnessed Uhud, Khandaq, Khaybar, and the other battles, and grew exceedingly close to the Prophet ﷺ, who entrusted him with intimate household tasks — carpentry, repaying debts, pitching the Prophet's tent during Hajj, and even carrying messages between the Prophet ﷺ and Maymuna (ra) during their marriage.

The Prophet ﷺ taught him that, as a freed slave of the household, he was now of the family — and thus charity (zakat) was forbidden to him just as it was to the Prophet's family. Abu Rafi treasured this hadith as a badge of honor. After the Prophet ﷺ passed, Abu Rafi remained attached to the Ahl al-Bayt, fought in the conquests under the rightly-guided caliphs including the liberation of Jerusalem, stayed close to Ali (ra), and narrated roughly forty hadith preserved in the Musnad of Imam Ahmad.

## Key Moments
- His instant conviction upon first seeing the Prophet ﷺ when sent by Quraysh, declaring he would never return to them.
- The Prophet ﷺ refusing to break a covenant or imprison a messenger, sending him back to conceal his faith.
- Secretly practicing Islam in Mecca with Umm al-Fadl and the family of al-Abbas.
- Crying out "By Allah, those are the angels!" at the news of Badr, and being beaten by Abu Lahab — who then died from Umm al-Fadl's blow.
- Being freed by the Prophet ﷺ and married to Salma (ra) after migrating to Medina.
- Witnessing the miracle at Khandaq when a single sheep's foreleg kept multiplying as long as he was asked to bring it.
- Learning excellence in repaying debts when the Prophet ﷺ repaid a young camel with a superior one: "The best of people are those who pay off their debts in the best of ways."
- The Sahaba naming him as the rare example of a person with a truly pure heart.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ honored people of every class, marrying the freed and enslaved to one another and elevating Abu Rafi into his own family.
- A truly "pure heart" (*makhmum al-qalb*) is one free of sin, transgression, malice, and envy — not an excuse for poor character.
- Integrity in dealings: refusing to break covenants, and repaying debts with excellence rather than calculating exactness.
- Faith can be held firmly even when concealed and vulnerable, as Abu Rafi did in hostile Mecca.
- Quiet, sincere service and a heart without rancor can earn one the highest testimony from the believers.
- Lending and borrowing within a community can build social cohesion and harmony.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "I do not betray a covenant, nor do I imprison messengers."
- The Prophet ﷺ on the household: "The freed slave of a people is of them — and it is not lawful for us, the family of the Prophet, to take charity."
- The Prophet ﷺ on debts: "The best of people are those who pay off their debts in the best of ways."
- The Prophet ﷺ on the best of people: those of "a pure heart and a truthful tongue" — explaining the pure heart is "God-conscious and purified, with no sin, no transgression, no malice, and no envy."
- At Khandaq, the Prophet ﷺ: "By Him in whose hand is my soul, had you not asked, the forelegs would have kept coming as often as I asked."
