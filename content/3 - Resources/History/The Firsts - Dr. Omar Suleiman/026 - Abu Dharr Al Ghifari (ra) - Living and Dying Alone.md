# Episode 26 — Abu Dharr al-Ghifari (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 26 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bQEOA-tB5g) · ⏱ 48:20

**Who they were:** Jundub ibn Junada, known as Abu Dharr al-Ghifari (ra), one of the earliest converts to Islam — a fiercely truthful ascetic and loner whose curiosity and questions to the Prophet ﷺ preserved much of the religion for later generations.

## Summary
Abu Dharr al-Ghifari (ra) was born Jundub ibn Junada of the Ghifar tribe, a clan notorious as highway bandits living on the outskirts of cities south of Madinah. A black Arab by descent, Abu Dharr stood apart from his people: he refused to take part in their robbery, kept to himself as a serious and detached young man, and had already inclined toward monotheism, rejecting the idols of his time. He fit the profile of many early Muslims — counted among the marginalized and the weak (du'afa) yet drawn naturally to Tawhid.

While shepherding in the desert, Abu Dharr heard of a man claiming prophethood in Mecca. He sent his brother Anis to investigate, but Anis returned only with a report of a man of noble character who enjoined good and forbade evil — not enough for the seeking Abu Dharr. He travelled to Mecca himself, drinking from Zamzam and sitting in the Haram until Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra) noticed him as a stranger and quietly hosted him. After several days, Abu Dharr confided his purpose, and Ali led him discreetly to the Prophet ﷺ. Abu Dharr greeted him with "As-salamu alayka ya Rasul Allah" — becoming the first person to greet the Prophet ﷺ with the greeting of salam — and accepted Islam on the spot.

Though the Prophet ﷺ instructed him to conceal his faith and return to his people, Abu Dharr's fearless nature drove him to proclaim his Islam openly at the Kaaba. He was beaten nearly to death twice, and twice al-Abbas (ra) saved him by reminding the Quraysh that Ghifar controlled their trade routes. Returning home, Abu Dharr brought his brother, his mother, and eventually large numbers of the Ghifar and Banu Aslam tribes into Islam — so many that when he later brought them to Madinah they resembled an arriving army, delighting the Prophet ﷺ.

In Madinah, Abu Dharr once insulted a man (reported to be Bilal (ra)) by referring to his mother's race. The Prophet ﷺ rebuked him, saying he was a man still carrying traits of Jahiliyyah. Abu Dharr made sincere tawbah, became known for freeing slaves and treating those under his care as equals, and narrated the famous teaching that no one has virtue over another except through taqwa. Despite this lapse, the Prophet ﷺ praised him as the most truthful person the sky has shaded and the earth has carried.

Because of his truthfulness and humility, Abu Dharr constantly asked the Prophet ﷺ how to keep pace with the righteous, and through these questions much hadith reached us. The Prophet ﷺ advised him against seeking leadership, telling him he was "weak" for the burden of authority. At Tabuk, when Abu Dharr's exhausted camel fell behind, he carried his belongings on foot to catch up; seeing a man approaching in a cloud of dust, the Prophet ﷺ said, "Let it be Abu Dharr," then prayed, "May Allah have mercy on Abu Dharr — he lives alone, dies alone, and will be resurrected alone."

In the era of Uthman (ra), troubled by the growing wealth of the Ummah, Abu Dharr requested permission to live in seclusion at al-Rabadha, east of Madinah on the road to Iraq. There, as death approached, he reassured his grieving wife that the Prophet ﷺ had foretold that a group of believers would find and bury him. After he died, his wife laid his shrouded body by the road — and Allah sent none other than Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (ra), his lifelong companion from their shared early days in Islam, travelling with his students. Ibn Mas'ud wept, kissed his forehead, and recalled the Prophet's words, fulfilling the prophecy that Abu Dharr would live, die, and be raised alone.

## Key Moments
- Sent his brother Anis to investigate the new Prophet ﷺ, then journeyed to Mecca himself when the report seemed insufficient.
- Was guided to Ali ibn Abi Talib (ra) at the Kaaba, who secretly led him to the Prophet ﷺ.
- Became the first person to greet the Prophet ﷺ with "As-salamu alayka."
- Proclaimed his Islam publicly at the Kaaba, was beaten twice, and was saved both times by al-Abbas (ra).
- Brought the entire tribes of Ghifar and Banu Aslam to Islam, presenting them to the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah.
- Was rebuked by the Prophet ﷺ for insulting a man's mother, made sincere tawbah, and became known for freeing slaves and treating servants as brothers.
- Fell behind at Tabuk and ran on foot to rejoin the army, prompting the Prophet's prophecy that he would live, die, and be resurrected alone.
- Died in seclusion at al-Rabadha and was found and buried by Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (ra), fulfilling the prophecy.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Good qualities held before Islam are only polished and perfected within it: "The best of you in the days of ignorance are the best of you in Islam."
- Never withhold Islam or da'wah from anyone — even a tribe of bandits embraced it; you never know who will come forward.
- Falling into error (even being admonished by the Prophet ﷺ) does not bar one from the highest stations; sincere repentance and truthfulness can lift a person to be praised as the most truthful of people.
- True virtue lies only in taqwa — there is no superiority of one race or skin color over another.
- Truthfulness includes being honest with oneself: Abu Dharr's constant feeling of deficiency kept him aspiring for more and never complacent.
- Different personalities — even an introverted loner — can each reach elite spiritual status in the sight of Allah.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- "Innama bu'ithtu li-utammima makarim al-akhlaq" — "I was only sent to perfect good character." (the Prophet ﷺ)
- The Prophet ﷺ on Abu Dharr: "The sky has not shaded, nor has the earth carried, anyone more truthful than Abu Dharr."
- "Ya Abu Dharr, innaka da'eef..." — the Prophet ﷺ advising him against leadership, calling it a trust that becomes regret on the Day of Judgment if mishandled.
- "Alayka bi-husni al-khuluq wa tul al-samt" — "Hold to good character and long periods of silence," named as two deeds light to bear but heavy on the scale.
- "Anta ma' man ahbabt" — "You will be with the one whom you love."
- "May Allah have mercy on Abu Dharr — he lives alone, dies alone, and will be resurrected alone." (the Prophet ﷺ, later repeated by Ibn Mas'ud (ra) at his burial)
