# Episode 107 — Juwayriya bint al-Harith (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 79 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Uk8xOtLBPwc) · ⏱ 47:34

**Who they were:** A wife of the Prophet ﷺ and a Mother of the Believers; daughter of al-Harith, the chief of the Banu Mustaliq. Her marriage to the Prophet ﷺ became the means by which her entire captured people were freed and embraced Islam — earning her the description of the greatest blessing any woman ever brought to her people.

## Summary
Dr. Omar frames Juwayriya's story within a pattern of the Prophet's ﷺ marriages used to reconcile hostile tribes. The Banu Mustaliq were a powerful tribe allied to Quraysh, living between Mecca and Medina near the watering place of al-Muraysi', and they kept the famous idol Manat. Instigated by Quraysh, they planned a surprise attack on Medina, expecting the Muslims would not anticipate aggression from them.

When the Prophet ﷺ received word of the plot, he did not act on rumor. He sent the companion Buraydah al-Aslami (whose people had embraced Islam during the Hijra) as a spy, posing as a mercenary seeking spoils. Buraydah confirmed the planned attack, after which the Prophet ﷺ marched out with the army to preemptively engage them. The expedition was momentous: Zayd ibn Haritha was left in charge of Medina, Abu Bakr carried the banner of the Muhajirun, and the hypocrites joined eagerly, seeing easy spoils.

The Prophet ﷺ laid siege and had the archers rain arrows for about an hour until the tribe surrendered, with very few casualties (seven to ten of their men, and one Muslim killed by accident). On this expedition Manat was destroyed, Abdullah ibn Ubayy's plot to incite the Muhajirun against the Ansar and stage a coup was exposed and foiled by revelation, and the slander against Aisha (ra) (along with the loss of her necklace) occurred.

Among the captives was Juwayriya — then named Barra — daughter of the chief al-Harith. Her husband had been killed and her father had escaped. She fell to the share of Thabit ibn Qays and invoked a ransom contract to free herself. She came to the Prophet ﷺ seeking help with her ransom; he offered something better — marriage — which she accepted. When people learned the Prophet ﷺ had married into the Banu Mustaliq, they freed all their captives, unwilling to hold the Prophet's in-laws as slaves. Over a hundred captives and their families were released, and many embraced Islam.

Her father al-Harith then came to ransom her, hiding two prized camels along the way in the valley of al-Aqiq. When the Prophet ﷺ told him exactly where he had hidden them — something only Allah could have known — al-Harith immediately accepted Islam, along with his two sons. This solidified the Islam of the entire tribe, turning former enemies into allies of Medina. The Prophet ﷺ changed her name from Barra ("free of all sin") to Juwayriya, disliking the implication of exaggerated self-praise.

After marriage, Juwayriya became a devoted worshipper, distinguished by frequent fasting and especially by her dhikr (remembrance of Allah). She kept nothing in her home, giving away food as soon as it arrived. She lived through the great fitnas of the ummah without involving herself in any conflict, and died 56 years after the Hijra.

## Key Moments
- The Prophet ﷺ sent Buraydah al-Aslami undercover to verify the Banu Mustaliq's plot before acting — demonstrating justice over rumor.
- The expedition to al-Muraysi' (5 AH): siege by archers, the destruction of the idol Manat, and few casualties.
- Abdullah ibn Ubayy's attempted coup and his words ("the noble will expel the humiliated") were exposed by Qur'anic revelation.
- The slander of Aisha (ra) and the loss of her necklace took place on the return from this expedition.
- Juwayriya, daughter of the chief, was taken captive; she invoked a ransom contract and came to the Prophet ﷺ, who offered marriage instead.
- Upon the marriage, the Muslims freed all the Banu Mustaliq captives — Aisha called Juwayriya the greatest blessing to her people.
- Her father al-Harith embraced Islam when the Prophet ﷺ revealed the hiding place of his two camels, known only to Allah.
- The Prophet ﷺ taught her the famous tasbih, weighing more than hours of dhikr.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Justice in warfare: the Prophet ﷺ verified the threat before acting and did not punish people based on a rumor.
- Every marriage of the Prophet ﷺ carried divine wisdom — here, the reconciliation and conversion of an entire hostile tribe.
- The transformative effect of the Prophet's ﷺ company: a woman of royalty and wealth became known for asceticism, fasting, and constant dhikr.
- Quality of dhikr outweighs quantity: a few sincere, weighty words of remembrance can surpass hours.
- The one who teaches a good deed shares its reward — Juwayriya reaps the reward of every Muslim who recites the dhikr she transmitted.
- When scorned for her past as a captive, she found comfort with the Prophet ﷺ, who reframed her status with dignity (her ransom/dowry being the highest among the wives).

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Aisha (ra): "I do not know of any woman who was a greater blessing to her people than Juwayriya."
- Al-Harith upon his conversion: "No one knew where I hid those two camels except Allah. I bear witness that there is no god but Allah and that you are the Messenger of Allah."
- The Prophet ﷺ taught her after finding her still in dhikr hours later: "Subhan Allahi wa bi hamdihi, 'adada khalqih, wa rida nafsih, wa zinata 'arshih, wa midada kalimatih" — said three times, outweighing all she had recited.
- The hadith on fasting Friday: the Prophet ﷺ asked if she had fasted the day before or intended to fast the day after; when she said no, he told her to break her fast — establishing that Friday is not to be fasted alone.
