# Episode 134 — Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 52 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oM3fhwcvks0) · ⏱ 1:18:49

**Who they were:** Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (ra) was the paternal uncle of the Prophet ﷺ — only about three years his senior — and one of only two uncles to embrace Islam. He is most known for being "Ammu Rasulillah" (the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ), the man so honored that the Prophet ﷺ declared "the uncle is like the father," and the link between Mecca and Medina across the Prophet's life.

## Summary
Al-Abbas (ra) appears throughout almost the entire Seerah yet rarely receives his own biography, often overshadowed by his son Abdullah ibn Abbas. Imam adh-Dhahabi titles him simply "Al-Abbas, the uncle of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ." Within decades of the Prophet's ﷺ passing, he was arguably the most honored man in Medina — companions and Tabi'in alike would rise, kiss his hand, and seek his dua because he was the last living father-figure of the Prophet's ﷺ legacy. The Prophet ﷺ himself said "the paternal uncle is like the father" while describing him. Of four uncles alive at the time of revelation, Al-Abbas and Hamza were the youngest, both hybrids of uncle and elder brother. Their names both mean "lion" — Abbas the lion as it circles and roars before attacking, Hamza the lion after it strikes — fitting their personalities: Abbas calculated and analytical, Hamza fearless and diving in.

Al-Abbas was noble, awe-inspiring (mahib), intelligent, eloquent, fair-skinned, very tall, and possessed a famously powerful voice that could carry for miles. He was wealthy, a spice merchant and traveler, custodian of Zamzam after his father Abdul Muttalib, and the first person pilgrims and merchants would ask about news of Mecca. His wife Lubabah (Umm al-Fadl) was among the very first women to accept Islam after Khadijah, and his servant Abu Rafi and son Abdullah were also Muslims. Al-Abbas, however, kept his own Islam private for a long time — never hostile to the Prophet ﷺ, always defending and protecting him, but not publicly professing the religion. The exact moment of his conversion is the largest disputed gap of any companion.

When Abu Talib died, Al-Abbas assumed the protective role, accompanying the Prophet ﷺ to meet visiting tribes and seeking a host city while his brother Abu Lahab stoned and slandered the Prophet ﷺ. He was central to the pledge of the Ansar at Aqaba: skeptical of their youth, he spoke first, warning the seventy of the gravity and danger of taking the Prophet ﷺ in, and witnessed the pledge though he himself was not yet an outward Muslim. He thus "handed off" the Prophet ﷺ to Medina.

At Badr, the Prophet ﷺ ordered that Al-Abbas not be killed since the Meccans forced him out. The small Abu al-Yusr captured the huge Al-Abbas — aided, the Prophet ﷺ said, by a noble angel. Al-Abbas was found standing motionless and weeping. The Prophet ﷺ refused to treat his uncle differently, requiring him to ransom himself, Aqil, and Nawfal; he revealed knowledge of money Al-Abbas had secretly hidden with Umm al-Fadl, proving his prophethood. Al-Abbas paid the largest ransom of Badr, and Allah later multiplied his wealth as the Qur'an promised. He never again fought against the Prophet ﷺ.

After Badr, Al-Abbas drew steadily closer: facilitating the Prophet's ﷺ marriage to Maymunah, and serving as the Prophet's ﷺ negotiator and middleman during the Conquest of Mecca — reconciling Abu Sufyan, the children of Abu Lahab, and the relatives who had cut him off. The Prophet ﷺ defended him fiercely against any Ansar who treated him as a former enemy: "Whoever hurts Al-Abbas has hurt me." At Hunayn, Al-Abbas held the reins of the Prophet's ﷺ mount and used his thunderous voice to call the fleeing army back. The Prophet ﷺ honored him publicly, abolishing riba beginning with the debts owed to Al-Abbas, and made dua for his forgiveness and offspring.

Al-Abbas lived in Medina for over two decades, beloved and honored. Umar gave him the largest share of war spoils; Umar and Uthman dismounted to honor him; Umar let Al-Abbas stand on his back to restore a water-pipe. During the Year of Famine (Aam ar-Ramadah), Umar prayed for rain through the presence of Al-Abbas as the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, and rain poured. He died at 86 in Uthman's caliphate, shortly after Lubabah, washed by Ali and his sons. His funeral, possibly the largest of any companion in Medina, drew crowds so vast that Uthman had to send guards so Banu Hashim could bury him. His descendants — including Abdullah ibn Abbas, "the scholar of the ummah" — went on to found the Abbasid caliphate.

## Key Moments
- Defended and explained the Prophet ﷺ to inquirers like Ibn Mas'ud and Afif al-Kindi, while himself remaining outwardly non-Muslim and protecting the Prophet ﷺ after Abu Talib's death.
- Spoke first at the Pledge of Aqaba, testing the resolve of the seventy Ansar and witnessing their pledge — bridging Mecca to Medina though not yet a public Muslim.
- Captured at Badr by the small Abu al-Yusr (with angelic aid); found standing like an idol with tears in his eyes; the Prophet ﷺ ordered he not be killed.
- The Prophet ﷺ revealed the hidden money Al-Abbas left with Umm al-Fadl, proving prophethood; Al-Abbas paid the largest ransom of Badr.
- Facilitated the Prophet's ﷺ marriage to Maymunah and served as chief negotiator during the Conquest of Mecca, reconciling former enemies and relatives.
- Held the reins of the Prophet's ﷺ mount at Hunayn and rallied the fleeing army back with his powerful voice.
- The Prophet ﷺ abolished riba beginning with Al-Abbas's debts and made dua for his forgiveness and descendants.
- Umar sought rain through Al-Abbas during the Year of Famine; the rain came in abundance.
- His funeral was so vast that Uthman sent security so Banu Hashim could complete the burial — likely the largest janazah of a companion.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Respect for elders and lineage: the Prophet ﷺ embodied "the uncle is like the father," honoring Al-Abbas with deep love and never letting him be treated as a former enemy.
- Justice over nepotism: the Prophet ﷺ refused to treat his own uncle differently from other prisoners at Badr, just as he said he would apply the law even to Fatima.
- Reform begins at home: the Prophet ﷺ abolished riba starting with his own family's debts.
- Allah replaces what is given for His sake — Al-Abbas's ransom loss was multiplied into far greater wealth, as the Qur'an promised the captives.
- Sincere private faith is known to Allah ("Allah knows your way"), even when outward circumstances are complicated.
- A righteous child is ongoing charity (sadaqa jariya) — Abdullah ibn Abbas's scholarship and dua became part of his father's enduring legacy.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Al-Ammu mithlu al-walid" — the paternal uncle is like the father.
- Al-Abbas, when asked who was older: "He is older (greater), but I happened to be born before him."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Abu al-Yusr: "A noble angel supported you in capturing Al-Abbas."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "What is it with people who hurt me by hurting my uncle? Whoever hurts Al-Abbas has hurt me." And: "No one's faith will be complete until they love you for the sake of Allah and for my sake."
- Qur'an (al-Anfal 8:70, paraphrased): "O Prophet, say to the captives in your hands: if Allah knows good in your hearts, He will give you better than what was taken from you, and He will forgive you; and Allah is Oft-Forgiving, Most Merciful."
- Umar at the rain prayer: "O Allah, this is the uncle of Your Prophet ﷺ; we turn to You through him, so give us rain."
- The Prophet ﷺ's dua: "O Allah, forgive Al-Abbas and his children an outward and inward forgiveness, leaving no sin; and bless his offspring after him."
