# Episode 71 — Mihja, Umayr ibn Abi Waqqas, and Ubayda ibn al-Harith (ra): The Martyrs of Badr

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 71 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UxRPswlks2s) · ⏱ 10:59

**Who they were:** Three early Muhajirun who were among the first to give their lives at the Battle of Badr — Mihja' (ra), the very first martyr of Badr; Ubayda ibn al-Harith (ra), an elder cousin of the Prophet ﷺ and one of Islam's first flag-bearers; and Umayr ibn Abi Waqqas (ra), a young teenager who begged to fight and earned martyrdom.

## Summary
In this closing episode of the season's short biographies, Dr. Omar Suleiman turns toward Madinah and the events of Badr, honoring "the first of the first" — the earliest Muhajirun to die as martyrs on that blessed day. Badr was Yawm al-Furqan, the day of distinction, when the best of companions and the best of angels were present, making the status of its martyrs immense.

The first is Mihja' ibn Salih (ra), about whom history preserves almost nothing. He was a severely persecuted slave in Mecca, freed not by Abu Bakr (ra) but by Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), to whom he remained loyal and devoted for the rest of his life. He was known as a great worshipper who prayed at night alongside Umar. The Prophet ﷺ named him among the best of all black men and women — together with Luqman al-Hakim and Bilal (ra). At Badr he repeated, "I am Mihja', and to my Lord I am returning," before being struck by an arrow as the very first martyr of the day.

The second is Ubayda ibn al-Harith ibn Abd al-Muttalib (ra), a cousin of the Prophet ﷺ about ten years his senior, who embraced Islam before Dar al-Arqam. He endured the boycott, made the Hijrah, and became the second flag-bearer in Islam after Hamza (ra), leading sixty Muhajirun. In the opening duels of Badr he fought alongside Hamza and Ali (ra) against Utbah, al-Walid, and Shaybah. While Hamza and Ali quickly slew their opponents, Ubayda and Shaybah wounded one another; Hamza and Ali finished Shaybah, but Ubayda was gravely wounded and died on the return from Badr at a place called As-Safra, where he lies buried alone. Years later, those passing the spot smelled musk, and the Prophet ﷺ explained it was because Ubayda was buried there.

The third is Umayr ibn Abi Waqqas (ra), the younger brother of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (ra), who embraced Islam very young. The Prophet ﷺ initially sent him away from Badr for being too young, but Umayr wept and begged to fight, saying he did not want to miss out on Paradise. Moved by his emotion, the Prophet ﷺ allowed him, and the young teenager was martyred at Badr.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The greatest honors can belong to those history barely records — Mihja' (ra) is the first martyr of Badr, yet only a line or two survives about him.
- True worth lies in sincerity and sacrifice, not lineage or status; both a freed slave and a noble cousin of the Prophet ﷺ stood among Badr's martyrs.
- Loyalty and gratitude: Mihja' devoted his life to Umar (ra) who freed him, pairing service with deep worship.
- Longing for the Hereafter over worldly achievement — Umayr (ra) cared only not to miss Paradise, regardless of his youth or future prospects.
- Martyrdom at Badr carried a unique, blessed station, as shown by the musk that lingered over Ubayda's grave.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Mihja' (ra) at Badr: "Ana Mihja' wa ila rabbi arji'" — "I am Mihja', and to my Lord I am returning."
- The Prophet ﷺ (narrated in al-Hakim): the best of all black men and women are three — Luqman al-Hakim, Bilal, and Mihja' (ra).
- On the fragrance at As-Safra, the Prophet ﷺ said: "How could it not be such, when Ubayda (ra) is buried there?"
- Umayr (ra): "I don't want to miss out on al-Jannah."
