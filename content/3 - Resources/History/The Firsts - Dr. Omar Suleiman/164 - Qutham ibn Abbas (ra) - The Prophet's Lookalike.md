# Episode 164 — Qutham ibn Abbas (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 22 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xCh21-heG20) · ⏱ 19:15

**Who they were:** Qutham ibn Abbas (ra) was the youngest son of Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, a cousin of the Prophet ﷺ, and among those said to resemble the Prophet ﷺ most closely in appearance. He was the last person to rise from the Prophet's ﷺ grave, a courageous warrior in the early conquests, and was martyred and buried in Samarqand.

## Summary
Qutham (ra) was the baby of the family of Abbas (ra), born into a household of "superstars." Despite this, he carved out his own distinction: of all the family members, the scholars debated that it may have been Qutham who resembled the Prophet ﷺ most closely in physical appearance. Because he was an infant in the Prophet's ﷺ lifetime, this resemblance became most apparent after the Prophet ﷺ passed away and Qutham grew into adulthood.

Dr. Omar opens with a refresher on those who resembled the Prophet ﷺ: from his family, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (whom the Prophet ﷺ said resembled him in creation and character, sent as ambassador to Abyssinia), Hasan ibn Ali, Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith (the Prophet's ﷺ childhood best friend and cousin), and Qutham himself. From outside the family, Mus'ab ibn Umayr and another companion also bore resemblance — and notably, two of these lookalikes (Ja'far and Mus'ab) served as the Prophet's ﷺ ambassadors.

Qutham's mother, Umm al-Fadl Lubaba, had a dream while pregnant that a part of the Prophet's ﷺ flesh was in her house. The Prophet ﷺ interpreted it: she would give birth to a child and would breastfeed the Prophet's ﷺ grandson Husayn (ra) alongside him. Thus Qutham and Husayn became foster brothers through nursing, and Umm al-Fadl came to Medina to raise her child alongside Husayn — part of the family life the Prophet ﷺ built in his final months.

Qutham had his own cherished moments with the Prophet ﷺ. Along with Abdullah ibn Abbas, the Prophet ﷺ would scoop the children up onto his riding animal as they played. A famous account tells of his father Abbas (ra), still in Mecca during the Battle of the Trench, holding Qutham tightly — because he looked most like the Prophet ﷺ — reciting a poem of love and longing while awaiting news. When word came that the Prophet ﷺ and the companions had survived, Abbas kissed the messenger on his forehead and freed him.

In his later life, Qutham (ra) achieved great distinction. He was the last person to rise from the grave of the Prophet ﷺ after the burial. Ali (ra) appointed him governor of Medina. A man of integrity, he refused a thousand extra shares of war spoils, directing them to the Muslims instead. He fought in the conquests of Iraq, Persia, and beyond, carrying the same banner of Banu Hashim that his eldest brother al-Fadl had carried beside the Prophet ﷺ at Hunayn. He was martyred at the opening of Samarqand and buried there — the very region where Imam al-Bukhari, the great compiler of hadith, would later be born.

Dr. Omar closes by reflecting on the family of Abbas (ra), from whose descendants came the Abbasid Caliphate — recorded as having 39 caliphs in roughly six centuries, an empire spanning nearly a thousand years of Islamic history.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Don't only focus on the "superstar" of a family — look at what greatness is quietly being built around them; each of Abbas's sons found their own path.
- True resemblance to the Prophet ﷺ was both in appearance and in character, as exemplified by Ja'far (ra).
- The Prophet's ﷺ tenderness with children — gentle correction over a child's tears — reflects mercy and patience that should shape how we treat the young.
- Integrity means refusing what is not rightfully ours, even honors earned through bravery, as Qutham did with the extra spoils.
- The labor of the early companions carried Islam to distant lands like Samarqand, paving the way for later giants such as Imam al-Bukhari — history Allah crafted with beautiful purpose.
