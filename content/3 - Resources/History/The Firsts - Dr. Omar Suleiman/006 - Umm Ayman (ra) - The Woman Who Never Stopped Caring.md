# Episode 6 — Umm Ayman (Baraka bint Tha'laba) (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 6 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QrrIdK5AjgI) · ⏱ 39:22

**Who they were:** Baraka bint Tha'laba (ra), known as Umm Ayman, was an Abyssinian woman whom the Prophet ﷺ called "my mother after my mother." She is the only person who was with the Prophet ﷺ from the moment of his birth until his death — even outliving him.

## Summary
Umm Ayman was born around 557 CE in Abyssinia and was purchased as a young slave girl by Abdullah ibn Abdul Muttalib, the father of the Prophet ﷺ. She lived in the household with Abdullah and Amina bint Wahb. When Abdullah set out on a trade journey to Syria (Ash-Sham), Amina — newly pregnant — would send Baraka daily to await his return. It was Baraka who delivered the devastating news that Abdullah had died, and she consoled and accompanied Amina throughout her pregnancy.

Baraka was the only other person in the room when the Prophet ﷺ was born; she was the first to hold him, clean him, and hand him to his mother, and she was one of three women who nursed him (along with his mother Amina and Halima al-Sa'diyya). She witnessed Amina's death when the Prophet ﷺ was six — Amina entrusted her to care for him "as if you are his mother" — and later the death of his grandfather Abdul Muttalib when he was nine. Though technically inherited by the Prophet ﷺ, he freed her, and when he married Khadija (ra) he introduced Baraka with the words, "She is my mother after my mother."

At Khadija's and the Prophet's ﷺ urging, Baraka married Ubaid ibn Zayd of the Khazraj of Yathrib and bore a son, Ayman. After Ubaid died (before Islam), she returned to the Prophet's ﷺ household. She was the second woman to believe in the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet ﷺ then said, "Whoever wishes to marry a woman from Paradise, let him marry Umm Ayman," and Zayd ibn Harithah (ra) — himself freed by the Prophet ﷺ — volunteered, though far younger than her. Despite her advanced age, Allah blessed them with Usama ibn Zayd (ra), beloved of the Prophet ﷺ.

Already over 70, Umm Ayman made the difficult Hijrah to Medina. During the journey, suffering from thirst, she described a bucket descending from the heavens by an unseen rope; she drank and was never thirsty again for the rest of her life, making fasting the easiest worship for her. When she arrived with swollen feet and a dust-covered face, the Prophet ﷺ gave her glad tidings of Paradise a second time.

She witnessed every battle, watching over the Prophet ﷺ like a mother, caring for the wounded, and at Uhud she took up a sword to defend him while others fled. The Prophet ﷺ visited her daily, joked with her, and was comforted by her devotion. Across her long life she buried her first husband Ubaid, her son Ayman (martyred at Hunayn), her husband Zayd (martyred at Mu'tah), the Prophet ﷺ, and her son Usama. She lived through the caliphates of Abu Bakr and Umar, dying about 20 days after Umar's assassination — said by some to have reached 100 — and was buried in al-Baqi', positioned in line with the grave of the Prophet ﷺ.

## Key Moments
- As the only other person present, she was the first to hold the newborn Prophet ﷺ and one of three women who nursed him.
- She delivered the news of Abdullah's death to Amina and later consoled the orphaned Prophet ﷺ at the deaths of his mother and grandfather.
- The Prophet ﷺ introduced her to Khadija (ra) as "my mother after my mother," and she famously said, "I never left him and he never left me."
- She was the second woman to accept Islam.
- The Prophet ﷺ declared, "Whoever wishes to marry a woman from Paradise, let him marry Umm Ayman" — and Zayd ibn Harithah (ra) married her, fathering Usama ibn Zayd (ra).
- During the Hijrah, water descended to her from the heavens by an unseen rope, and she was never thirsty again.
- At Uhud she took up a sword to defend the Prophet ﷺ when others fled; she tended the wounded after battles.
- When asked how she was, she replied, "As long as Islam is good, I am good."
- After the Prophet's ﷺ death, when Abu Bakr and Umar visited, she wept — not over his passing to a better place, but because revelation had ceased to descend from the heavens.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ elevated Umm Ayman despite her lacking tribe, wealth, lineage, and despite her skin color and status — embodying his teaching that no race or color holds virtue over another, only taqwa.
- True commitment to the cause of faith means staying steadfast and uncomplaining; her words "I am good as long as Islam is good" model dedication beyond personal hardship.
- A lifetime of selfless service can define a person's purpose — she never once complained throughout decades of sacrifice for the Prophet ﷺ.
- The Prophet's ﷺ tenderness — joking with her, visiting daily, honoring her as his mother — shows the humanity and gratitude at the heart of his character.
- Grief can be rooted in love and mercy rather than complaint against Allah's decree, as the Prophet ﷺ explained when he wept over the dying child.
- Her deepest sorrow at the Prophet's ﷺ death was the loss of revelation — a reminder of the supreme value of the connection to Allah's guidance.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on Umm Ayman: "هي أمي بعد أمي" — "She is my mother after my mother."
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever wishes to marry a woman from Paradise, let him marry Umm Ayman."
- The Prophet ﷺ to her at Medina: "O my mother, indeed for you is a place in Paradise."
- Umm Ayman: "ما تركته ولم يتركني" — "I never left him and he never left me."
- Umm Ayman, when asked how she was: "مادام الإسلام بخير فأنا بخير" — "As long as Islam is good, I am good."
- The Prophet ﷺ on the dying child: "The believer is in good whatever the situation. Even when his soul is being pulled from his body, he remains in praise of Allah."
- Umm Ayman, on weeping after the Prophet's ﷺ death: "I am crying because the revelation has ceased to come from the heavens."
