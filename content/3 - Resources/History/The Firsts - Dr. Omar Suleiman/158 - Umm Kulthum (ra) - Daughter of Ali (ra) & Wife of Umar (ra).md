# Episode 158 — Umm Kulthum bint Ali (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 28 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0v7WoMGbHhg) · ⏱ 23:15

**Who they were:** Umm Kulthum (ra) was a granddaughter of the Prophet ﷺ, the daughter of Ali (ra) and Fatimah (ra), and the sister of Hasan, Husayn and Zaynab (ra). She is best known as the wife of Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra), through whom Umar sought a lasting connection to the Prophet ﷺ.

## Summary
Umm Kulthum (ra) was born near the very end of the Prophet's ﷺ life — likely too young to remember him — and was younger than her sister Zaynab. Her life would be marked by repeated tragedy, but it is defined above all by her marriage to Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra). When Umar proposed to Ali (ra) for her hand, two objections arose: the large age gap, and Ali's wish to reserve his daughters for the sons of Ja'far. But Umar pressed, swearing that no man on earth would care more for her companionship than he would, and Ali consented.

Umar's joy at this marriage was profound. He went to the Sahaba asking them to congratulate him, explaining that he had heard the Prophet ﷺ say that every cause (sabab), every lineage (nasab), and every in-law connection (sihr) will be severed on the Day of Judgment — except his own. Umar reasoned that he already possessed the sabab through his companionship and the sihr through marrying his own daughter Hafsa to the Prophet ﷺ; by marrying Umm Kulthum he secured the final connection so as not to be cut off from the Prophet ﷺ on that Day. Umar gave her a dowry of 40,000 to honor her noble lineage. She bore him two children, Zayd and Ruqayyah.

Among the famous incidents in which she appears is Umar's night patrol, when he discovered a poor travelling couple where the wife was giving birth alone. Umar returned home and invited Umm Kulthum to join him in earning good deeds; she went to help deliver the baby while Umar cooked for the family. Neither felt above this humble service despite their rank and lineage. Umar later promised the man a pension for the newborn.

Tragedy struck when Umar was assassinated. Umm Kulthum, a young woman with two small children, was among those nursing his stab wounds before he passed away, leaving her a young widow. After Umar's death, Ali returned to his original intention and married her to the sons of Ja'far in succession — first Awn, then (after his death) Muhammad ibn Ja'far, and after his death Abdullah ibn Ja'far. She remarked, out of shyness before her mother-in-law Asma, her fear that a third of Asma's sons would die while married to her.

In the end it was Umm Kulthum herself who died first. One night her son Zayd went out to break up a tribal skirmish and was struck in the head and killed; on hearing the news she wept and died that same night. Their funeral prayer was held together the next morning, led by Abdullah ibn Umar, and from this arrangement scholars derive rulings on praying the janazah over a woman and a man together.

- The incident of the honey and butter: when she takes a small amount from the public stores, Umar pays three dirhams for it, upholding the dignity of the Prophet's ﷺ family.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A connection to the Prophet ﷺ — whether by companionship, lineage, or marriage — was treasured above worldly status; on the Day of Judgment only his connection endures.
- True nobility shows in humility: neither Umar nor Umm Kulthum considered themselves above serving a poor, unknown family in the night.
- The household of the Prophet ﷺ upheld scrupulous integrity, paying for even a small amount taken from public stores.
- Allah tests those He loves most; the repeated trials Umm Kulthum endured were a sign of her noble rank.
- Marriages between the families of Ali and Umar (ra) refute later attempts to sow division between the companions and the Prophet's family — love of the Ahl al-Bayt and love of the Sahaba belong together.
