# Episode 135 — Urwa ibn Mas'ud al-Thaqafi (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 51 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oUiFP5Ecnbg) · ⏱ 46:10

**Who they were:** Urwa ibn Mas'ud (ra) was the most noble man of the Banu Thaqif tribe of Ta'if, a respected diplomat and peacemaker connected by marriage to the leaders of Mecca. He embraced Islam late, served as a negotiator at Hudaybiyah, and was martyred calling his own people to Islam — a man the Prophet ﷺ said resembled Jesus (Isa, peace be upon him).

## Summary
Urwa ibn Mas'ud was the foremost chief of Banu Thaqif in Ta'if, a town about 70 miles from Mecca whose nobles intermarried and shared mutual respect with the nobles of Quraysh. His father Mas'ud was among the most noble men of Ta'if, and his mother Subay'a was a noble and famously intelligent woman of Quraysh. As a child Urwa witnessed her shrewdness during a great pre-Islamic battle, when she pitched a tent to shelter and treat the wounded, earning her family the lasting gratitude of those she saved. Urwa himself built a reputation as a peacemaker, paying blood money (diya) from his own pocket to avert tribal warfare and restore calm in Mecca — a debt some historians say he was still repaying until his death.

Urwa was deeply embedded in both societies through marriage, connected to figures like Safwan and Abu Sufyan. The Prophet ﷺ described him as resembling Isa (as): in an authentic hadith about the Night Journey, the Prophet ﷺ said that the one who most resembled Jesus son of Mary was Urwa ibn Mas'ud. The Qur'an alludes to him too: when the Meccans asked why revelation didn't descend on "one of the two great men of the two towns," they meant a leader like Urwa and al-Walid. Dr. Omar notes that Allah pointedly did not decree their punishment — a subtle glad tiding of Urwa's eventual repentance.

Ta'if had largely ignored Islam after rejecting and stoning the Prophet ﷺ, but Urwa entered the story at the Treaty of Hudaybiyah. After other negotiators failed, Urwa volunteered, leveraging his maternal Meccan ties. He first tried a rough approach, belittling the Prophet's followers as a "mixture of people" who would flee him in battle. Abu Bakr (ra) angrily rebuked him with vulgar words, and Urwa, recalling a past favor Abu Bakr had done him, declined to retaliate. When Urwa reached to stroke the Prophet's beard, his own nephew al-Mughira ibn Shu'ba — in full armor — struck his hand back with the flat of a sword, warning him not to touch the Prophet ﷺ.

Watching the Companions' overwhelming love and reverence for the Prophet ﷺ, Urwa was transformed. He returned to Quraysh and declared that he had visited the courts of Caesar, Kisra (Khosrow), and the Negus, yet never seen a people adore their leader as the Companions adored Muhammad ﷺ — racing to catch his spit, lowering their voices, unable even to gaze at him directly. He urged Quraysh to accept the fair terms, but they refused.

After the conquest of Mecca and the Battle of Hunayn, the Prophet ﷺ besieged Ta'if for about a month but, having a heart for its people, withdrew without storming it, praying for their guidance rather than against them. Urwa, who had been away learning siege weaponry, heard of the Prophet's mercy and amnesty and traveled to Medina to embrace Islam voluntarily. The Prophet ﷺ joyfully received him. Urwa insisted on returning to call his people to Islam; the Prophet ﷺ warned, "They will kill you," but Urwa trusted his people's love. After giving the adhan from his rooftop, his own tribesmen shot him with arrows. Forbidding any revenge in his name, he asked to be buried with the martyrs of Hunayn. The Prophet ﷺ likened him to the believer of Surah Ya-Sin who called his people to God and was killed.

## Key Moments
- His intelligent mother, Subay'a, sheltered and treated the wounded during a great pre-Islamic battle, winning her family lasting honor.
- Urwa paid blood money from his own wealth to end tribal bloodshed and restore peace in Mecca, possibly carrying that debt until death.
- The Prophet ﷺ described Urwa as the man who most resembled Isa (Jesus), peace be upon him, from the Night Journey.
- At Hudaybiyah, Urwa belittled the Muslims as a "mixture of people" who would abandon the Prophet ﷺ — provoking a sharp rebuke from Abu Bakr (ra).
- Urwa declined to retaliate against Abu Bakr's harsh words because of a past favor Abu Bakr had done him — an act of loyalty (wafa').
- His nephew al-Mughira ibn Shu'ba, in full armor, struck Urwa's hand away from the Prophet's beard.
- Urwa testified before Quraysh that no king on earth — Caesar, Kisra, or the Negus — was as beloved to his people as the Prophet ﷺ.
- After Mecca's conquest and Ta'if's siege, Urwa came to Medina, embraced Islam, then returned home and was martyred giving the adhan.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The Prophet ﷺ harbored mercy and hope for those who harmed him most, praying for the guidance of Ta'if rather than its destruction.
- Loyalty and remembering favors done to you (wafa') is a noble trait Islam refined rather than abolished — yet forgetting favors you do for others is the higher virtue.
- Responding to insults with patience and better speech is the default; harshness is a rare exception, not the norm, as shown in Abu Bakr's reaction.
- True leadership commands love and willing obedience, not domination through wealth or pomp — the Prophet ﷺ lived simply yet was adored.
- Allah's precise silence in the Qur'an about Urwa and al-Walid was itself a subtle glad tiding of future repentance.
- Sincere callers to the truth may be rejected and even killed by their own people, yet meet Allah with a forgiving heart.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on the Night Journey: "I saw Jesus the son of Mary, and the one who most resembles him is Urwa ibn Mas'ud... and I saw Abraham, and the one who most resembles him is your companion (himself)."
- Qur'an (paraphrased): "Why was this Qur'an not sent down upon a great man from one of the two towns?" — Allah quoting Quraysh without decreeing the two men's fate.
- Urwa to Quraysh: "I have entered upon the kings — Caesar, Kisra, and the Negus — and I have never seen a king adored by his people the way Muhammad is adored by his companions."
- Urwa, when warned he would be killed: "My people love me so much that if they saw me asleep, they wouldn't dare wake me."
- The Prophet's reply: "[They will treat you as my people treated] me."
- After his martyrdom, the Prophet ﷺ likened Urwa to the believer of Surah Ya-Sin who was killed by his people, of whom Allah said: "If only my people knew how my Lord has forgiven me and made me among the honored."
