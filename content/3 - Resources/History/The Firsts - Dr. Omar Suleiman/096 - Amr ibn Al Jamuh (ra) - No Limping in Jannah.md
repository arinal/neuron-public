# Episode 96 — Amr ibn Al-Jamuh (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 91 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sKVO1ifs_M4) · ⏱ 17:27

**Who they were:** Amr ibn Al-Jamuh (ra) was the chief of the tribe of Banu Salima in Medina — an elderly, generous peacemaker who, after a late and reluctant conversion, attained martyrdom at the Battle of Uhud and was promised by the Prophet ﷺ that he would walk in Jannah without his limp.

## Summary
Amr ibn Al-Jamuh was the chief of Banu Salima and one of the few surviving elders of the Aws and Khazraj after the Bu'ath wars, known for his role as a peacemaker, his wealth, and his generosity. By the time the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Medina, Amr was in his sixties. Dr. Omar notes an interesting contrast between the idol worship of Mecca, which was largely commercial and status-driven, and that of Medina, which seemed more sincerely devotional. Amr embodied this: he had a personal idol named Manat (not the famous Qur'anic idol) which he loved deeply, perfuming it, dressing it, and adorning it with jewelry.

When Islam spread through Medina, Amr had no political quarrel with the Prophet ﷺ but had no interest in abandoning his religion or his beloved idol. His wife Hind and his three sons — including Mu'adh — embraced Islam secretly along with most of the Ansar, and they kept it from him, fearing his devastation, while gently warming him to the faith. His son Mu'adh's close friend Mu'adh ibn Jabal was constantly in the household.

Hind eventually convinced him to at least listen to what the new message contained. His son recited to him a portion of Surah Yusuf, and Amr, despite his attachment to his idol, admitted how beautiful and perfect the words were. Still, he insisted he would not convert until he consulted his idol Manat. The young men then carried out a series of "tricks": they knocked over and desecrated the idol, tied it to a dead animal, and threw it in the dumpster. When Amr found Manat tied to a dead dog, unable to defend itself, he declared that something incapable of defending itself could not be a god. He gathered his people, affirmed his leadership over them, and declared his belief in what Muhammad ﷺ had received.

Though he converted late and reluctantly, Amr tasted the sweetness of iman with a beautiful simplicity: worship Allah, pray, and reach Jannah. He never became a scholar or scribe, but redirected the same devotion he had shown his idol toward Allah. His sons had stopped him from fighting at Badr because of his old age and severe limp, which upset him deeply. At Uhud, despite being even older, he insisted on joining the battle, weeping to the Prophet ﷺ that "my kids are stopping me from going to Jannah." The Prophet ﷺ told the family not to forbid him, hoping Allah would grant him martyrdom.

Amr asked the Prophet ﷺ whether, if he were killed, he would be able to walk on both feet in Jannah — and the Prophet ﷺ said yes. He fought through the day crying "I want Jannah," alongside his son Khallad, until he was martyred. After the battle, the Prophet ﷺ paused over his body and declared that he could see Amr walking in Jannah on both feet, without any limp. He was buried in one shroud with his companions among the martyrs of Uhud.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Sincere devotion misplaced in falsehood can, by Allah's mercy, be redirected into profound devotion to Him.
- One does not need to be a scholar; a simple, pure intention — worship Allah and seek Jannah — can elevate a believer to the highest ranks.
- Confronting the powerlessness of false objects of worship is a path to recognizing the truth of tawhid.
- Late conversion does not preclude greatness; Amr embraced Islam in old age yet attained martyrdom.
- The purity of intention matters more than physical ability — a disabled elder's longing for Jannah was honored above the excuses offered for him.
- The afflictions and disabilities of this world are erased in Jannah, where the believer is restored to full health.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Amr to his idol Manat: in effect, if you were truly a god you would not be found dead, tied to a dog in a dumpster, unable to defend yourself.
- Amr to his sons: "You stopped me from Jannah on the day of Badr. By Allah, if I have the opportunity, I will enter Jannah."
- Amr's du'a on leaving for Uhud (narrated by his wife Hind): "O Allah, don't turn me back, don't turn me back."
- Amr to the Prophet ﷺ: "O Messenger of Allah, if I am killed in this battle, do you see me being able to walk on both of my feet in Jannah?" — and the Prophet ﷺ answered, "Yes."
- The Prophet ﷺ over his martyred body: "I can see you walking in Jannah right now without a limp."
