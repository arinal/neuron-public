# Episode 166 — Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 20 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IEn1_nc8_Ms) · ⏱ 1:26:56

**Who they were:** Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr (ra) was the first Muslim child born to the Muhajirun in Medina, the grandson of Abu Bakr (ra), son of az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra) and Asma bint Abi Bakr (ra). A renowned warrior, worshipper, and orator, he became Caliph over most of the Muslim world before being besieged and martyred defending the Kaaba in his seventies.

## Summary
Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr (ra) was born into one of the most illustrious lineages in Islam. His grandfather was Abu Bakr (ra), his mother Asma (ra) — the "possessor of the two waist belts," his father az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (ra) — the disciple of the Prophet ﷺ and the first to draw a sword for Islam, his maternal aunt Aisha (ra), his paternal great-aunt Khadijah (ra), his grandmother Safiyyah (ra), and his great-uncle the martyr Hamza (ra). His kunya is Abu Bakr (also Abu Khubayb, after his son named for the martyred Companion Khubayb), and he physically resembled his grandfather Abu Bakr. Every branch of his family carried a story of sacrifice for Allah.

His parents began married life in poverty — Asma described grazing az-Zubayr's horse, grinding dates for his camel, and walking long distances carrying food. Both had migrated to Abyssinia and later to Medina. During the Hijra, while heavily pregnant with Abdullah, Asma carried provisions to the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr in the cave, enduring Abu Jahl's violent interrogation and a slap so hard her earring flew off. She survived, and the unborn Abdullah survived with her.

When Asma reached Quba, she gave birth — the first child born to the Muhajirun, dispelling rumors that a spell had been cast to prevent Muslim births. The Prophet ﷺ performed the tahnik, placing his chewed-date saliva in the baby's mouth, named him Abdullah, and Abu Bakr held him up through Medina's streets as the Muslims chanted "Allahu Akbar." His first word was "sayf" (sword); he played with wooden swords as a child, narrated two hadith remembered from age three, and as a boy pledged allegiance to the Prophet ﷺ. In a famous incident, he drank the Prophet's cupping blood, prompting the Prophet ﷺ to say, "Woe to you from the people and woe to the people from you."

He grew into a man of extraordinary physical strength, bravery, worship, and eloquence — a "mini az-Zubayr." He witnessed Yarmuk on his father's horse, and in the North African campaign (near modern Tunisia) personally killed the enemy commander after walking into his camp disguised as a messenger. He also fought toward Constantinople. In worship he was likened to the Prophet ﷺ in prayer, nicknamed "the pigeon of the masjid" for his stillness; he divided his nights between standing, bowing, and prostration, and remained absorbed even when a snake fell on his son during prayer.

After the assassination of Uthman (ra) — whom he helped defend, suffering 26 wounds — and through the ensuing fitna, Abdullah refused to pledge allegiance to Yazid. He established himself in Mecca as Hussein (ra) went to Iraq, and his caliphate eventually spread across Yemen, Egypt, Iraq, and most of the Muslim world, with coins minted in his name. He survived an early siege of Mecca, but ultimately al-Hajjaj besieged the city for months, starving its people and bombarding the Kaaba with catapults. At over seventy, fighting nearly alone after his army fled, he had his final heartrending conversation with his hundred-year-old mother Asma, who urged him to die in dignity upon truth rather than submit to humiliation. He fought to the end, was killed while praying near the Kaaba, and his body was crucified by al-Hajjaj — yet a sweet fragrance emanated from it. Asma demanded his body be brought down, declaring "These bodies are nothing; it is the souls that are with Allah." She died a natural death two weeks later, having seen her son granted martyrdom.

## Key Moments
- His mother Asma carried provisions during the Hijra while pregnant with him, enduring Abu Jahl's violent slap, yet both survived.
- Born in Quba as the first child of the Muhajirun, dispelling rumors of a curse; the Prophet ﷺ performed his tahnik and named him, and Abu Bakr proclaimed takbir through the streets holding him aloft.
- His first word was "sword," and he played with wooden swords as a toddler.
- As a child he drank the Prophet's cupping blood, earning the words, "Woe to you from the people and woe to the people from you."
- As a ten-year-old, he refused to flee from the imposing Umar (ra), telling him the road had room for both — earning Umar's embrace and praise: "This is the son of his father."
- In North Africa he killed the arrogant enemy commander after entering his camp disguised as a messenger, triggering the rout of a vastly larger army.
- He helped defend Uthman (ra), receiving 26 wounds he hoped would be among his greatest deeds.
- His final conversation with his mother Asma before his martyrdom, and her insistence that his crucified body be brought down with dignity.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- Courage and conviction can be cultivated and inherited; Abdullah's fearlessness reflected the sacrifice woven through his entire family.
- True awe of Allah produces caution — like his father az-Zubayr, Abdullah was sparing in narration, fearing to misquote the Prophet ﷺ.
- Devotion in worship and devotion on the battlefield are not opposites; he excelled in both prayer and combat, even praying through mortal danger.
- Dignity upon truth is better than humiliation — "to be struck by a sword in dignity is better than a hundred lashes in humiliation."
- Faith must anchor in eternal realities: "These bodies are nothing; it is the souls that are with Allah." Worldly cruelty and visible destruction do not define ultimate outcomes.
- Trials and apparent defeat can shake faith (as it did for some in his community), but they are moments to re-anchor in trust in Allah's decree, not to despair.
