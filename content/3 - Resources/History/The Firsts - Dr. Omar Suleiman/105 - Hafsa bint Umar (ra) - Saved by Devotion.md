# Episode 105 — Hafsa bint Umar (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 81 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aHfBzeccvQg) · ⏱ 1:08:11

**Who they were:** Hafsa bint Umar (ra) was the eldest daughter of Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) and a wife of the Prophet ﷺ, renowned for her devotion to fasting, night prayer, and the Qur'an — so much so that Jibreel (as) himself praised her. The original compiled Mus'haf of the Qur'an was kept in her home.

## Summary
Hafsa (ra) was born about five years before prophethood into one of the earliest Muslim families. Her father was Umar ibn al-Khattab (ra) — who carried the kunya Abu Hafs after her — and her mother was Zaynab bint Maz'un (ra), sister of Uthman ibn Maz'un (ra), the first Muhajir buried in Medina. Her full brothers were Abdullah ibn Umar and Abdur-Rahman. Dr. Omar notes she was a "copy of her father" in her spiritual qualities, and that her very existence disproves the fabricated story that Umar buried daughters alive in the days of ignorance.

Her first husband was Khunays ibn Hudhafah (ra), brother of Abdullah ibn Hudhafah as-Sahmi, and one of the first twenty to embrace Islam. He migrated to Abyssinia, then to Medina, and was the only man of his tribe (Banu Sahm) to fight at Badr. He died after Badr and was buried beside Uthman ibn Maz'un (ra), leaving Hafsa a childless young widow.

Umar (ra) then sought a match for her, offering her first to Uthman ibn Affan (ra), who had just lost his wife Ruqayyah, and then to Abu Bakr (ra) — both responded with silence or deferral, deeply offending Umar. When he complained to the Prophet ﷺ, he learned the reason: the Prophet ﷺ himself intended to marry Hafsa, while Uthman would marry his daughter Umm Kulthum. Overjoyed, Umar exclaimed "Allahu Akbar." The marriage took place in Sha'ban, three years after Hijrah, solidifying the Prophet's family ties with all four future Khulafa ar-Rashidun.

In Medina, Hafsa and Aisha (ra) became close friends, sharing several incidents — the playful prank with the hawdaj, the affair of Surat at-Tahrim over the honey from Zaynab bint Jahsh, and the time they both broke a voluntary fast and confessed it. Hafsa, described as "the daughter of her father," shared Umar's terror of ever displeasing the Prophet ﷺ. Through her, both Umar and Abdullah ibn Umar gained access to the Prophet ﷺ; she relayed his interpretation of Abdullah's dreams and the gentle encouragement, "What a good young man Abdullah is — if only he would pray a little at night."

Hafsa's distinguishing trait was worship. Nearly all of her roughly sixty narrations concern the Prophet's ﷺ devotions: his refusal of a more comfortable bed lest it slow his night prayer, his sunnah of Fajr at home, his slow measured recitation, and his standing prayer until the last year of his life. She memorized the entire Qur'an, learning to read and write from Ash-Shifa bint Abdullah al-Adawiyyah (ra). The most famous incident involving her is the episode of the Prophet's withdrawal from his wives, narrated through Ibn Abbas and Umar, during which Jibreel (as) instructed the Prophet ﷺ to take Hafsa back "for she fasts much and prays much, and she is your wife in Paradise."

After the Prophet's ﷺ death, Hafsa resigned herself to a quiet life of worship. She kept the original Mus'haf, which Uthman (ra) later borrowed to make the standard copies. She cared for orphans, avoided the political fitan — declining to join Aisha (ra) at the Battle of al-Jamal on the advice of Abdullah ibn Umar — and fasted virtually every day until she died 45 years after Hijrah. Abu Hurayrah (ra) helped carry her body, and her brother Abdullah placed her in the grave.

## Key Moments
- Her family lineage: daughter of Umar ibn al-Khattab and Zaynab bint Maz'un, linking her to the earliest Muslim families.
- Her marriage to Khunays ibn Hudhafah (ra), an early convert and Badr veteran who died young, leaving her a childless widow.
- Umar's failed proposals to Uthman and Abu Bakr, and the Prophet's ﷺ revelation that he would marry Hafsa himself.
- The marriage to the Prophet ﷺ in Sha'ban, 3 AH, completing the family ties with all four Rightly-Guided Caliphs.
- The episode of breaking a voluntary fast with Aisha — establishing the ruling that breaking a voluntary fast carries no sin.
- Jibreel's (as) intervention during the Prophet's withdrawal: "Take back Hafsa, for she fasts much and prays much, and she is your wife in Paradise."
- Her keeping the compiled Mus'haf in her home, later used by Uthman (ra) to produce the standard copies.
- Her tearful exchange with Umar (ra) over his patched clothes, both weeping at the memory of the Prophet's ﷺ poverty.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True intercession and honor come through devotion, not lineage — Hafsa was saved by her own fasting and prayer, not merely by being Umar's daughter.
- The Prophet ﷺ chose the hereafter over worldly power and comfort, refusing even a slightly softer bed lest it weaken his worship.
- Quiet, consistent acts of worship — fasting and night prayer — can earn a person renown "in the heavens" even without public prominence.
- A believer fears displeasing Allah and His Messenger; Hafsa, like her father, treated any such displeasure as devastating.
- Caring for orphans was a hallmark of Umar's family, continued by Hafsa and Abdullah ibn Umar, who refused to eat without an orphan present.
- Wisdom can mean staying out of fitna — Hafsa declined to join the Battle of al-Jamal and was grateful she "did not dip her hands in any of it."

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Jibreel (as) to the Prophet ﷺ: "Take back Hafsa, for she is a woman who fasts and a woman who prays, and she is your wife in Paradise."
- The Prophet ﷺ on Abdullah ibn Umar: "What a good young man Abdullah is — if only he would pray a little at night."
- The Prophet ﷺ to Umar regarding worldly power: "Are you in doubt, O son of Khattab? They are a people whose good things have been hastened for them in this worldly life [while our reward is in the hereafter]."
- Umar's supplication: "O Allah, I ask You for martyrdom in Your path and death in the city of Your Prophet ﷺ" — granted when he was stabbed leading prayer in the Prophet's mosque.
- Nafi' (ra): "Hafsa did not die until [it was as though] she never broke her fast" — describing her near-constant fasting.
