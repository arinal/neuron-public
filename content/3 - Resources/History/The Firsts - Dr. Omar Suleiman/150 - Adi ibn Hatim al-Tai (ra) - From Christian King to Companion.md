# Episode 150 — Adi ibn Hatim al-Tai (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 36 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gRfpRe_L0q4) · ⏱ 48:12

**Who they were:** A Christian chieftain of the tribe of Tayy and son of the legendary Hatim al-Tai (the Arab byword for generosity), Adi ibn Hatim (ra) was a longtime enemy of Islam who, after a transformative encounter with the Prophet ﷺ, embraced the faith and became one of the most loyal and righteous companions.

## Summary
Adi was the son of Hatim al-Tai, perhaps the most famous man in Arab history for his generosity — so generous that Arabic poetry uses him as the measure of charity. Hatim kept a fire lit outside his home to guide travelers to free provisions, sacrificed all his animals (including his prized horse and his armor) to feed the needy during famine, and even instructed that pots of food and water be kept full at his grave for passing travelers. The Prophet ﷺ later said Hatim was a good man who died "between Jesus and Muhammad," leaving his fate to Allah while praising his charitable nature.

Adi inherited his father's wealth, prestige, and the leadership of Tayy, but not his generosity. He notably took a quarter of his people's war spoils for himself — the highest such share recorded among the Arabs. When Islam arose, he immediately declared himself an enemy of the Prophet ﷺ, viewing the new faith as a political threat to his tribal arrangement rather than engaging it religiously. For nearly two decades he opposed and reportedly helped finance hostility against the Muslims, though he never met the Prophet ﷺ.

As Islam spread after the conquest of Mecca, Adi prepared to flee to Sham (greater Syria) to be among fellow Christians should the Prophet's army approach. When the scouts of Khalid ibn al-Walid neared, Adi fled with his wife and children — but forgot his sister, Safana. She was captured and brought to Medina, where she three times appealed to the Prophet ﷺ. Learning she was Hatim's daughter, the Prophet ﷺ praised her father, freed her, and out of his noble character insisted she wait for a trustworthy caravan, then provided her with a camel, fine clothing, and provisions for her journey.

Reunited with Adi in Sham, Safana scolded him fiercely for abandoning her, then urged him to go to Muhammad ﷺ: "If he is a prophet, you will have followed a prophet; and if he is a king, you will not lose anything by being near him." Adi, miserable in Sham and unwelcome among the Byzantine Christians, resolved to test the man for himself.

In Medina, the Prophet ﷺ received Adi with honor — standing, taking his hand, and walking him to his home. Along the way he stopped to patiently help a poor old woman, showing Adi this was no king's behavior. His "palace" was a tiny mud chamber with a single cushion, which the Prophet ﷺ insisted Adi sit upon. The Prophet ﷺ then addressed Adi's Christianity directly — telling him to remove his golden cross, citing the Qur'an about taking rabbis and priests as lords, exposing how his sect made the lawful unlawful, and reminding him that taking a quarter of the spoils was forbidden in his own faith. He then prophesied three things: a woman would one day travel safely from Hira to the Kaaba fearing none but Allah; the treasures of Khosrow (Kisra) of Persia would fall into Muslim hands; and wealth would become so abundant that men would struggle to find anyone to accept charity. Overwhelmed by the Prophet's certainty, Adi embraced Islam.

Adi lived to about 120 years and witnessed two of the three prophecies fulfilled — the safe traveling woman and the conquest of Persia's treasures (he was in the army that took them), the third coming during the just rule of Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz. He returned to lead his people in Islam, became renowned for his generosity like his father and his deep love of prayer, and remained loyal during the Ridda wars, refusing to join the false prophet Tulayha. He served Abu Bakr, was praised by Umar, defended his honor before Uthman, and fought alongside Ali in every battle, losing an eye in his service. In his final years he was always the first into the mosque and the last to leave, passing away 66 years after the Hijra.

## Key Moments
- Hatim al-Tai's legendary acts of generosity — feeding travelers, sacrificing his prized horse and armor, and leaving instructions to feed visitors even at his grave.
- Adi's roughly 20-year enmity toward the Prophet ﷺ, whom he never met, viewing Islam purely as a political threat.
- His flight to Sham as Khalid ibn al-Walid's battalion approached, leaving his sister Safana behind.
- The Prophet ﷺ honoring Safana, freeing her, and generously providing for her journey home.
- Safana urging Adi: "If he is a prophet, you will have followed a prophet; if he is a king, you will lose nothing."
- The Prophet ﷺ greeting Adi personally, pausing to help a poor old woman, and revealing his bare, single-cushion home.
- The Prophet ﷺ confronting Adi with his own Christian beliefs and his unlawful quarter of the spoils, then making three prophecies.
- Adi witnessing two of the three prophecies fulfilled in his lifetime; his lifelong loyalty to Ali and his old-age devotion to the mosque.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- A parent's good character and generosity can become a legacy of mercy and respect for their children — as Hatim's charity benefited Adi.
- The Prophet ﷺ taught honoring noble people of their communities ("If a noble man comes to you, honor him") while never neglecting the weak and downtrodden.
- True leadership is shown by humility — the most powerful man on earth lived in a bare mud room with a single cushion.
- Two decades of prejudice and "islamophobia" were dismantled by a single sincere, dignified human encounter.
- Conversion can be a "win-win" of sincerity: approaching truth honestly leads only to good.
- Genuine faith is proven through trial — Adi's steadfastness during the Ridda wars validated the sincerity of his Islam.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ on Hatim: "Your father indeed was a good man... had he died upon Islam we would have asked Allah's mercy for him."
- Safana to Adi: "Go quickly to this man. If he turns out to be a prophet, then you would have followed a prophet; and if he is just a king like other kings, you will not find from him except good."
- Qur'an (cited by the Prophet ﷺ): "They took their rabbis and their priests as lords besides Allah."

- Umar (ra) to Adi: "You believed when they disbelieved, and you stayed loyal when others betrayed, and you moved forward when others moved back."
- Adi (ra) on his love of prayer: "The prayer has not come a single time since the day I became Muslim except that I was already ready for it."
