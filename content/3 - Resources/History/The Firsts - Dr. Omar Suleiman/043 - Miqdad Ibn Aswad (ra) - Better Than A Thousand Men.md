# Episode 43 — Al-Miqdad ibn al-Aswad (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 43 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V9iYK7fFZS0) · ⏱ 51:34

**Who they were:** One of the earliest converts to Islam, among the seven who declared their faith openly in Mecca, and the first horseman to fight in the path of Allah at Badr. A beloved companion whom Allah commanded the Prophet ﷺ to love, renowned for his unwavering courage and his profound counsel to later generations.

## Summary
Al-Miqdad was born in Hadhramaut, Yemen, a people beloved to the Prophet ﷺ for their good nature (fitrah). His given name was al-Miqdad ibn Amr ibn Tha'laba, but after accidentally killing a fellow tribesman in a fight, he fled to Mecca seeking protection. There, al-Aswad ibn Abd Yaghuth al-Kindi of Banu Zuhra took him in and eventually adopted him, so he became known as al-Miqdad ibn al-Aswad. When the verse abolishing adoptive lineage (tabanni) was later revealed, he reverted to being called Ibn Amr — and the books record him under both names.

Al-Miqdad was physically striking: very tall, muscular, dark-skinned, with a large belly, abundant hair, big round eyes, a prominent nose, and a beard he dyed blonde. He heard of the Prophet ﷺ early — even before Dar al-Arqam — and embraced Islam immediately, belonging to the lower social class alongside Bilal, Suhaib, and Ammar (ra). Because he had no protecting tribe and his adoptive father disowned and publicly tortured him upon learning of his conversion, he was among the five companions whom Quraysh dragged in iron coats under the hot sun. He migrated twice — first to Abyssinia, then to Medina, where the Prophet ﷺ paired him in brotherhood with Jabbar ibn Sakhr (ra) of the Ansar.

The Prophet ﷺ arranged al-Miqdad's marriage himself, wedding him to his cousin Duba'a bint al-Zubayr ibn Abd al-Muttalib — a woman of status, beauty, and faith — after a misunderstanding with Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf (ra). From Duba'a comes the famous hadith on stipulating a condition in ihram for Hajj or Umrah: "Allahumma mahilli haythu habastani" ("O Allah, I will end my ihram wherever You stop me").

At Badr, al-Miqdad was the sole horseman on the Muslim side and was placed in command of the left flank. When the Prophet ﷺ informed the Muslims they would have to fight a full army, al-Miqdad's booming declaration of loyalty lit up the Prophet's ﷺ face with joy. He fought in all the major battles, served in the conquests of Egypt and al-Sham, recited Qur'an to boost morale before and during battles, and earned from Umar (ra) the testimony that he was "better than a thousand men." He later supported Ali (ra) during the fitna, then served in Muawiyah's army in the conquest of Cyprus.

His most defining moment, however, was his powerful response to a man of the Tabi'een who envied him for having seen the Prophet ﷺ — a reminder to be grateful for the generation and circumstances Allah places one in. Al-Miqdad died in Damascus, leaving three children — Abdullah, Ma'bad, and Karima — who narrated hadith from both their parents.

## Key Moments
- Fled Yemen after accidentally killing a tribesman; was sheltered and then adopted by al-Aswad ibn Abd Yaghuth in Mecca, giving him the name Ibn al-Aswad.
- Among the seven who first declared Islam publicly and among the five tortured in iron coats under the desert sun.
- Made hijrah twice — to Abyssinia and then to Medina.
- The Prophet ﷺ personally arranged his marriage to his own cousin, Duba'a bint al-Zubayr.
- At Badr he was the only Muslim horseman and declared: "We will not say what the people of Musa said... but we will fight on your right, your left, before you and behind you," bringing visible joy to the Prophet's ﷺ face.
- The famous "incident of Miqdad": tempted by hunger, he drank the milk saved for the Prophet ﷺ, despaired of Shaytan's whispers, yet was met with the Prophet's ﷺ dua and a miracle of overflowing milk.
- Asked never to be made a leader again, fearing for his sincerity (ikhlas) when he saw people placing leaders on pedestals.
- Threw pebbles at a flatterer praising Uthman (ra), citing the Prophet's ﷺ command to "throw dirt at the flatterers."
- Umar (ra) called him "better than a thousand men" when sending reinforcements to Amr ibn al-As in Egypt.
- His final bequest included 36,000 dirhams for al-Hasan and al-Husayn (ra) and 7,000 dirhams for each of the Mothers of the Believers; he died in Damascus.

## Lessons & Takeaways
- The most overlooked names among the Sahaba often carry the deepest contributions; status with Allah is not measured by fame among people.
- Shaytan exploits hardship to drive a person toward despair and flight, but Allah's mercy can transform a moment of weakness into one of reward.
- True sincerity guards against the corruption that praise, flattery, and leadership can bring to one's intentions.
- The early Muslims suffered not only physical torture but the anguish of being divided from and persecuted by their own families for the sake of faith.
- Be grateful for the time and circumstances Allah has placed you in; do not romanticize other eras, for each generation carries its own trials and opportunities to stand for the truth.
- Allah burdens no soul beyond its capacity — every believer has a unique share of struggle and a unique chance to support the truth.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- Ibn Mas'ud (ra): The first seven to declare Islam openly were the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr, Ammar, his mother Sumayya, Suhaib, Bilal, and Miqdad.
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Indeed Allah has commanded me to love four, and informed me that He loves them" — Ali, al-Miqdad, Abu Dharr, and Salman (ra).
- Al-Miqdad at Badr: "O Messenger of Allah, we will not say as the people of Musa said, 'You and your Lord go and fight'; rather we will fight on your right and your left, before you and behind you."
- The Prophet's ﷺ dua over the milk: "Allahumma at'im man at'amani wasqi man saqani" — "O Allah, feed the one who feeds me and give drink to the one who gives me drink."
- Duba'a's hadith on ihram: "Allahumma mahilli haythu habastani" — "O Allah, I will end my ihram wherever You detain me."
- Al-Miqdad to the Tabi'i who envied him: "What makes you wish to be present at a time that Allah made you absent from, without knowing what you would have been had you been present?" — citing the verse on those granted "the coolness of our eyes" through faithful spouses and children, and being made "leaders for the righteous."
