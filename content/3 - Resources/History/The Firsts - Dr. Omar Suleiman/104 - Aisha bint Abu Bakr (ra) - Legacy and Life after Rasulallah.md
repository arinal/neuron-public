# Episode 104 — Aisha bint Abu Bakr (ra)

> [!info] The Firsts · Episode 82 · Dr. Omar Suleiman
> 🎥 [Watch on YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g4Oc3PbWR0s) · ⏱ 1:15:22

**Who they were:** Aisha bint Abu Bakr (ra) was the beloved wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, daughter of Abu Bakr As-Siddiq (ra), and one of the greatest scholars and jurists among the companions. This episode covers her life after the death of the Prophet ﷺ — the five decades in which she became a teacher, mufti, and living conduit of his legacy.

## Summary
When the Prophet ﷺ passed away in her home, Aisha (ra) was around 20 years old. She would live another fifty years, making the vast majority of her life one of extending the legacy of the Prophet ﷺ. Though she narrated over 2,000 hadith, she did so sitting next to his grave, having received the glad tidings — through Jibreel and through the Prophet's own vision of her in Jannah — that their separation was only temporary.

Her home became the burial place of "the three moons" she had once seen in a dream: the Prophet ﷺ, her father Abu Bakr (ra), and Umar (ra). Abu Bakr died at 63 on a Monday, deliberately mirroring the Prophet's death. When Umar (ra) lay dying after being stabbed, he asked permission — without pressure — to be buried beside the Prophet and Abu Bakr in the spot Aisha had reserved for herself. In one of her greatest acts of generosity, she preferred Umar over herself, knowing it would have pleased the Prophet ﷺ. Out of modesty toward Umar, she wore her hijab when entering that room even after his death.

After the Prophet's death, Aisha became a primary source for matters of creed, tafsir, fiqh, du'a, and the personal life of the Prophet ﷺ. She clarified subtle points — that the "easy reckoning" differs from being scrutinized, that the severity of Judgment Day surpasses concerns of modesty, and that those who "rush to good" are the devout who fear non-acceptance. She taught the du'a of Laylatul Qadr and the du'a for visiting the graveyard, and she instructed women fearlessly in matters of purity.

Beyond her teaching, her lived worship was extraordinary. Urwah said no one's prayer resembled the Prophet's ﷺ like Aisha's. She recited the Qur'an through the night, weeping over single verses, and fasted as though perpetually. Yet her most distinguishing trait was charity: she gave away gifts of 100,000 dirhams in a single day, never elevating her own poverty even fifty years after the Prophet's death, weeping at full meals because he ﷺ never ate his fill twice in one day.

She raised and educated orphans, teaching them to read, write, and master grammar and poetry so they would not be exploited. As a mufti, the senior companions — Abu Hurairah, Ibn Umar, and others — turned to her with their hardest questions, including complex inheritance. She corrected misconceptions in narrations, was the most eloquent of speakers, and was uniquely learned in medicine, having attentively listened to the Arab physicians who came to treat the Prophet ﷺ in his final illness.

In the fitna following Uthman's (ra) assassination, Aisha rode out seeking swift justice for his killers, leading to the Battle of the Camel — a battle ignited by the people of fitna attacking both sides at night. She never intended combat; afterward Ali (ra) honored and protected her, and she deeply regretted the episode. She died on the 17th night of Ramadan, 58 AH — comforted by Ibn Abbas (ra) that nothing remained between her and reunion with the Prophet ﷺ except her soul leaving her body — and was buried in al-Baqi'.

## Key Moments
- She gave up her own burial place beside the Prophet ﷺ and her father so Umar (ra) could be buried there, preferring what pleased the Prophet over her own wishes.
- Abu Bakr (ra) confirmed her dream of three moons: the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr, and Umar would all be buried in her home.
- Urwah found her repeating one verse from Surat al-Tur, weeping and supplicating, for so long that he went to the market and shopped and returned to find her still in the same place.
- She gave away a gift of 100,000 dirhams from Muawiyah before iftar, keeping nothing for herself though she was fasting and had no dirham left.
- The story of Barirah, the lone cup of barley given to a beggar at iftar, and the goat that arrived after Maghrib — illustrating trust in Allah's provision.
- She freed over 40 (in one narration over 100) slaves to expiate an oath she made never to speak to her nephew Abdullah ibn Zubair.
- During Hajj, Said ibn Hisham asked about the Prophet's character — she answered, "His character was the Qur'an," citing Surat al-Mu'minun, and described his qiyam as Surat al-Muzzammil.
- Ibn Abbas (ra) consoled her at death; she passed away on the 17th of Ramadan, the same day as Badr, with a janazah so large that Medina "rumbled."

## Lessons & Takeaways
- True love prefers the beloved's pleasure over one's own comfort — Aisha gave up her grave because it would please the Prophet ﷺ and thereby please Allah.
- Wealth was never allowed to soften her devotion; she sustained the Prophet's life of simplicity for fifty years and gave charity relentlessly.
- The fiqh of sincere charity: she asked which du'a recipients made so she could repeat it, ensuring the full reward of her sadaqa remained for the hereafter (citing "we seek no reward nor thanks").
- Modesty does not hinder true learning — like the women of the Ansar, she pursued knowledge of religion without embarrassment.
- The taqwa of guarding one's oaths — she wept at having broken an oath even after fully expiating it.
- Educating and empowering the vulnerable, especially orphans, is among the highest forms of charity.
- Even amid fitna and conflict, the companions reconciled, honored, and defended one another — Ali (ra) protected Aisha's honor and she referred people to him for knowledge.

## Notable Quotes & Narrations
- The Prophet ﷺ: "Whoever faces accountability (is scrutinized) on the Day of Judgment will certainly be punished" — distinguishing this from the "easy reckoning" (the mere presentation of deeds).
- Du'a of Laylatul Qadr taught to her: "Allahumma innaka afuwwun tuhibbul afwa fa'fu anni."
- Aisha (ra) on the Prophet ﷺ: "By Allah, he never had his fill of bread or meat twice in a day."
- To a beggar given a single grape: "How many atoms' worth are in it?" — reciting, "Whoever does an atom's weight of good will see it."
- "When you trust Allah more than you trust what is in your possession, Allah will provide for you."
- On the Prophet's ﷺ character: "His character was the Qur'an."
- Al-Zuhri: "If the knowledge of Aisha were gathered against the knowledge of all women, the knowledge of Aisha would be greater."
- Ibn Abbas (ra) at her deathbed: "There is nothing between you and being reunited with the Prophet ﷺ and the beloved ones except that your soul leaves your body... your innocence will forever be recited in every masjid, day and night."
